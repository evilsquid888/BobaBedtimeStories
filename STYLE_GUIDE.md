# Style Guide

## Voice and Tone

- **Narrator:** Warm, playful, slightly cheeky. Talks to the reader occasionally ("And you know what happened next? Of course you do."). Makes food puns without shame.
- **Register:** Middle-grade (ages 9-12). Smart but accessible. No talking down.
- **Humor:** Silly physical comedy, wordplay, food puns, exaggerated reactions. A laugh line in every major scene.
- **Emotion:** Genuine warmth without sentimentality. Cozy, not saccharine.

## Story Structure

Each story follows this template:

```
# Story NN: [Title]

**Main Character:** [Name] ([Dessert ingredient])
**Guest Characters:** [list, if any]
**Setting:** [Location in Taiwan]
**Theme:** [one-line theme]
**Mandarin Spotlight:** [key term] ([pinyin]) = [translation]

---

[Opening Hook — first 150 words, fun and vivid]

[Main narrative — adventure, problem, resolution ~1500 words]

[Warm ending — character gets "eaten" or joyfully sacrifices for deliciousness]

---

## Goodnight Blessing
[Cozy closing line — soft, sleepy, warm]

## AI Image Prompts
1. [Prompt 1 — key scene]
2. [Prompt 2 — character portrait]
3. [Prompt 3 — closing scene]
```

## Word Count

- Target: 1800-2000 words per story
- Opening hook: ~150 words (fun must start immediately)
- Main narrative: ~1500 words
- Ending + blessing: ~150-350 words

## Mandarin Integration

### Rules
- On first use in a story: **bold Chinese** (pinyin) = English
- After first use: can use just the Chinese or just the English, reader's choice
- Use naturally — in dialogue, narration, signs, food names
- 3-5 Mandarin terms per story minimum
- The Mandarin Spotlight term in the header is the key vocabulary word

## Character Voice Guidelines

Each character has a distinct speaking style:

- **Boba:** Short, punchy. Action verbs. "Let's GO!"
- **Taro:** Proverbs, patience. "Remember, the taro that steams slowly..."
- **Aiyu:** Honest, short. "I... I think that's not true."
- **Mochi:** Exclamation marks!!! Huggy language. "GROUP HUG!!!"
- **Caǎo:** Deadpan. Cool. "Yeah. I saved everyone. No big deal."
- **Feng:** Formal, gift metaphors. "Allow me to present..."
- **Bīng:** ALL CAPS DRAMA. "This is LITERALLY the WORST day!"
- **Mango:** Sports metaphors. Fast. "Gotta stay in the zone!"
- **Hóng Dòu:** Grandmotherly. "Have you eaten? Let me make you something."
- **Tāng Yuán:** Inclusive. "We're all in this together, family!"
- **Dòuhuā:** Poetic, zen. "The stream does not rush. It arrives."
- **Lìzhī:** Dramatic socialite. "Darling, DARLING, you simply must..."
- **Bǎi:** Dares and pranks. "Bet you won't try THIS."
- **Dìguā:** Street-smart, humble. "Listen kid, I've been around the cart."
- **Yēguǒ:** Surfer dude. "Duuude, just ride the wave, bro."
- **Dàn:** Carnival barker. "STEP RIGHT UP, folks!"
- **Bùdīng:** Anxious reassurance. "I'm fine, totally fine, definitely not wobbling—"
- **Zhīma:** Bold action hero. "We roll NOW."
- **Yángmáo:** Dreamy wishes. "I wish... I wish..."

## "Getting Eaten" Endings

This is crucial. When a character gets eaten at the end of a story, it must be:

- **Joyful:** They're fulfilling their delicious destiny
- **Funny:** Play it for laughs, not tears
- **Proud:** The character is honored to become part of a wonderful dessert
- **Never scary:** No fear, no sadness, no pain
- **Think of it like:** A firework finally getting to light up the sky. A singer hitting the stage. A superhero saving the day. It's their MOMENT.

Not every story needs an "eaten" ending — some end with the character simply going to sleep, or looking at the stars, or hugging friends. But when the eaten ending happens, make it celebratory.

## AI Image Prompts

Each story includes 3 prompts for potential AI illustration:

1. **Key Scene:** The most exciting or visually striking moment
2. **Character Portrait:** The main character in a characteristic pose
3. **Closing Scene:** The warm, cozy ending moment

Prompts should be detailed, specifying:
- Art style (warm, whimsical, storybook illustration)
- Color palette
- Character details
- Setting elements
- Mood/lighting
