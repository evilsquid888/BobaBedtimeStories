# Story 23: Egg Waffle and the Demon Chicken Festival

**Main Character:** Dàn (雞蛋糕 jīdàngāo) — Egg Waffle
**Guest Characters:** None
**Setting:** Shilin Night Market, Taipei
**Theme:** The best shows end with a bang
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 雞排 (jīpái) = fried chicken cutlet

---

"LADIES AND GENTLEMEN! GHOSTS AND GHOULS! SPIRITS, SPECTERS, AND ANYONE ELSE WITH A MOUTH AND AN APPETITE — WELCOME TO THE GREATEST CULINARY CATASTROPHE IN THE HISTORY OF THE SPIRIT WORLD!"

Dàn stood on the rim of a bubbling fryer basket, his tiny showman's top hat tilted at a rakish angle, steam pouring off his golden-brown crust like he was his own personal fog machine. His voice — which had no business being that loud coming from a waffle the size of a golf ball — echoed through the neon-drenched corridors of **士林夜市** (Shìlín yèshì) = Shilin Night Market, the biggest and most famous night market in all of Taipei.

And tonight, Shilin had visitors.

Strange visitors.

The kind of visitors who arrived in a cloud of sulfurous smoke, wore matching tiny chef hats, and immediately tried to eat a street sign.

They were **魔神仔** (móshénzǐ) = mischievous demons — a troupe of twelve spirits from the other side who had, apparently, been watching Taiwanese cooking shows for the past century and had finally decided they were ready to throw their own food festival. They called themselves "The Infernal Kitchen Brigade," and they had banners, business cards, and absolutely zero cooking ability.

(Yes, dear reader, you read that correctly. Demons. At a night market. With business cards. Sometimes the world is a strange and beautiful place.)

The leader was a squat red demon named Big Wok, who wore three tiny chef hats stacked on top of each other and spoke in a booming voice that rattled the corrugated tin roofs. He'd marched straight into the center of Shilin Night Market at 9 PM on a Saturday, planted a flag made from a spatula and a dish towel, and announced:

"WE ARE HERE TO CELEBRATE **雞排** (jīpái) = FRIED CHICKEN CUTLET! THE GREATEST FOOD IN HUMAN OR DEMON HISTORY! WE WILL HOST A COMPETITION! WE WILL FIND THE ULTIMATE CHICKEN! WE WILL — Grak, stop eating the flag."

A skinny green demon named Grak paused mid-chew, the dish towel flag hanging from his mouth like a sad, saucy tongue.

"It smelled like chicken," Grak said defensively.

"It's COTTON."

"Demon taste buds are different!"

Now, if you've never had jīpái, let me paint you a picture. Imagine a piece of chicken the size of your FACE. Not a baby face — YOUR face. Pounded flat, marinated in a secret blend of five-spice and garlic and soy and magic, coated in crispy batter, and deep-fried until it's golden and crunchy and so fragrant that you can smell it from two blocks away. It comes in a paper bag, dusted with chili powder, and eating it while walking through a night market is basically a rite of passage in Taiwan.

The demons LOVED jīpái. They were absolutely, spiritually, cosmically obsessed with it. The problem was that their love was entirely one-sided, because every time they tried to cook it, something went horribly wrong.

Round One of the Demon Chicken Festival began at 9:30 PM, and Dàn — who had elbowed, rolled, and sweet-talked his way to the announcer's position — was ready.

"ROUND ONE!" he bellowed from his perch on the judging table, his crust steaming with excitement. "THE CHALLENGE: A BASIC FRIED CHICKEN CUTLET! DEMONS, START YOUR FRYERS!"

What happened next was, in Dàn's professional opinion, the most entertainingly terrible cooking he had ever witnessed.

A purple demon named Sizzle heated his oil to what he called "a nice medium temperature," which turned out to be the actual temperature of hellfire. The oil didn't just bubble — it erupted. A column of flame shot up three meters and singed the awning of a nearby stall selling **臭豆腐** (chòu dòufu) = stinky tofu. The stinky tofu vendor didn't even flinch. "Eh," she said. "I've smelled worse."

Big Wok attempted a more refined approach. He selected his chicken carefully, marinated it in a blend of soy sauce, five-spice, and what he called "ancient demon seasoning" (it was glitter), then lovingly coated it in flour, dipped it in egg wash, and deep-fried... a shoe.

"That's a SHOE, Big Wok!" Dàn cackled from the judging table.

Big Wok stared at the bubbling sneaker with genuine confusion. "But I JUST had the chicken."

"You put it down on the ground!"

"The ground is where food GOES!"

"NOT BEFORE YOU COOK IT!"

The crowd — which had grown from a few confused bystanders to roughly four hundred people, all holding their phones up and absolutely losing their minds — roared with laughter. Dàn lived for this. He BREATHED this. Every explosion, every disaster, every demon looking befuddled at a fried shoe was another chance to whip the audience into a frenzy.

"ROUND TWO!" he announced, hat nearly flying off from sheer enthusiasm. "THE MARINATION CHALLENGE! Demons, you have TEN MINUTES to prepare the most flavorful chicken the world has ever tasted!"

Grak, the dish-towel-eating demon, confidently pulled out what he believed was a beautiful chicken breast. He rubbed it lovingly with garlic. He drizzled it with soy sauce. He whispered sweet nothings to it.

It was a rock.

"Grak," Dàn said, peering over the edge of the table. "That is a rock."

Grak looked at Dàn. Looked at the rock. Looked back at Dàn. "It's a free-range rock," he said with dignity.

The crowd screamed. A teenager laughed so hard he dropped his bubble tea. Somewhere, a night market cat yawned.

By Round Three, the situation had escalated beautifully. Demons were flinging batter at each other. One demon had somehow set the marinade on fire (the marinade was WET — nobody could explain this). Another demon had gotten his chef hat stuck in the fryer and was running around with a deep-fried hat smoking on his head like a crispy, golden crown.

And through it all, Dàn conducted the chaos like an orchestra.

"AND IN LANE FOUR, WE HAVE SIZZLE ATTEMPTING A DOUBLE-FRY TECHNIQUE — oh, that's not a technique, that's just a fire! SOMEONE GET A BUCKET! NOT THAT BUCKET, THAT'S THE BATTER — okay, now the bucket's on fire too! MAGNIFICENT!"

The crowd was in absolute heaven. Humans and demons side by side, laughing, cheering, filming, eating stinky tofu and grilled corn and pearl milk tea while watching the most spectacular cooking disaster in night market history.

But then came the final round.

The Championship.

Big Wok stepped forward, and for the first time all night, the demons got serious. The laughter in the air shifted to something electric and expectant. Big Wok had one perfect, beautiful, face-sized chicken cutlet — properly sourced this time, not a shoe, not a rock, not a dish towel. It was marinated. It was pounded thin. It was ready.

There was just one problem.

"The coating," Big Wok said, holding up the naked chicken breast. "Every time we fry it, the coating falls off. We can't get the CRUNCH. The crunch is everything. Without the crunch, jīpái is just... sad hot chicken."

He looked at the demons. The demons looked at each other. Twelve sets of glowing eyes filled with genuine anguish. They had traveled from the spirit world for this moment, and they couldn't get the coating to stick.

The crowd went quiet. Even Dàn paused, his hat settling to a stop on his golden head.

And then — slowly, magnificently, with the dramatic timing of a born performer — Dàn looked down at his own body.

Golden-brown. Crispy. Covered in a perfect grid pattern of tiny egg-shaped bumps. A texture that was, if he was being honest, EXACTLY what you'd want coating the most spectacular fried chicken in night market history.

Oh.

OH.

"Ladies and gentlemen," Dàn said softly, and somehow the softness carried further than any shout. The entire crowd leaned in. "I have been a showman my whole life. I have sung, danced, flipped, hollered, and performed on every counter and griddle this island has to offer."

He removed his tiny top hat and held it to his chest.

"But every great show needs a FINALE."

He placed the hat gently on the judging table.

"And the best finales?" He looked at the crowd — the humans with their phone lights glowing like a galaxy of stars, the demons with their tiny chef hats and hopeful, glowing eyes. "The best finales end with a BANG."

Dàn turned to face the fryer basket. The oil shimmered below, golden and hot and waiting. Big Wok held up the championship chicken cutlet, and the other demons began to understand. They pressed their claws to their mouths. The crowd held its breath.

Dàn backed up three steps on the counter. He crouched. Steam exploded from his crust like a rocket preparing for launch.

"THE BEST SHOWS," he roared, "END WITH A — "

He ran.

"BAAAAAAAAANG!"

Dàn launched himself off the counter in the most spectacular stage dive in the history of Taiwanese street food. He soared through the air, golden and magnificent, top-hat-less and free, and landed — SPLAT — directly onto the championship chicken cutlet, wrapping himself around it like the crunchiest, most golden, most PERFECT coating the world had ever seen.

Big Wok, tears streaming down his red face, lowered the Dàn-coated chicken into the fryer.

The sizzle that followed was legendary. It was the sizzle of destiny. The sizzle of a showman who knew his final act had to bring the house down. The oil sang, the steam rose in great golden clouds, and when Big Wok lifted the basket three minutes later, what emerged was not just fried chicken.

It was ART.

The championship jīpái was golden, impossibly crunchy, covered in Dàn's perfect egg-waffle grid pattern that held the seasoning in every tiny pocket. The aroma hit the crowd like a warm, delicious tidal wave. Five hundred people groaned in unison.

The demon judges took the first bite.

They cried.

Not dramatic, performative tears. Real tears. Big, glowing, demon tears that sizzled when they hit the ground. Twelve demons, standing in a row, weeping with joy and clutching each other's chef hats.

"It's perfect," Big Wok whispered, chicken crumbs falling from his trembling lips. "It's the most perfect **雞排** I have ever eaten."

And Dàn — golden, crunchy, spectacular Dàn — felt himself being shared among humans and demons alike, piece by crispy piece. Every bite brought a gasp, a laugh, a happy tear. He was the crunch. He was the coating. He was the grand finale.

"Step right up," he murmured, warm and golden and fading into the most delicious ending a showman could ask for. "Step right up... to the greatest show... on earth..."

His tiny top hat sat on the judging table, right under the neon lights of Shilin Night Market. A little girl picked it up and put it on her finger like a thimble, and laughed, and took another bite of the best fried chicken that had ever existed.

The Demon Chicken Festival was declared, by unanimous vote, the greatest food event in the history of both the human and spirit worlds. The demons vowed to return every year. The stinky tofu lady said, "Eh." The night market cat finally looked mildly impressed.

And high above the market, carried on steam and grease-smoke and the laughter of five hundred people, Dàn's voice echoed one last time — not a shout this time, but a warm, satisfied, perfectly timed whisper:

"And THAT'S how you close a show."
