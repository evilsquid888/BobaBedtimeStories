# Story 14: Sweet Potato and the Underground Adventure

**Main Character:** Dìguā (地瓜 dìguā) — Sweet Potato
**Guest Characters:** Timmy (Uncle Piggle Wiggle universe)
**Setting:** Beitou, Taipei
**Theme:** Humility and hidden treasure
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 地瓜 (dìguā) = sweet potato

---

Dìguā didn't look like much, and he'd be the first to tell you so.

"I'm golden on the inside," he said, tapping his rough, dirt-crusted skin. "But you gotta dig to find it. That's the whole point."

**地瓜** (dìguā) = sweet potato — Taiwan's humblest hero. Not glamorous like mango. Not fancy like lychee. Not even particularly pretty. Just a lumpy, brown, earth-covered root vegetable that tasted like caramel when you roasted it and cost about thirty cents from any street cart in the country.

Dìguā lived where all self-respecting sweet potatoes live: underground. Specifically, under a sweet potato cart in **北投** (Běitóu) = Beitou, the hot springs district in northern Taipei where volcanic sulfur vented from the earth and the whole neighborhood smelled like hard-boiled eggs crossed with a spa.

His cart was parked near the Beitou Hot Springs Museum — a beautiful Japanese-era building surrounded by steaming streams and sulfuric mist. The cart's owner, Uncle Cài, had been roasting sweet potatoes in a converted oil drum for twenty-eight years. The drum glowed orange with charcoal heat, and the sweet potatoes inside slowly transformed from hard, dirty roots into soft, golden, caramelized perfection.

"That's the thing about sweet potatoes," Dìguā often said. "We start ugly. We start UNDERGROUND. We start in the dirt, with worms and rocks and darkness. But give us time and heat, and we become the best thing you ever tasted. It's a metaphor, kid."

He said "kid" a lot. Dìguā was a street-smart root vegetable who talked like he'd seen things.

Everything changed on the morning a boy literally fell into his life.

The boy was about eleven, sandy-haired, American, and he came tumbling down a muddy hillside behind the hot springs and landed in a heap next to Uncle Cài's cart. He was covered in dirt, breathing hard, and clutching a hand-drawn map that looked like it had been through a washing machine.

"I FOUND IT!" the boy yelled, then looked around, realized he was in a parking lot, and deflated. "Or... I didn't find it. Again."

"**你還好嗎**?" (nǐ hái hǎo ma) Uncle Cài asked — "are you okay?"

"I'm fine! Sorry! I'm Timmy! I'm looking for the Underground Kingdom!"

Uncle Cài blinked. Then he went back to turning sweet potatoes.

Dìguā, however, was intrigued. He poked his brown, lumpy head out from the pile of unroasted sweet potatoes in the cart and studied the boy.

"Underground Kingdom, huh?" Dìguā said.

Timmy stared at the talking sweet potato. He processed this for about two seconds, then shrugged. After his adventures with Uncle Piggle Wiggle — the jolly, mustachioed, magical man who had cured him of his video game addiction with a potion that made real life more exciting than any game — Timmy had learned that the world was full of strange and wonderful things, and talking vegetables barely registered on his weirdness meter.

"Yeah!" Timmy said, spreading his muddy map on the ground. "See, Uncle Piggle Wiggle told me that Beitou has a secret underground world. The volcanic heat created caves and tunnels under the whole district, and some of them connect to ancient passages that nobody's explored in centuries. He said there's a treasure down there — something really special."

"Uncle Piggle Wiggle sent you?"

"He gave me the map. And a bottle of something called 'Courage Nectar,' but I drank it all on the plane."

Dìguā studied the map. It was hand-drawn in Uncle Piggle Wiggle's distinctive swirly handwriting, and it showed a network of tunnels starting from somewhere near the hot springs and spiraling deep underground.

"Listen, kid," said Dìguā. "I know underground. I was BORN underground. Grew up there. Lived my whole life in the dark until someone dug me up. If there's a tunnel system under Beitou, I'll find it."

"Really?"

"I'm golden on the inside. Time to prove it."

They found the entrance behind the hot springs museum — a crack in the volcanic rock, just wide enough for a boy to squeeze through if he turned sideways and held a sweet potato like a flashlight. (Dìguā didn't actually produce light, but he claimed he did, and Timmy was too polite to argue.)

The tunnel was warm. Not pleasant warm — volcanic warm. The rocks radiated heat from deep inside the earth, and the air smelled of sulfur and wet stone. Steam hissed from cracks in the walls, creating ghostly wisps that curled around their feet.

"This is AWESOME," Timmy breathed. "It's like a real-life dungeon level."

"It's not a game, kid."

"I know! That's what makes it awesome! In video games, you can always restart. This is real. No extra lives. No save points."

Dìguā grumbled, but secretly, he liked this kid. Timmy had the kind of enthusiasm that comes from discovering the world is bigger than your screen — the wide-eyed wonder of someone who recently learned that adventure doesn't have a pause button.

They descended. The tunnels branched and twisted, and Dìguā navigated by instinct — the deep, earthy instinct of a root vegetable that had spent its whole life in the dark.

"Left here," he said. "I can feel it. The earth is softer on the left. That means water. Water means caves."

"How do you KNOW that?"

"Kid, I'm a sweet potato. I know dirt the way fish know water. This is my element."

The left tunnel opened into a cavern that made them both gasp.

It was enormous — a cathedral of volcanic rock, its ceiling lost in darkness, its walls streaked with mineral deposits in colors that shouldn't exist underground: vivid yellows, electric blues, deep crimson reds. Hot springs bubbled up from the floor in pools of various sizes, each one a different temperature and a different color. Steam rose and mixed and swirled, creating a warm fog that made the whole cavern feel like a dream.

"**太棒了**" (tài bàng le) Timmy whispered — "awesome."

"I've been under the ground my whole life," said Dìguā, his voice unusually soft. "And I never knew THIS was down here."

They explored the cavern. Timmy found crystals growing from the walls — not diamonds or rubies, but mineral formations that sparkled like them. Dìguā found a pool of warm mud that was, he reported, "the most comfortable thing I have ever experienced. It's like a spa for potatoes."

But the treasure they were looking for wasn't crystals or hot springs.

It was in the back of the cavern, half-buried in volcanic soil: a stone tablet, ancient and carved with characters so old they were barely readable.

Timmy brushed off the dirt. Dìguā squinted at the characters.

"I can read some of this," Dìguā said. "It's old, old Mandarin. Classical. It says... it says this place was sacred. The indigenous people who lived here thousands of years ago believed these caves were the earth's kitchen — where the planet cooked its treasures. Hot springs, minerals, fertile soil... they all came from here."

"The earth's kitchen," Timmy repeated. "That's the treasure?"

"Keep reading."

The tablet continued: "The greatest treasure is not gold or jade. The greatest treasure is the food that grows in humble soil, nourished by hidden fire. To find it, dig."

Dìguā stared at the tablet for a long time.

"It's talking about sweet potatoes," he said. "The treasure is SWEET POTATOES."

"...Seriously?"

"Think about it, kid! Taiwan's soil, heated by volcanic energy, enriched by mineral-rich water — it grows the best sweet potatoes in the world. The ancient people knew it. The treasure isn't some golden idol in a cave. It's the food that feeds millions of people, grown in dirt nobody looks twice at, powered by underground fire nobody sees."

Timmy sat down on a rock. He thought about this. He thought about video games, where treasure was always shiny and obvious — gold coins, glowing swords, sparkly armor. And he thought about real life, where the most valuable things were almost always hidden.

"I'm golden on the inside," Dìguā repeated. And this time, the catchphrase didn't sound like a joke. It sounded like the truest thing anyone had ever said.

They made their way back up through the tunnels, following the heat gradients and Dìguā's dirt-sense. When they emerged behind the hot springs museum, blinking in the afternoon sun, Timmy was filthy, sweaty, and happier than he'd ever been in front of a screen.

"**謝謝**" (xièxie) he said to Dìguā — "thank you."

"For what? I didn't do anything special."

"You showed me that the coolest stuff is underground. Hidden. Not showy. Not obvious. You have to dig for it."

Dìguā would have blushed, but he was already brown, so the effect was minimal.

They walked back to Uncle Cài's cart. The charcoal drum was glowing, and the smell of roasting sweet potatoes filled the Beitou afternoon — caramel and earth and warmth, the smell of humble things becoming extraordinary.

Uncle Cài pulled a fresh sweet potato from the drum. He split it open. The inside was pure gold — deep orange-yellow flesh, steaming, soft, so sweet it barely needed anything else.

He handed it to Timmy.

"**吃飽了嗎**?" (chī bǎo le ma) Uncle Cài asked — "have you eaten yet?"

Timmy took a bite. His eyes closed. The flavor was volcanic heat and patient earth and hidden treasure, transformed by fire into something golden.

"**好吃**" (hǎochī) he said — "delicious" — and he meant everything: the sweet potato, the cave, the adventure, and the lesson that the best things in life don't sparkle on a screen.

They grow in the dark.

And they're golden on the inside.
