# Story 03: Aiyu and the Mountain Spring

**Main Character:** Aiyu (愛玉 àiyù) — Aiyu Jelly
**Guest Characters:** None
**Setting:** Alishan (阿里山)
**Theme:** Honesty and transparency
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 愛玉 (àiyù) = aiyu jelly

---

Aiyu was see-through, and she hated it.

Not completely see-through, mind you. She wasn't invisible or anything dramatic like that. She was translucent — a wobbly, pale golden-amber cube of jelly with tiny lemon-seed freckles and the unfortunate ability to be seen through, like a window made of dessert.

"You can see right through me," she sighed, staring at her own reflection in a mountain puddle. "Literally. There's a beetle behind me and you can count its legs through my stomach."

The beetle, for the record, had six legs. It also had no opinion about Aiyu's transparency issues.

Aiyu lived on the slopes of **阿里山** (Ālǐshān) = Alishan, the most famous mountain in all of Taiwan. Alishan was the kind of place that made poets write poems and photographers forget to press the shutter button because they were too busy crying about the beauty. Ancient cypress trees taller than apartment buildings. Clouds that rolled through the valleys like slow, fluffy rivers. Sunrises that painted the sky in colors that crayons hadn't been invented for yet.

And the **愛玉** (àiyù) = aiyu jelly? It grew wild here. The aiyu vine — a type of fig — clung to the trees and dangled seeds that, when rubbed in water, produced a magical, translucent jelly. It was one of Taiwan's most special treats: served cold with lemon and honey, it was the perfect refreshment for a hot mountain hike.

Aiyu knew all of this. She was proud of her heritage. But she still wished she wasn't see-through.

"Nobody takes you seriously when they can see your internal organs," she complained to the cypress tree she lived beside.

The cypress tree, being three thousand years old, said nothing. It had learned long ago that the best response to complaining was patient silence.

Everything changed on the morning the hiking group arrived.

They came up the Alishan Forest Railway — the ancient narrow-gauge train that chugged up the mountain like an asthmatic caterpillar — and spilled out at the trailhead in a riot of hiking boots, selfie sticks, and oversized water bottles. There were about thirty of them, led by a guide named Mr. Chén who had a megaphone and no sense of volume control.

"**來來來**!" (lái lái lái) he bellowed — "come come come!" — his voice bouncing off the mountains like a ping-pong ball. "Today we hike to the Sacred Spring! The water is so pure you can see to the bottom! So clear! So honest! Just like the famous Alishan aiyu jelly!"

Aiyu perked up. "He's talking about me! Well, about my species. That's basically me."

The hikers followed Mr. Chén up the trail, chatting and laughing and taking approximately seven thousand photographs per minute. Aiyu, curious, jiggled along behind them, hopping from rock to rock. She moved the way all jelly moves — with a lot of wobble and very little dignity, but surprisingly good speed.

The trail wound through a cathedral of cypress trees, their trunks wrapped in moss like green velvet coats. Sunlight filtered through the canopy in golden beams, and the air smelled like mint and cedar and the distant promise of rain.

"This is the most beautiful place in Taiwan," announced a woman in the hiking group.

"No, Jiufen is," argued her husband.

"No, Taroko Gorge."

"No, the Shilin **夜市** (yèshì) = night market at 2 AM when you're eating stinky tofu and questioning your life choices."

They all had a point.

About an hour into the hike, the trail crossed a narrow wooden bridge over a stream, and that's where the trouble started. One of the hikers — a boy about twelve years old named Wèi — leaned over the railing to take a photo of the water and dropped his phone.

"NO!" Wèi screamed, watching his phone bounce off a rock and disappear into a deep, dark pool beneath the bridge.

Now, this pool was famous. The locals called it the Liar's Pool, because the water was so dark with tannins from fallen leaves that you couldn't see the bottom. Everything it swallowed, it kept. Coins, cameras, one unfortunate hiking boot, and now Wèi's phone.

"My mom is going to KILL me," Wèi moaned. "That phone has all my homework on it."

"I thought you said you lost your homework," said his sister.

"I DID lose it. I lost it just now. In a pool. It counts."

Mr. Chén tried to be helpful. He poked the pool with a stick. The stick disappeared. He threw a rock in. The rock disappeared. He considered jumping in himself, but his megaphone was not waterproof and he had priorities.

"**不好意思**" (bù hǎo yìsi) he said awkwardly — "sorry" — "but I think the phone is gone."

Wèi looked like he might cry. Not the dramatic kind of crying — the quiet, embarrassed kind where you clench your jaw and stare at the ground and pretend you have allergies.

Aiyu, watching from behind a fern, felt her translucent heart squeeze.

She could help. She KNEW she could help. But it meant doing the one thing she hated most: being seen through.

"Oh, for goodness' sake," she muttered to herself. "Aiyu. Your name literally means 'love jade.' Act like it."

She jiggled out from behind the fern, bounced down the rocks, and plopped into the Liar's Pool.

The water was cold — mountain-spring cold, the kind of cold that makes your teeth hurt and your brain go "WHAT ARE YOU DOING." But Aiyu was jelly. Cold was her natural habitat. She sank slowly through the dark water, glowing faintly like a tiny amber lantern.

And here was the thing about Aiyu — the thing she had always thought was her biggest weakness:

She was transparent.

The dark water that hid everything from everyone? Aiyu could see through it. Not because the water was clear — it wasn't. But because SHE was clear. Her translucent body let the faintest light pass through, and that light bounced off the objects on the bottom of the pool and back through her like a living lens.

She could see EVERYTHING.

There were the coins — hundreds of them, tossed by hikers making wishes. There was the hiking boot (size 42, blue, only mildly moldy). There were fallen leaves and smooth stones and the skeleton of an ancient fish that had lived a long and presumably fulfilling life.

And there, wedged between two rocks, was Wèi's phone.

Aiyu wrapped her wobbly arms around it — which was like trying to hug a brick with wet noodles — and slowly, painstakingly, jiggled her way back to the surface.

She broke through the water with a gasp, holding the phone above her head like a trophy.

Wèi stared.

Mr. Chén stared.

The entire hiking group stared.

"Is that... a cube of jelly... holding a phone?" whispered someone.

"I believe it is," whispered someone else.

"**太棒了**!" (tài bàng le) Wèi shouted — "awesome!" — and scooped Aiyu and the phone out of the water in one motion. The phone was wet but alive, its screen still glowing. Wèi's homework was saved. His mother would not commit filicide. The universe was balanced.

"How did you find it?" Wèi asked, holding Aiyu up to eye level. "The water is so dark! Nobody can see in there!"

Aiyu jiggled nervously. Through her translucent body, Wèi could see his own fingers on the other side, tinted amber.

"I can see through things," she said softly. "That's... that's kind of my whole deal. I'm see-through. Transparent. What you see is what you get."

She expected him to laugh. Or to look disappointed. Or to say "that's weird" the way the opaque desserts always did.

Instead, Wèi's face broke into the biggest grin she had ever seen.

"That's the coolest thing EVER," he said.

And Aiyu, for the first time in her wobbly little life, felt proud of being transparent.

The hike continued. They reached the Sacred Spring — a pool of water so clear that you could see every pebble, every fish, every ripple of light on the sandy bottom. Mr. Chén explained that the spring was sacred because it was honest. "No secrets," he said. "No hiding. The water shows you everything exactly as it is."

"Just like aiyu jelly," Wèi said, glancing at Aiyu, who was sitting on his backpack and trying not to melt in the sun.

Mr. Chén looked confused. "What?"

"Nothing."

At the top of the trail, there was a refreshment stand. An old man with a kind face and a straw hat was selling cups of fresh aiyu jelly — hand-rubbed from wild seeds that morning, served with local honey and a squeeze of lemon in a clear plastic cup.

Aiyu looked at the cups. She looked at the golden, wobbly jelly inside them, glowing in the afternoon sun like captured fragments of the Sacred Spring. She looked at the hikers, tired and sweaty and happy, reaching for the cups with grateful hands.

"**吃飽了嗎**?" (chī bǎo le ma) the old man asked each one — "have you eaten yet?"

Aiyu slid off the backpack and made her way to the stand. The old man saw her and smiled — the smile of someone who recognizes a relative.

"Ah," he said. "Come to join your family?"

"I think," said Aiyu, "I'm ready to be exactly what I am. Transparent. Honest. And — I hope — refreshing."

She slipped into a cup of fresh lemon water, and the moment the cold liquid touched her skin, she felt herself dissolve — not painfully, not sadly, but like a sigh of relief. Like taking off a costume you'd been wearing too long. Like finally, finally being seen for exactly who you are.

Wèi was the one who drank the cup.

"**歐伊西**!" (ōuyīxī) he announced — "yummy!" — and smacked his lips. "This is the best aiyu I've ever had. It tastes like... honesty."

His sister looked at him strangely. "Honesty doesn't have a taste."

"It does," said Wèi, looking at the empty cup with a small, knowing smile. "It tastes like lemon and honey and mountains. And it's completely, perfectly, beautifully clear."

## Goodnight Blessing

Tonight, little one, may you have the courage of Aiyu — to be transparent, to be honest, and to know that the things that make you different are the things that make you extraordinary. You don't need to hide. You don't need to be opaque. Just be yourself, clear and true, and the world will see how beautiful you are. Close your eyes now and dream of mountain springs and lemon-sweet breezes. Goodnight, honest heart. Goodnight.
