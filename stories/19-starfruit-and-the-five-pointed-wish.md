# Story 19: Starfruit and the Five-Pointed Wish

**Main Character:** Yángmáo (楊桃 yángtáo) — Starfruit
**Guest Characters:** None
**Setting:** Yangmingshan at night
**Theme:** Dreams and perseverance
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 楊桃 (yángtáo) = starfruit

---

Yángmáo had five points, and each one was a wish.

"Every point is a wish," she whispered, holding herself up to the twilight sky and watching the stars appear one by one above Yangmingshan, like someone was poking holes in a dark blanket to let the light through.

**楊桃** (yángtáo) = starfruit — that magical, improbable fruit that, when sliced, becomes a perfect five-pointed star. Pale green-yellow, waxy, translucent at the edges, with a flavor somewhere between tart apple and sweet grape. In Taiwan, starfruit grew in the warm southern and central regions, but Yángmáo had traveled north — all the way to Taipei — because she had something important to do.

She had come to make a wish on a star. But not just any star.

**陽明山** (Yángmíngshān) = Yangmingshan — the national park that sat on the mountains above Taipei, a volcanic landscape of sulfur vents, hot springs, bamboo forests, and the clearest night skies within a hundred kilometers of the city. On a clear night, from the right spot on Yangmingshan, you could see the entire Milky Way stretching across the sky like a river of powdered sugar.

Yángmáo had heard that once every ten years, the stars above Yangmingshan aligned in a pattern that matched the five points of a starfruit. An old farmer had told her. He'd heard it from his grandmother. She'd heard it from a dream. The reliability of this information was questionable at best.

But Yángmáo was a dreamer, and dreamers don't need reliable information. They need a direction and a reason to walk.

Her five wishes were these:

Point One: To see the ocean from above the clouds.
Point Two: To taste rain before it touches the ground.
Point Three: To make someone she'd never met smile.
Point Four: To understand why the moon changes shape.
Point Five: The biggest wish. The one she kept secret, even from herself.

She began the climb at dusk.

The trail up Yangmingshan started in the suburbs of Taipei — neat apartment buildings and 7-Elevens and scooter repair shops — and quickly transformed into something wild. Bamboo forests closed in on both sides, their stalks creaking in the wind like a thousand tiny doors opening and closing. The air cooled with each step upward, and the smell changed from city exhaust to sulfur to pine to something clean and sharp that had no name.

"**加油**" (jiāyóu) Yángmáo told herself — "keep going" — as she bounced and rolled up the trail. Starfruit are not natural hikers. Their five-pointed shape means they roll unpredictably, spinning on one point, tipping to another, never quite stable. But instability, Yángmáo had decided long ago, was just another word for adaptability.

At the first rest stop — a wooden platform overlooking a valley — she met Point One.

The clouds had settled below her. Not above, not around, but BELOW, filling the valley like a slow white ocean. And beyond them, far in the distance, the actual ocean — the Pacific — glinted under the last light of the sun, a thin silver line between the clouds and the sky.

"The ocean from above the clouds," Yángmáo breathed. "Point One."

She marked the moment by holding very still and letting the wind turn her slowly, like a pinwheel. The sight was so beautiful it hurt, the way all truly beautiful things do — not because they're sad, but because they remind you how much world there is and how small your five points are in comparison.

She continued climbing. The trail steepened. The bamboo gave way to cloud forest — gnarled trees draped in moss and ferns, their branches heavy with moisture. Everything dripped. The air was more water than air.

And then: rain.

Not regular rain. This was mountain cloud rain — the kind that forms when a cloud simply walks into a mountain and disintegrates. It didn't fall from above. It materialized around you, out of nowhere, tiny droplets appearing in the air like magic.

Yángmáo opened her mouth.

A raindrop formed an inch from her face, hung in the air for a fraction of a second, and then — before gravity could claim it — landed on her tongue.

"Rain before it touches the ground," she whispered. "Point Two."

It tasted like clouds taste, which is to say: like the memory of water, cool and clean and slightly electric, as if the sky had a flavor and it was this.

The trail wound past sulfur vents — cracks in the mountainside where volcanic steam hissed out like the earth was breathing. The smell was pungent but not unpleasant, a reminder that beneath the peaceful surface, this mountain was alive. Very much alive.

Yángmáo passed a hot spring pool where steam rose in ghostly coils. She passed a meadow of silver grass, its white plumes swaying in the evening breeze like a ghost army doing synchronized yoga. She passed a stone monument marking the highest point on the trail, worn smooth by a million hands touching it for luck.

She touched it too. Five points against cold stone. A tiny wish in a big mountain.

Near the summit, she found Point Three.

A man was sitting on a bench beside the trail. He was middle-aged, wearing a wrinkled suit with the tie loosened, and he was staring at his hands with the empty expression of someone who had driven up the mountain after a very bad day and didn't know what to do next.

Yángmáo rolled up to the bench. The man didn't notice her. She climbed onto the armrest. He still didn't notice.

She cleared her throat. "**不好意思**" (bù hǎo yìsi) — "excuse me."

The man looked sideways. He saw a starfruit sitting on a bench armrest.

He blinked.

"Did you know," said Yángmáo, "that when you slice a starfruit, it becomes a star?"

"I... what?"

"A star. Five points. Look." She turned so he could see her cross-section — the five-pointed star shape, glowing faintly in the last light. "I'm a fruit that turns into a star. How amazing is that? I spend my whole life looking like a weird waxy lump, and then someone slices me and suddenly — STAR. It's like finding out your boring neighbor is secretly a wizard."

The man stared. Then, slowly, against every intention his bad day had given him, the corner of his mouth twitched.

Then the other corner.

Then — fully, genuinely, helplessly — he laughed.

"A wizard," he repeated, shaking his head. "A fruit wizard."

"**對**" (duì) — "exactly," said Yángmáo. "We're all stars, waiting to be discovered. Some of us are just... not sliced yet."

The man laughed again. It was a good laugh — rusty, like a machine that hadn't been used in a while but still worked. He picked up Yángmáo gently and held her up to the sky, where the first real stars were appearing.

"Thank you," he said. "I needed that."

"That's three," Yángmáo whispered to herself. "Point Three."

The man set her back on the bench, stood up, straightened his tie — not because he needed to, but because it felt like starting over — and walked back to his car. He was still smiling.

Point Four came at the summit.

The moon was rising over Taipei — a waxing crescent, thin as a fingernail, glowing like a curved lantern in the dark sky. Yángmáo sat on a rock and stared at it.

"Why do you change shape?" she asked the moon.

The moon, predictably, did not answer. But sitting there, watching the silver crescent slowly climb the sky, Yángmáo understood anyway.

The moon didn't actually change. It was always a sphere — always whole, always complete. It just showed different faces depending on where the light was. Sometimes bright and full. Sometimes dark and hidden. Sometimes a sliver, a crescent, a half.

But always, underneath the shadow, the full moon was there.

"Like me," Yángmáo realized. "I look different from every angle. Slice me this way, I'm a star. Look at me from the side, I'm a ridged lump. But I'm always the same fruit. The light just hits differently."

Point Four: the moon changes shape because light changes angle. But the moon never stops being whole.

That left Point Five.

The secret wish. The one she'd carried up the entire mountain without naming.

Yángmáo sat on the highest rock she could find, surrounded by silver grass and sulfur mist and a sky so full of stars it looked like someone had spilled a bag of sugar across black velvet. The city of Taipei glowed below like a galaxy that had fallen to earth. The wind was cold and clean.

She took a breath.

"I wish," she said, very softly, "to know that my dreams matter."

That was it. Not to have her dreams come true — she knew that wasn't how wishes worked. Just to know they MATTERED. That dreaming wasn't foolish. That wanting to see oceans from clouds and taste rain and make strangers laugh wasn't silly or pointless or a waste of a starfruit's time.

She looked up at the sky. The stars looked back.

And in that moment — that specific, perfect, once-in-a-decade moment — five stars in the sky aligned in a pattern that looked, from exactly this angle, like a five-pointed star.

Yángmáo gasped.

It lasted three seconds. Maybe four. Then the earth rotated, the stars shifted, and the pattern dissolved back into random cosmic scatter.

But for those three seconds, the sky had answered her.

Your dreams matter. You are a star.

"**謝謝**" (xièxie) Yángmáo whispered to the sky — "thank you" — and if her translucent edges glowed a little brighter in the starlight, well, that was just what starfruit do when they're happy.

She made her way back down the mountain in the dark, following the trail by the glow of her own edges and the distant lights of Taipei below. She was tired. She was cold. Her five points ached from climbing.

But she was full.

At the bottom of the trail, a night market vendor was selling fresh starfruit juice — sliced starfruit blended with honey and ice, poured into cups that caught the streetlight.

Yángmáo rolled up to the cart.

"**歐伊西**!" (ōuyīxī) a customer said, sipping the juice — "yummy!"

And Yángmáo, watching her kin being sliced into perfect five-pointed stars and dropped into cups of golden juice, felt not sadness but pride. Because every slice was a star. Every star was a wish. And every wish — spoken, unspoken, whispered to a mountain or shouted into the wind — mattered.

Every single one.

Close your eyes and let the Milky Way carry you to sleep. Every point is a wish. And every wish matters. Goodnight, little star. Goodnight.
