# Story 17: Pudding and the Wobble Contest

**Main Character:** Bùdīng (布丁 bùdīng) — Pudding
**Guest Characters:** None
**Setting:** Breeze Center mall, Taipei
**Theme:** Embracing imperfection
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 布丁 (bùdīng) = pudding

---

Bùdīng was wobbling, and he wanted everyone to know that he was FINE.

"I'm fine! I'm fine! ...I'm wobbling."

He was always wobbling. That was the problem. Or was it the point? Bùdīng could never decide, which made him wobble more, which made him more anxious, which made him wobble even MORE, in a vicious cycle of existential jiggle.

**布丁** (bùdīng) = pudding — specifically, the Taiwanese-style caramel custard pudding that comes in little plastic cups at every convenience store, every dessert shop, and every school lunchbox in the country. It was simple, sweet, wobbly, and beloved by approximately every child in **台灣** (Táiwān) = Taiwan.

Bùdīng was a classic: golden custard on top, dark caramel drizzled down the sides, with the shape of an upside-down cup — wider at the top, narrower at the bottom, which made him fundamentally, architecturally, unavoidably unstable.

He wobbled when he walked. He wobbled when he talked. He wobbled when he stood perfectly still, because the air conditioning in Breeze Center mall created tiny currents that set his gelatinous body into constant micro-oscillation.

"I'm fine!" he announced to his reflection in the mall floor. His reflection wobbled back at him mockingly.

**微風廣場** (Wéifēng Guǎngchǎng) = Breeze Center was one of Taipei's fanciest malls — all marble floors, designer boutiques, and a food court that served desserts so beautiful they belonged in museums. Bùdīng lived in the dessert display of a patisserie on the B1 level, surrounded by cakes that did NOT wobble.

The macarons sat perfectly still, in perfect rows, in perfect pastel colors. The mille-feuille stood at precise right angles, its layers of pastry and cream as stable as architecture. The chocolate truffles were smooth, round, and immovable. Even the crème brûlée, which was technically Bùdīng's cousin, had a hardened sugar crust that held it firmly in place.

Bùdīng had none of these advantages. He was just... custard. Held together by eggs and hope and a thin layer of caramel that was slowly, slowly dripping down his sides like golden tears.

"Stop wobbling," he told himself.

He wobbled.

"Seriously. STOP."

He wobbled harder.

The head pastry chef, a woman named Chef Yáng who had trained in Paris and had zero tolerance for imperfection, walked past the display case. She paused. She looked at Bùdīng. She frowned.

"That pudding is wobbling," she said.

"I'M FINE!" Bùdīng squeaked.

"It's not centered in the cup. The caramel distribution is uneven. And it won't. Stop. Wobbling." She sighed. "We have the mall's Dessert Showcase tomorrow. Every patisserie in Breeze Center is presenting their best work. I need perfection." She looked at Bùdīng with the expression of someone looking at the weakest link. "Not wobble."

Bùdīng felt his caramel go cold.

The Breeze Center Dessert Showcase was the Super Bowl of Taipei's pastry world. Every fancy dessert shop in the mall competed to present the most beautiful, most impressive, most Instagram-worthy creation. Last year, a patisserie on the third floor had won with a sugar sculpture of Taipei 101 that took seventy-two hours to build. The year before that, a chocolate fountain shaped like Sun Moon Lake.

These were NOT wobbly puddings.

"I'll be perfect," Bùdīng whispered. "I'll stop wobbling. I'll be firm and stable and—"

A wobble rippled through his body from top to bottom.

"—never mind."

That evening, after the mall closed and the lights dimmed to their overnight glow, Bùdīng practiced being still. He planted himself in the center of the display case, took a deep breath, and concentrated.

For three seconds, he was perfectly still.

Then the air conditioning kicked in and he wobbled so hard he slid two inches to the left.

"**不好意思**" (bù hǎo yìsi) he mumbled — "sorry" — to the macaron he bumped into.

"Ugh," said the macaron, who had been perfectly positioned and was now point-zero-three millimeters off-center, which in macaron terms was a CATASTROPHE.

Bùdīng spent the entire night trying to stop wobbling. He tried holding his breath. He tried clenching his custard. He tried thinking very firm thoughts, like oak trees and concrete and the tax code.

Nothing worked. He wobbled through every attempt.

By morning, he was exhausted, off-center, and his caramel had dripped into a lopsided puddle around his base that looked less like elegant sauce and more like a golden accident.

"I'm a mess," he said. "I'm a wobbly, dripping, imperfect mess."

The Showcase was at noon. Displays were being set up throughout the mall's central atrium. Each patisserie had a table, a spotlight, and a crowd of food bloggers with cameras. The pressure was immense. Chef Yáng arrived with a tower of perfect profiteroles, a geometric chocolate sculpture, and — relegated to the back corner of the display — Bùdīng.

"Just... try not to wobble," Chef Yáng said, positioning him on a tiny pedestal. "Can you do that? For one hour? Just be STILL?"

"I'll try," said Bùdīng. "I'm fine. Totally fine. Definitely not—"

Wobble.

Chef Yáng closed her eyes, prayed to the pastry gods, and went to check on her profiteroles.

The Showcase began. Crowds gathered. Cameras flashed. Food bloggers narrated in hushed, reverent tones, as if they were documenting the discovery of a new civilization rather than looking at cakes.

"And HERE we have a deconstructed matcha opera cake with gold leaf—"

"A stunning yuzu tart with crystallized citrus—"

"An architectural marvel in dark chocolate, representing the bridges of Dadaocheng—"

Nobody looked at Bùdīng. He sat on his tiny pedestal in the back corner, wobbling silently, caramel dripping, watching the world admire everything he was not.

And then: a kid.

She was maybe six years old, wandering away from her mother, drawn not to the spectacular chocolate sculptures or the pristine macarons, but to the wobbling thing in the back corner that looked like it was having a nervous breakdown.

She stood in front of Bùdīng and stared.

Bùdīng wobbled.

The girl giggled.

Bùdīng wobbled again.

The girl laughed harder.

Bùdīng, confused and anxious, wobbled EXTREMELY, his entire body oscillating like a tiny golden earthquake.

The girl HOWLED with laughter. She grabbed her mother's hand and dragged her over. "Mama! MAMA! Look at the pudding! It's DANCING!"

"I'm not dancing! I'm WOBBLING! There's a DIFFERENCE!"

"It's dancing! It's doing the pudding dance!"

More kids gathered. Then their parents. Then — inexorably, as crowds always do when something is genuinely, authentically funny — a full ring of people surrounded Bùdīng's pedestal, all watching the world's most anxious pudding wobble, drip, panic, and wobble some more.

And they were LAUGHING. Not mean laughing. Not at-him laughing. WITH-him laughing. The delighted, helpless, tears-in-your-eyes laughing that happens when something is so genuinely, unscriptedly, beautifully imperfect that it bypasses your brain and goes straight to your joy.

A food blogger pointed her camera at Bùdīng. "This is the most relatable dessert I've ever seen," she said to her followers. "It's literally me at every party. Trying to be cool. Wobbling instead."

"IT ME," someone commented.

"BIG PUDDING ENERGY."

"WE ARE ALL BÙDĪNG."

Within an hour, the video had fifty thousand views. Within two hours, two hundred thousand. Bùdīng — wobbly, dripping, perfectly imperfect Bùdīng — was the star of the Showcase.

Chef Yáng watched the crowd around her pudding with an expression that evolved from horror to confusion to reluctant understanding to — finally — a small, private smile.

"**太棒了**" (tài bàng le) she murmured to herself. "Awesome."

The Showcase judges — three of the most prestigious pastry critics in Taipei — made their rounds. They admired the chocolate sculptures. They praised the geometric tarts. They wrote careful notes about flavor profiles and presentation scores.

And then they reached Bùdīng.

The head judge — a stern woman with silver hair and glasses that could cut glass — looked at the pudding. The pudding wobbled.

She looked at the crowd around it. They were all smiling.

She looked at the caramel dripping unevenly down the sides. She looked at the off-center position on the pedestal. She looked at the tiny golden footprints where Bùdīng had shuffled nervously.

"This pudding," she announced, "is the most honest dessert in this Showcase."

Silence.

"Everything else here is trying to be perfect. This pudding is trying to be perfect too — and failing — and THAT is what makes it perfect. Because perfection is boring. Perfection is forgettable. But a pudding that wobbles? A pudding that wobbles is ALIVE."

She awarded Bùdīng the Showcase's Special Jury Prize: a tiny golden spoon with the inscription "For Excellence in Being Yourself."

Bùdīng wobbled so hard when they gave him the award that it fell off the pedestal and he had to chase it across the table.

The crowd cheered.

"I'm fine!" he shouted, clutching the golden spoon. "I'm totally fine! I'm definitely not crying! ...I'm wobbling AND crying!"

That evening, Chef Yáng carried Bùdīng back to the patisserie and placed him — gently, carefully — in the center of the display case.

"You know what?" she said. "Don't stop wobbling."

"Really?"

"Really. I spent my whole career trying to make things perfect. Smooth lines, precise angles, flawless surfaces. But you know what people remember? You know what makes them FEEL something?"

"Wobbles?"

"Wobbles. Imperfections. The messy, wobbly, dripping, beautiful proof that something was made by hand, with love, by someone who cared enough to try even when they couldn't get it right."

"**謝謝**" (xièxie) Bùdīng whispered — "thank you."

Then he wobbled, because that's what Bùdīng does, and that's what makes Bùdīng perfect.

## Goodnight Blessing

Tonight, wobbly dreamer, may you embrace every wobble, every stumble, every imperfect, dripping, golden moment. You don't have to be firm. You don't have to be still. You just have to be YOU — wobbling and all. The world loves a perfect sculpture, but the world ADORES a pudding that tries its best and wobbles anyway. Close your eyes and let your worries melt like caramel, and know that you are exactly, wonderfully, wobblingly enough. Goodnight, sweet pudding. Goodnight.
