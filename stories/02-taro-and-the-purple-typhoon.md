# Story 02: Taro and the Purple Typhoon

**Main Character:** Taro (芋頭 yùtou) — Taro
**Guest Characters:** None
**Setting:** Jiufen Old Street
**Theme:** Patience and strength
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 颱風 (táifēng) = typhoon

---

Taro had been sitting on the same shelf in the same dessert shop on the same winding street in Jiufen for exactly three weeks, and he didn't mind one bit.

"Good things take time to steam," he said to nobody in particular, adjusting his tiny reading glasses and turning the page of a very small book about the history of root vegetables. (It was a surprisingly gripping read. Chapter seven, about the great parsnip migration of 1847, had him absolutely riveted.)

Jiufen was the kind of place that made you believe in magic. Perched on a mountainside overlooking the ocean, the old mining town was a maze of narrow stone steps, red lanterns, and tea houses stacked on top of each other like a delicious game of Jenga. The streets smelled like taro balls in sweet soup — which, if you were Taro, was either very comforting or slightly alarming, depending on how you felt about smelling your own relatives cooking.

Taro felt fine about it. He was a philosopher.

"The taro that panics becomes mush," he often said. "The taro that waits becomes magnificent."

His shop, Grandma Lin's Taro Ball Parlor, was famous throughout all of **台灣** (Táiwān) = Taiwan. Every day, hundreds of tourists climbed the steep stone steps of **九份老街** (Jiǔfèn lǎo jiē) = Jiufen Old Street just to taste Grandma Lin's taro balls — soft, chewy, purple spheres of glory served in warm sweet soup with red bean and grass jelly.

Grandma Lin herself was nearly as old as the mountain. She had been making taro balls since she was twelve years old, and she was now somewhere north of eighty. She moved slowly, spoke softly, and made the most extraordinary taro balls anyone had ever tasted.

"Her secret," Taro told visitors who asked, "is patience. She never rushes. She steams the taro for exactly the right amount of time. She kneads the dough until it's perfect. She rolls each ball with love. You cannot rush love."

The other dessert ingredients in the shop — a jumpy pile of sweet potato balls, a bowl of nervous tapioca pearls, and a vat of grass jelly that never said much — all looked up to Taro. He was the eldest. The wisest. The purplest.

Everything was peaceful in Jiufen.

Until the **颱風** (táifēng) = typhoon came.

It started as a whisper. A breeze that was slightly too warm. A sky that turned the color of a bruise. The ocean, visible from the shop's window, began to churn like a washing machine full of gray laundry.

"**小心**" (xiǎoxīn) whispered Grandma Lin, peering out the window — "be careful." She began pulling in the hanging lanterns, folding up the outdoor tables, and battening down the wooden shutters with the efficiency of someone who had survived fifty typhoons and wasn't about to lose to number fifty-one.

But this typhoon was different.

The weather reports called it Typhoon Zǐsè — The Purple Typhoon — because of the strange violet tinge to its clouds. Meteorologists were baffled. Weather bloggers were ecstatic. And Taro felt a strange tingling in his starchy core, as if the storm were calling specifically to him.

"That's ridiculous," said the sweet potato balls, who were huddled together in a bowl like a rugby team in a scrum. "Typhoons don't call taro. Typhoons call insurance adjusters."

"Hush," said Taro. "Listen."

They listened. The wind was rising, howling through the narrow stone streets of Jiufen like a thousand ghosts playing kazoos. Rain hammered the roof. The lanterns outside, the ones Grandma Lin hadn't brought in yet, swung wildly on their strings like tiny red pendulums.

And then, through the noise, Taro heard it.

A voice. Small, scared, and coming from somewhere up the mountain.

"Help! Please! I'm stuck!"

Without thinking — which was unusual for Taro, who normally thought about everything for a very long time before doing anything — he rolled off his shelf, dropped to the floor with a dusty purple THUD, and headed for the door.

"Are you INSANE?" shrieked the tapioca pearls. "There's a typhoon out there!"

"Someone needs help," said Taro simply.

"But you'll get mushed! You'll get waterlogged! You'll get—"

"I'll get there when I get there," said Taro. "Good things take time."

He squeezed through the crack under the door and stepped out into the storm.

The wind hit him like a freight train made of rain. For a moment, he couldn't see anything — just sheets of water and swirling purple clouds. But Taro was heavy. Dense. Solid. While lesser ingredients would have been blown away like leaves, Taro planted his stubby legs and leaned into the wind.

Step. By. Step.

The stone stairs of Jiufen were rivers now, water cascading down them in waterfalls. Taro climbed them one at a time, gripping the rough stone with his earthy skin. His reading glasses were immediately swept away — "Those were prescription," he muttered sadly — but he pressed on.

The voice came again: "Help! Up here!"

It was coming from the old temple at the top of the hill — the one that overlooked the entire bay. Taro climbed and climbed, past shuttered shops and swaying trees and a very confused cat that was clinging to a mailbox with all four paws and a look of profound betrayal.

"**加油**!" (jiāyóu) Taro called to the cat — "keep going!" The cat yowled back something that probably wasn't polite.

Finally, he reached the temple. Or what was left of it. The wind had torn away part of the roof, and the old wooden doors were banging open and shut like clapping hands. And there, trapped under a fallen beam at the entrance, was the smallest, most frightened taro ball Taro had ever seen.

She was barely an inch across — a baby taro ball, freshly rolled, pale purple with wide, terrified eyes. The wooden beam across her middle wasn't crushing her — she was too squishy for that — but it had her pinned to the ground, and the rising water was creeping toward her.

"I—I was trying to see the typhoon," she sniffled. "Mama said to stay in the kitchen, but I wanted to see, and then the wind knocked over the beam, and—"

"Shh," said Taro. "I'm here now. Let's get you out."

He studied the beam. It was thick — too heavy for one old taro chunk to lift alone. But Taro had something better than muscles: he had patience.

He found a loose tile on the temple floor. He wedged it under the beam. Then he found another tile, and stacked it on the first. Then a small stone. Then a piece of broken pottery.

"What are you doing?" asked the little taro ball.

"Building a lever," said Taro. "It's physics. If I put the fulcrum here and the effort there, even someone as small and old as me can move a beam ten times his weight. **太棒了** (tài bàng le) = awesome, right?"

"It's going to work?"

"Good things take time to steam," said Taro, positioning a sturdy stick he'd found against the tile stack. "But they always, always cook through in the end."

He pushed.

Nothing happened.

He pushed harder.

A creak.

He pushed with everything he had — every ounce of starchy, purple, steamed-to-perfection strength — and the beam groaned, tilted, and rolled just enough for the little taro ball to squeeze out.

She popped free like a cork from a bottle and immediately threw her tiny arms around Taro's middle. "**謝謝**!" (xièxie) she cried — "thank you!"

"You're welcome," Taro wheezed. "Now let's get off this mountain before we both become soup."

They made their way down the stone steps together, the enormous old taro and the tiny young taro ball, step by step through the howling purple storm. The wind screamed. The rain hammered. Twice, Taro lost his footing and slid down three steps, landing on his purple backside with a sound like a sack of flour hitting a trampoline. Each time, the little taro ball helped him up.

"We're almost there," she said, tugging his arm. "**加油**, Uncle Taro!"

By the time they reached Grandma Lin's shop, the worst of the typhoon had passed. The sky was clearing from the edges, revealing patches of star-filled darkness behind the retreating purple clouds. The lanterns that had survived the storm swung gently now, like tired dancers catching their breath.

Grandma Lin opened the door, saw the two taro characters dripping on her doorstep, and did what any Taiwanese grandmother would do in that situation.

She made soup.

Hot, sweet, gingery soup, with fresh taro balls bobbing on the surface like happy purple planets. She set the bowl down on the counter and smiled her ancient, crinkly smile.

"**吃飽了嗎**?" (chī bǎo le ma) she asked — "have you eaten yet?" — which is the Taiwanese way of saying "I love you."

Taro looked at the soup. He looked at the little taro ball, who was already eyeing the bowl with the hungry enthusiasm of someone who had been trapped in a typhoon and was absolutely famished. He looked at Grandma Lin, whose patient hands had shaped a thousand taro balls with the same care and gentleness.

"I believe," said Taro, slowly lowering himself into the warm sweet broth, "that it is time for me to become exactly what I was always meant to be."

The warmth enveloped him. The sweet ginger soup hugged every inch of his purple skin. The little taro ball splashed in beside him, giggling.

"Is this what it feels like?" she asked. "Being in the soup?"

"This," said Taro, closing his eyes with a peaceful smile, "is what it feels like to be home."

The little girl who had been waiting for her soup — the daughter of the temple caretaker, who had been worried sick about her missing taro ball — picked up her spoon and took a bite.

"**歐伊西**!" (ōuyīxī) she squealed — "yummy!"

And somewhere in that warm, sweet bowl, Taro smiled. Because he had been patient. He had been strong. He had taken his time. And like all good things that steam slowly, he had turned out absolutely perfect.

## Goodnight Blessing

Tonight, little one, may you have the patience of Taro — the wisdom to wait, the strength to keep climbing, and the warmth to know that you'll always get there in the end. Storms pass. Steps lead somewhere. And the best things in life are worth steaming slowly for. Curl up in your blankets like a taro ball in warm soup, and let the sweet ginger of sleep carry you to morning. Goodnight, purple dreamer. Goodnight.
