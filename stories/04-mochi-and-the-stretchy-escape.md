# Story 04: Mochi and the Stretchy Escape

**Main Character:** Mochi (麻糬 máshǔ) — Mochi
**Guest Characters:** Willa Wobble (Little Blue Penguin, Penguinsnowboard)
**Setting:** Taipei MRT (Mass Rapid Transit)
**Theme:** Friendship
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 麻糬 (máshǔ) = mochi

---

Mochi had a problem, and the problem was that she loved everyone too much.

Not in a normal, healthy, "hey, I like you, let's hang out sometime" way. In a full-body, arms-stretched-across-the-room, "I WILL NEVER LET GO OF YOU EVER" way. Because Mochi was made of glutinous rice, and glutinous rice does one thing better than anything else in the world: it sticks.

"GROUP HUG!!!" Mochi yelled, launching herself at a surprised commuter on the Taipei **捷運** (jiéyùn) = MRT, which is what people in Taipei call their subway system. The commuter — a businessman in a suit who had been quietly reading the news on his phone — suddenly found himself with a softball-sized white blob stuck to his briefcase.

"What the—" he sputtered.

"I just love your energy!" Mochi beamed. "You look like you need a hug! Everyone needs a hug! STICK TOGETHER, that's what I always say!"

The businessman peeled Mochi off his briefcase with some difficulty (she left a powdery starch residue on the leather) and set her on the orange plastic seat beside him. "Please don't do that," he said.

"Okay!" said Mochi brightly. Then she hugged the seat.

This was a typical morning for Mochi.

She lived in the Taipei MRT system — not by choice, exactly, but because she had fallen out of someone's paper bag on the Tamsui-Xinyi line three months ago and had been riding the trains ever since. She loved it. The MRT was clean, efficient, and full of people who looked like they needed hugs. (Most of them did not want hugs. Mochi had not yet accepted this information.)

The **麻糬** (máshǔ) = mochi in Taiwan was special. Not like the Japanese kind — Taiwanese mochi was its own thing, often filled with peanut powder or red bean or sesame, wrapped in a delicate skin of pounded glutinous rice. Mochi was proud of her heritage. She was soft, she was pillowy, she was dusted with potato starch, and she could stretch her arms to absolutely ridiculous lengths.

Which was about to become very important.

It happened at Taipei Main Station, the biggest, most confusing station in the entire MRT system. Taipei Main Station was the kind of place where you could walk in a straight line for ten minutes and end up exactly where you started, but somehow on a different floor. It had underground malls, food courts, six different subway lines, a regular train station, AND a high-speed rail terminal, all connected by a labyrinth of tunnels that even GPS gave up on.

Mochi was bouncing through the station, admiring the tile floors (so shiny! so huggable!), when she heard a small, frightened voice.

"Excuse me? Hello? Can anyone... can anyone help?"

The voice was coming from behind a vending machine in a quiet corner of the B1 level. Mochi stretched her neck around the corner (literally stretched it, like taffy, about two feet) and spotted the most unusual creature she had ever seen.

It was a penguin. A very small penguin. A Little Blue penguin, to be precise, standing about ten inches tall with slate-blue feathers and the most enormous worried eyes. She was carrying a tiny purple snowboard and looking around with the expression of someone who had been lost for approximately forever.

"Oh my gosh!" Mochi gasped. "A **企鵝** (qì'é) = penguin! In the MRT! This is the best day of my LIFE!"

The penguin flinched. "Please don't yell. I'm already very overwhelmed."

"Sorry! Sorry! I'm Mochi. I'm a mochi. It's both my name and what I am, which I realize is confusing. Who are you? Why are you in a subway station? Can I hug you?"

"I'm Willa Wobble," said the penguin quietly. "I'm from... somewhere else. Somewhere cold. I was on an adventure with my snowboard and I think I took a very wrong turn. I've been trying to find the exit for two hours but all the signs are in Chinese and I don't—"

"I'LL HELP YOU!" Mochi exploded. "That's what friends are for! Stick together, right? RIGHT?"

"We... we just met," said Willa.

"Details! Come on!"

And before Willa could protest, Mochi had wrapped one stretchy arm around the penguin's flipper and was dragging her into the bustling chaos of Taipei Main Station.

Now, here is where I need to explain something about Willa Wobble. Willa was small. She was shy. Her catchphrase was "The smallest step is still a step." She was the kind of person (well, penguin) who took thirty minutes to decide which flavor of ice cream to order, not because she was indecisive, but because she wanted to make sure she appreciated every option equally.

And Mochi was... Mochi.

"THIS WAY!" Mochi hollered, stretching her arm around a corner to point down a corridor while her body was still three meters behind. "No wait, THAT WAY! No wait—"

"Do you actually know where the exit is?" Willa asked.

Mochi paused. Her stretched arm snapped back to her body like a rubber band. "Not exactly. But I know every train line! I've been riding them for three months!"

"Riding them isn't the same as knowing where you're going."

"Isn't it, though?"

"No. It literally isn't."

They stood in the middle of the underground mall, surrounded by a swirling river of commuters. Signs pointed in seventeen different directions. An announcement echoed in Mandarin, Taiwanese, Hakka, and English, each version slightly different from the last. Willa clutched her tiny purple snowboard and trembled.

"I think," Willa said very quietly, "I might be stuck here forever."

Mochi looked at her new friend. She saw the trembling flippers. The too-big eyes. The way Willa held her snowboard like a security blanket. And for the first time in her sticky, huggy life, Mochi realized that what Willa needed wasn't a hug.

What Willa needed was someone to slow down.

"Okay," said Mochi, and her voice was softer now — still warm, still Mochi, but gentle. "Okay, Willa. Here's what we're going to do. We're going to take one step at a time. Your pace. Not mine."

Willa looked up. "Really?"

"Really. The smallest step is still a step, right? That's your thing?"

Willa's beak quivered. "How did you know that?"

"Lucky guess. Now: which direction feels right?"

Willa looked around slowly. She studied the signs, the flow of people, the direction of the escalators. She took a deep breath.

"Left," she said. "I think... left."

"Then left it is."

They went left. It was wrong.

They ended up in a food court, surrounded by steaming bowls of **牛肉麵** (niúròu miàn) = beef noodle soup and the aggressive scent of fried chicken. Mochi's stomach growled. Willa's beak opened in alarm.

"Okay, not left. That's fine! We eliminate options!" Mochi said cheerfully. "Scientists do that! We're basically scientists now!"

They tried right. It was also wrong. They ended up at the high-speed rail ticket counter, where a confused employee tried to sell Willa a ticket to Kaohsiung.

They tried straight ahead. Wrong again. They were now somehow in a bookstore.

"I'm never getting out of here," Willa whispered.

"Hey." Mochi gently placed a stretchy hand on Willa's flipper. "Hey. Look at me."

Willa looked.

"Do you know what mochi is made of? Glutinous rice. You know what glutinous means? Sticky. I was literally BORN to stick with people. And I'm sticking with you. We'll figure this out."

Willa sniffled. "You promise?"

"Cross my sticky heart."

It was Willa who finally cracked the code. She noticed something Mochi — who had been riding these trains for months — had never seen. The emergency evacuation maps on the walls. Small, detailed maps with clear arrows pointing to every exit.

"There," Willa said, pointing with a trembling flipper. "Exit M6. It leads to the street. We go down this corridor, turn right at the escalator, and we'll see daylight."

"How did you figure that out?!" Mochi was amazed.

"I read the map," Willa said simply. "The smallest step is still a step. And the first step is always to stop and look."

They followed the map. Right turn at the blue pillar. Left at the coffee shop. Down the long corridor with the mosaic murals. And then — THEN — they saw it.

Daylight. Actual, beautiful, Taipei daylight streaming down the stairs of Exit M6.

"**太棒了**!" (tài bàng le) Mochi screamed — "AWESOME!" — and she and Willa burst out of the station and into the warm, bright, scooter-buzzing chaos of Taipei.

Willa stood on the sidewalk, blinking in the sun, her tiny purple snowboard under one flipper. She took a deep breath of tropical air — which was very different from the Antarctic air she was used to, but in a good way — and smiled.

"I did it," she said. "I mean... WE did it."

"You did it," said Mochi. "I was just the sticky part. You were the smart part."

"We needed both parts."

They stood there for a moment, a small blue penguin and a small white mochi, in the middle of a Taipei sidewalk, while scooters whizzed past and a street vendor nearby called out "**來來來**!" and the whole enormous, delicious city hummed around them.

"So," said Mochi. "Now that you're out... what do you want to do?"

Willa looked at the city. She looked at her snowboard. She looked at Mochi.

"I think," she said, "I want to find some mochi. The eating kind. I heard Taiwanese mochi is extraordinary."

Mochi grinned — a wide, powdery, pillowy grin. "Oh, Willa. Have I got a treat for you."

She led Willa to the nearest mochi shop — a tiny place with a steamy kitchen and trays of fresh mochi dusted in peanut powder. The shopkeeper gave Willa a free sample (penguins in Taipei are apparently excellent for business) and Willa took one bite.

"This is..." Willa's eyes went wide. "This is the most extraordinary thing I've ever tasted."

"That's what I taste like!" Mochi said proudly. "I mean, that's what my people taste like. Soft, sticky, sweet, and full of heart."

Willa took another bite. And another. And when she was done, she looked at Mochi with the warmest, most grateful expression a penguin face can make, which is surprisingly warm.

"**謝謝**" (xièxie) Willa said — "thank you." Not just for the mochi. For everything.

"Stick together," Mochi whispered. "Always."

Willa nodded. Then she picked up her tiny purple snowboard, gave Mochi one last look, and waddled off down the busy Taipei street — the smallest penguin in the biggest city, taking one step at a time.

And Mochi? Mochi watched her go, feeling something she'd never quite felt before. Not the urge to hug. Not the need to stick. Just a quiet, warm, stretchy feeling in her heart that meant: I found a real friend today. And sometimes friendship isn't about holding on tight. Sometimes it's about knowing when to let go — and trusting that the bond stretches, no matter how far apart you are.

## Goodnight Blessing

Tonight, little friend, may you be as stretchy as Mochi and as brave as Willa. May you always find someone to help you out of the maze, and may you always BE that someone for somebody else. Friendship is the stickiest, stretchiest, most unbreakable thing in the world. So close your eyes, wrap yourself in blankets like mochi in peanut powder, and know that somewhere out there, your friends are thinking of you. Goodnight, sticky heart. Goodnight.
