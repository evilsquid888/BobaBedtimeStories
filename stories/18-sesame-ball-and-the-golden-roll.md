# Story 18: Sesame Ball and the Golden Roll

**Main Character:** Zhīma (芝麻球 zhīmaqiú) — Sesame Ball
**Guest Characters:** None
**Setting:** Sun Moon Lake, Nantou County
**Theme:** Bravery needs heart
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 芝麻球 (zhīmaqiú) = sesame ball

---

Zhīma rolled before he thought, charged before he planned, and apologized before he figured out what he'd crashed into.

"Roll with it!" he shouted, launching himself off the snack cart and down the hill toward the glittering expanse of **日月潭** (Rì Yuè Tán) = Sun Moon Lake, the most beautiful lake in all of Taiwan and — as of three seconds ago — Zhīma's next conquest.

**芝麻球** (zhīmaqiú) = sesame ball — a deep-fried ball of glutinous rice dough, coated in white sesame seeds, hollow inside with a sweet bean paste filling. Crispy, golden, round, and made for rolling. Zhīma was everything a sesame ball should be: bold, crunchy on the outside, sweet on the inside, and physically incapable of standing still.

Sun Moon Lake sat in the mountains of Nantou County, surrounded by misty peaks and aboriginal villages, its waters shifting between emerald green and midnight blue depending on the light. The lake got its name because the eastern half was shaped like a sun and the western half like a crescent moon. It was serene. Peaceful. Contemplative.

Zhīma was none of these things.

"INCOMING!" he yelled, rolling down the lakeside path at approximately the speed of a panicked armadillo, scattering tourists like bowling pins. He bounced off a railing, ricocheted off a trash can, and came to rest at the feet of a very old woman who was feeding fish from the pier.

"**不好意思**!" (bù hǎo yìsi) Zhīma gasped — "sorry!"

The old woman looked down at the golden, sesame-coated sphere at her feet. "You're in a hurry."

"I'm ALWAYS in a hurry! Life is short! Sesame balls are crunchy! Crunch waits for no one!"

"Where are you going?"

Zhīma paused. This was an excellent question, and he didn't have an answer.

"Somewhere! Anywhere! EVERYWHERE!"

The old woman smiled. She had the kind of smile that suggested she'd seen a thousand brave, foolish things charge past her and knew exactly how most of them ended up.

"Sun Moon Lake has been here for thousands of years," she said. "It will still be here after you've finished rolling."

"But I might NOT be here!" Zhīma protested. "I'm a fried snack! My crunch has an expiration date!"

"Then perhaps you should make sure you're rolling toward something that matters."

Zhīma considered this while his sesame seeds dried in the mountain air. It was, he had to admit, annoyingly wise.

The event that brought Zhīma to Sun Moon Lake was the annual Thao Tribe Swimming Carnival — a legendary race where swimmers crossed the lake from one shore to the other, a distance of about three kilometers. It was one of the biggest sporting events in Taiwan, drawing thousands of swimmers and tens of thousands of spectators.

But Zhīma wasn't there to swim. He was there because he'd heard a rumor.

There was a cave. Behind the waterfall on the north side of the lake. Inside the cave, according to local Thao legend, was a golden stone — the Heart of the Lake — that granted the holder not a wish, but something better: the courage to know what to wish FOR.

"That's what I need," Zhīma had told himself. "I've got bravery. I've got PLENTY of bravery. What I don't have is... direction. I roll and roll and roll, but where am I actually GOING?"

Finding the cave required getting to the north side of the lake, which required either taking the tourist boat (boring), hiking the trail (slow), or doing what Zhīma always did: rolling directly across the water.

He chose option three.

"Roll with it!" he shouted, and launched himself off the pier.

He hit the water and immediately sank.

Because sesame balls, as it turns out, are not boats.

"GLUB," said Zhīma, which is what you say when you're a fried snack sinking to the bottom of Taiwan's deepest lake.

He sank past startled fish. Past waving water plants. Past an ancient log that looked like it had opinions about impulsive sesame balls. The water was cold and dark and very, very deep, and Zhīma realized — with the sudden clarity that comes from drowning — that bravery without thinking is just falling.

But then something happened. The sweet bean paste inside Zhīma began to warm. Not from external heat, but from something internal — a stubborn, sticky, golden warmth that refused to go cold. It was his heart. His actual, literal, bean-paste heart. And it was saying: you're not done yet.

Zhīma kicked. His tiny golden legs were not designed for swimming, but they were designed for TRYING. He paddled and flailed and thrashed his way upward, his sesame seed coating acting like tiny flotation devices as air trapped beneath the seeds created buoyancy.

He broke the surface, gasping, spluttering, and profoundly grateful for physics.

"**加油**!" (jiāyóu) called a fisherman from a nearby boat — "keep going!" — either because he was encouraging or because he'd never seen a deep-fried snack swim before and wanted to see what happened.

Zhīma paddled. It was absurd. It was undignified. It was a golden ball covered in sesame seeds doing a doggy paddle across a sacred lake. But he did it. Stroke after stroke, splash after splash, he crossed the water.

It took forty-five minutes. By the time he reached the north shore, he was waterlogged, his crunch was compromised, and three of his sesame seeds had defected to a passing duck.

But he was there.

The waterfall was tucked into a fold in the mountainside, screened by ferns and bamboo. Water tumbled over mossy rocks into a pool so clear Zhīma could see every pebble on the bottom. Behind the curtain of water, partially hidden by spray and mist, was the cave entrance — a dark opening in the rock, just wide enough for a slightly soggy sesame ball to roll through.

Zhīma took a breath. "Roll with it," he whispered, and rolled in.

The cave was surprisingly warm. The volcanic geology of the region meant the rocks radiated gentle heat, drying Zhīma's soaked exterior and restoring some of his precious crunch. The passage was narrow, twisting, lit by glowing minerals in the walls that cast everything in a soft golden light.

He rolled deeper. The passage opened into a chamber.

And there, on a natural stone pedestal in the center of the chamber, was... nothing.

No golden stone. No Heart of the Lake. Just an empty pedestal with a small inscription carved into its surface.

Zhīma rolled up to it and read.

The inscription was in old Chinese characters, partially worn by time and moisture:

"勇氣不在外面，在裡面。" (yǒngqì bù zài wàimiàn, zài lǐmiàn.)

"Courage is not outside. It is inside."

Zhīma stared at the inscription for a long time.

Then he looked down at himself. His golden crust. His sesame seed coating. His hollow interior with its sweet bean paste heart.

"The treasure was never in the cave," he said slowly. "The treasure is the bean paste."

It sounded ridiculous. But it was true.

Zhīma had always thought his bravery came from his crunch — his hard exterior, his fearlessness, his willingness to charge into anything. But the crunch was just the outside. The REAL bravery, the thing that had kept him kicking when he was sinking to the bottom of the lake, was the warmth inside. The soft, sweet, stubborn heart that refused to stop believing.

Bravery needs heart.

Not bravery instead of heart. Not bravery OR heart. Bravery NEEDS heart. The outside can be as golden and crusty as it wants, but without the sweet filling on the inside, it's just an empty shell.

Zhīma rolled out of the cave and sat on the shore of Sun Moon Lake as the afternoon sun painted the water gold. The lake was calm. The mountains were reflected in its surface like a painting within a painting. Somewhere across the water, he could hear the cheers of the swimming carnival.

The old woman from the pier was there. She had walked the trail (the sensible route) and was sitting on a rock, feeding fish from a paper bag.

"Did you find what you were looking for?" she asked.

"I found out where I'm going," said Zhīma.

"And where is that?"

Zhīma looked at the lake. He looked at the mountains. He looked at the sun setting behind them, turning everything the color of his own golden crust.

"Wherever my heart takes me," he said. "But this time, I'm going to let it lead."

A vendor by the pier was selling fresh sesame balls — hot from the fryer, golden, dusted with sesame, filled with sweet red bean paste. A family walked up and bought a bag.

"**好吃**!" (hǎochī) the youngest child said, biting into one — "delicious!"

And the taste — crunchy outside, sweet inside, warm all the way through — was the taste of bravery with heart. The taste of a golden ball who finally learned that the treasure isn't the destination.

It's the bean paste you carry with you.
