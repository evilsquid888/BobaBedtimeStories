# Story 13: Passion Fruit and the Sour Dare

**Main Character:** Bǎi (百香果 bǎixiāngguǒ) — Passion Fruit
**Guest Characters:** None
**Setting:** Taitung hot springs
**Theme:** Being bold
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 百香果 (bǎixiāngguǒ) = passion fruit

---

Bǎi was trouble, and she loved every single second of it.

"Pucker up, buttercup!" she cackled, squirting a stream of tangy orange juice at a passing butterfly. The butterfly dodged. Bǎi did not care. She was already looking for her next target.

Bǎi was a **百香果** (bǎixiāngguǒ) = passion fruit — round, wrinkly, purple-brown on the outside, and absolutely EXPLODING with bright orange pulp and crunchy black seeds on the inside. She was the kind of fruit that made your face scrunch up on the first taste (SOUR!) and then relax into a grin on the second taste (sweet!). She was surprise in fruit form. She was a dare with a stem.

She lived in the wild, gorgeous, slightly-unhinged countryside of **台東** (Táidōng) = Taitung, on Taiwan's east coast. Taitung was the island's last frontier — a place where mountains crashed into the Pacific Ocean, where rice paddies stretched to the horizon, and where the hot springs bubbled up from the earth like the planet was slowly making tea.

The hot springs were Bǎi's favorite place. Specifically, the hot springs at Zhīběn, a small village nestled in a valley of tropical trees where natural thermal water pooled in rocky basins, steaming in the morning mist like nature's own bathtubs.

Humans came from all over Taiwan to soak in these springs. They said the water was healing. Relaxing. Rejuvenating.

Bǎi said the water was the perfect temperature for pulling pranks.

"Dare you to jump in the sulfur pool!" she shouted at a nervous tangerine who was visiting from Taipei.

"No way! It smells like rotten eggs!"

"That's the sulfur, baby! It's GOOD for you! Pucker up, buttercup!"

The tangerine fled. Bǎi shrugged. Not everyone was built for adventure.

That was the thing about Bǎi — she was ALWAYS daring someone. Jump off that rock. Taste that weird herb. Swim in the cold river. Tell that boy you like him. Ask that teacher the question nobody wants to ask. Take the path that looks scary. Eat the thing that smells weird.

"Life is too short to be sweet all the time!" she declared. "Sometimes you gotta be SOUR!"

Most of the local fruits and food ingredients in Taitung tolerated Bǎi the way you tolerate a friend who keeps trying to get you on roller coasters. They loved her, but they also kept a safe distance.

Until the day a kid named Jiàn-Háo arrived at the hot springs.

Jiàn-Háo was thirteen years old, visiting Taitung with his school group for an outdoor education trip. He was the kind of kid who stood at the back of every line, spoke so quietly you had to lean in to hear him, and had perfected the art of being invisible in a crowd. Not because he was shy, exactly, but because being invisible felt safer than being noticed.

Being noticed meant people might laugh. Or judge. Or worst of all, expect things.

The school group was at the hot springs for an adventure activity: river tracing. This meant hiking upstream through a shallow mountain river, climbing over boulders, wading through pools, and navigating small waterfalls. It was the kind of activity that made adventurous kids whoop and cautious kids contemplate faking a stomachache.

Jiàn-Háo was firmly in the stomachache camp.

"I don't think I can do this," he whispered to his friend while the instructor explained how to use the safety ropes.

"It'll be fun!" his friend said, already wearing a helmet and looking like he was about to invade a small country.

"Fun for YOU. You're not scared of anything."

"**加油**!" (jiāyóu) the instructor shouted — "let's GO!"

The group waded into the river. The water was cool — spring-fed, mountain-cold, clear enough to see every stone on the bottom. The kids shrieked and laughed and splashed. Jiàn-Háo brought up the rear, moving as slowly as humanly possible without technically standing still.

Bǎi watched from a rock above the river. She had been napping in the sun — passion fruits love sun, it's what makes them ripen — and the commotion woke her up. She studied the group with her seed-speckled eyes and immediately spotted Jiàn-Háo.

"Oh ho," she murmured. "We've got a cautious one."

She bounced down from the rock, rolled along the riverbank, and popped up on a boulder right in Jiàn-Háo's path.

"Hey, kid!" she called.

Jiàn-Háo looked down. A wrinkly purple-brown fruit was sitting on a rock and talking to him. In a normal world, this would have been alarming. In Taitung, where things were just a little bit magic, it was only mildly surprising.

"Um," said Jiàn-Háo. "Hi?"

"You look like you're having the time of your life," Bǎi said, her voice dripping with cheerful sarcasm.

"I'm fine."

"You're TERRIFIED."

"I'm FINE."

"Pucker up, buttercup. I can see you shaking from here. What's the deal?"

Jiàn-Háo glanced ahead. The group was approaching a small waterfall — maybe two meters high — where the instructor was showing them how to climb up using handholds in the rock. One by one, kids were scrambling up, water cascading around them, reaching the top and pumping their fists.

"I can't climb that," Jiàn-Háo said.

"Why not?"

"Because I'll fall. And everyone will see. And they'll laugh."

"So?"

"So?! SO?! That's the worst thing that could possibly happen!"

Bǎi stared at him. Then she burst out laughing — a tangy, crunchy, seed-spraying laugh that echoed off the valley walls.

"Kid," she said, "let me tell you something about passion fruit. You know what happens when someone cuts me open for the first time?"

"They... eat you?"

"Before that. First, they SEE me. And what they see is goop. Weird, orange, seedy goop. It looks like something a alien sneezed. It is NOT pretty. It is not Instagram-worthy. It is, by any reasonable standard, alarming."

Jiàn-Háo almost smiled. "Okay..."

"And then they TASTE me. And the first thing they taste is SOUR. Full-face, eye-squinting, lip-puckering SOUR. And for about two seconds, they think they've made a terrible mistake."

"And then?"

"And then the sweet hits. After the sour. BECAUSE of the sour. The sweetness only works because the sourness came first. If passion fruit was just sweet, it would be boring. It's the sour that makes it EXCITING."

Bǎi rolled closer to Jiàn-Háo, leaving a tangy scent trail on the rock.

"The thing you're scared of — the falling, the laughing, the failing — that's the sour part. And yeah, it might happen. You might fall. People might laugh. But AFTER the sour comes the sweet. The moment you get to the top. The feeling of 'I DID THAT.' The look on your own face when you realize you're braver than you thought."

Jiàn-Háo looked at the waterfall. Water crashed over the rocks. Kids were cheering.

"Dare me?" he asked.

Bǎi grinned — a wide, wrinkly, mischievous grin.

"Dare."

Jiàn-Háo walked to the waterfall. The instructor helped him find the first handhold. The water was cold on his legs, cold on his arms, cold on his face. Every cell in his body screamed TURN BACK.

He climbed.

His foot slipped once. He caught himself. His hand slipped once. He caught himself again. Water poured over his helmet and into his eyes and he couldn't see but he could FEEL — the rock under his fingers, the current pulling at his legs, the pure, electric, terrifying, SOUR thrill of doing something he'd been too scared to do.

He reached the top.

For a moment, he just stood there, river water streaming off his body, staring at the valley and the mountains and the impossibly blue Taitung sky.

Then he pumped both fists in the air and YELLED.

Not words. Just sound. Raw, joyful, completely unselfconscious sound. The kind of sound that comes from the place where fear turns into freedom.

"**太棒了**!" (tài bàng le) his classmates shouted — "AWESOME!"

And they weren't laughing AT him. They were cheering FOR him.

The sweet after the sour.

Bǎi, watching from her rock, wiped a tiny tangy tear from her eye. "That's my boy," she whispered.

The school group finished the river trace and ended up at the hot springs, where they soaked their tired muscles in the warm sulfur water. A vendor at the springs was selling fresh passion fruit juice — halved fruits with a straw stuck in, the orange pulp and seeds swirling inside.

Jiàn-Háo bought one. He took a sip.

His face scrunched. SOUR.

Then relaxed. Sweet.

Then broke into the biggest grin of his life.

"**好吃**!" (hǎochī) he said — "delicious!" And he meant the juice, and the waterfall, and the whole wild, sour-sweet, terrifying, beautiful day.

Bǎi, sitting in the fruit basket, heard him laugh and felt her orange pulp glow with pride. Because that's what passion fruit does. It doesn't promise you an easy ride. It doesn't pretend life is always sweet. It says: pucker up, buttercup. Take the dare. Climb the waterfall. Taste the sour.

The sweet is coming.

It always is.
