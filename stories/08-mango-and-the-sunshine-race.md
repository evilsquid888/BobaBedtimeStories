# Story 08: Mango and the Sunshine Race

**Main Character:** Mango (芒果 mángguǒ) — Mango
**Guest Characters:** Barnaby Beaksworth (Chinstrap Penguin, Penguinsnowboard)
**Setting:** Pingtung farms, southern Taiwan
**Theme:** Making the most of your season
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 芒果 (mángguǒ) = mango

---

Mango was running late, and for Mango, running late was basically a crime against nature.

"Catch me if you can — I'm in season!" he shouted, zooming through the mango orchards of Pingtung like a small, orange, extremely competitive missile. His tiny sweatband was soaked with juice, his bright yellow-orange skin was flushed with a rosy blush, and he was leaving a trail of golden droplets on the red dirt path like a sprinter leaving sweat on a track.

It was June. Peak mango season. And in the orchards of **屏東** (Píngdōng) = Pingtung — the southernmost county in Taiwan, where the sun was stronger, the air was sweeter, and the mangoes were the best in the entire world — June meant one thing: the annual **芒果** (mángguǒ) = mango race.

The Pingtung Mango Marathon was the most prestigious sporting event in the Taiwanese fruit world. Mangoes from all over the island came to compete — Irwin mangoes, Keitt mangoes, tiny champagne mangoes, enormous Haden mangoes — all racing across a five-kilometer course through the sun-drenched orchards.

And Mango intended to win.

He had been training since April. Running laps around the orchard. Doing push-ups on tree branches. Practicing his "victory slide" — a dramatic finish-line move where he skidded across the ground on his belly, spraying juice in a golden arc. It was athletically questionable but aesthetically MAGNIFICENT.

"Gotta stay in the zone," he muttered, jogging through the morning mist between rows of mango trees so loaded with fruit they looked like they were wearing Christmas ornaments. "Focus. Speed. Victory."

The problem was, Mango had exactly three weeks of season left.

That's the thing about mangoes — about ALL seasonal fruit, really. You get a window. A beautiful, brief, perfect window where the sun and the rain and the soil all align, and you are the most delicious thing on the planet. And then the window closes. The season ends. The heat fades. And you either get eaten, get dried, get frozen, or get composted.

Mango did NOT intend to get composted.

"Three weeks," he said, studying a calendar he'd pinned to a tree trunk. "Twenty-one days of peak ripeness. I need to win this race, eat the most sunshine I can, and become the greatest mango Pingtung has ever produced. No time to waste."

He was so focused on the calendar that he didn't notice the penguin until he tripped over him.

"OOF!" Mango went flying, tumbled three times, and landed in a heap of orange juice and wounded pride.

"Oh dear, oh dear, I'm so sorry!" said a round, friendly voice. "There's always enough fish for one more — but I suppose in this case, enough room for one more on this path?"

Mango blinked juice out of his eyes and stared. Sitting on the dirt path, surrounded by snack wrappers and crumbs, was a chinstrap penguin. He was wearing a bib (for some reason), sitting on an orange pizza-shaped snowboard (for even less reason), and eating a mango sticky rice from a styrofoam container with every appearance of profound contentment.

"What," said Mango flatly, "are you."

"I'm Barnaby Beaksworth! Chinstrap penguin, snack enthusiast, and currently the happiest bird in all of Pingtung. This mango sticky rice is EXTRAORDINARY." He held out the container. "Would you like some?"

"That's..." Mango squinted. "That's MANGO sticky rice. You're eating my PEOPLE."

"Oh! Oh dear. Is that... is that rude? I didn't mean—"

"I'm joking. We're proud to be in sticky rice. It's like the Olympics of mango cuisine. But what are you doing in a mango orchard in PINGTUNG? Aren't you supposed to be somewhere cold?"

Barnaby wiped his beak. "I'm on a culinary tour of **台灣** (Táiwān) = Taiwan. I heard the mangoes here are the finest in the world, and as a snack oracle — that's my official title, self-appointed but very real — I simply HAD to investigate."

"Snack oracle."

"I can tell the future through food! Well, I can tell when food is good. Which is always. The future is always delicious, Mango."

Mango had no time for delusional penguins. He had a race to train for. "Look, Barnaby, it was nice meeting you, but I'm in training and I've only got three weeks of season left and—"

"Three weeks! How wonderful!"

"How is that WONDERFUL? Three weeks is NOTHING!"

Barnaby set down his sticky rice and looked at Mango with an expression of such warm, genuine confusion that Mango almost forgot to be annoyed.

"Three weeks of being the most delicious fruit on the entire island," said Barnaby. "Three weeks of sunshine and sweetness and being someone's absolute favorite thing. Do you know how many creatures in this world would give anything for three weeks of that?"

Mango opened his mouth. Then closed it. Then opened it again. "I... I never thought about it that way."

"That's because you're too busy running." Barnaby patted the ground beside him. "Sit. Eat. The best training for any race is knowing why you're running."

So Mango sat. In the middle of a mango orchard, under a tree dripping with fruit, beside a penguin on a pizza-shaped snowboard. He was supposed to be doing sprints. He was supposed to be in the zone.

Instead, he watched the sunrise.

The Pingtung sunrise was something else. It came up over the Central Mountain Range like a slow golden explosion, painting the sky in layers of orange and pink and purple. The mango trees caught the light and glowed — every fruit shining like a tiny lantern. The air warmed by degrees, and with each degree, the fragrance of ripe mango intensified until the entire orchard smelled like heaven had opened a smoothie shop.

"**好吃**" (hǎochī) Barnaby whispered — "delicious" — but he wasn't talking about food. He was talking about the moment.

"Okay," Mango admitted. "This is pretty great."

They spent the morning together. Barnaby took Mango to visit every mango stall, farm stand, and fruit vendor in Pingtung. They tasted everything: **芒果冰** (mángguǒ bīng) = mango shaved ice, mango smoothies, dried mango, mango with chili salt, mango with sticky rice, mango with condensed milk, mango with nothing at all — just a fresh, ripe mango cut into cubes and eaten with a toothpick.

At every stop, Barnaby said the same thing: "There's always enough fish for one more!" Then he'd immediately order more food.

"You eat an alarming amount," Mango observed.

"I appreciate an alarming amount," Barnaby corrected. "There's a difference."

By afternoon, they had circled back to the orchard. The race was the next morning, and Mango realized he'd spent his entire training day eating and watching sunrises with a penguin.

"I should be panicking," he said.

"Are you?"

"No. And THAT'S what's panicking me."

Barnaby laughed — a warm, bubbly, fishy laugh. "Mango, can I tell you something? As a snack oracle?"

"Sure."

"You're going to be wonderful. Not because you're the fastest mango. Not because you trained the hardest. But because you showed up during YOUR season, you soaked up YOUR sunshine, and you became the most YOU you could be. That's what makes a great mango. That's what makes a great anything."

The race the next morning was spectacular. Fifty mangoes lined up at the starting line, sleek and golden in the Pingtung sun. The air hummed with competitive energy and the scent of ripe fruit.

"**加油**!" (jiāyóu) the crowd shouted — "go for it!"

The gun fired. The mangoes launched.

Mango ran. He ran not with the frantic, panicked energy of someone trying to outpace his own expiration date, but with the joyful, golden, all-in energy of someone making the absolute most of his season. Every stride was sunshine. Every breath was sweetness. Every moment was peak ripeness.

He didn't come in first.

He came in third, behind a particularly aerodynamic Irwin mango from Tainan and a surprise champion champagne mango who was approximately the size of a golf ball but fast as lightning.

But when he crossed the finish line, juice streaming down his sides, sweatband soaked, rosy blush brighter than ever — he did his victory slide anyway.

The crowd went WILD.

"**太棒了**!" (tài bàng le) they screamed — "awesome!"

Barnaby was in the front row, waving an orange flag and holding a tray of mango sticky rice. "THIRD PLACE! That's BRONZE! There's always enough fish for one more — and enough podium for one more, too!"

Mango stood on the bronze podium, and for the first time, he didn't feel like third place was losing. It felt like exactly where he was supposed to be. He'd run his race. He'd tasted his season. He'd soaked up every drop of Pingtung sunshine.

That evening, a fruit vendor at the local market cut Mango into perfect golden cubes and arranged him on a plate with shaved ice and condensed milk.

"**芒果冰**!" the vendor announced. "Fresh Pingtung mango! Peak season!"

A family bought the plate. The youngest daughter — maybe five years old — ate the first cube. Her eyes went enormous. Golden juice ran down her chin.

"**歐伊西**!" (ōuyīxī) she screamed — "YUMMY!" — and the sound was so pure and so happy that if you listened carefully, somewhere in that sweetness, you could hear Mango laughing.

Three weeks of season. Twenty-one days of sunshine. And every single one of them had been absolutely, perfectly, gloriously delicious.
