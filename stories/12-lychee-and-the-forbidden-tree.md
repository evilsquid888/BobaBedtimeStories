# Story 12: Lychee and the Forbidden Tree

**Main Character:** Lìzhī (荔枝 lìzhī) — Lychee
**Guest Characters:** None
**Setting:** Nantou orchards, central Taiwan
**Theme:** Don't judge by appearances
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 荔枝 (lìzhī) = lychee

---

Lìzhī was, by her own estimation, the most fabulous fruit in all of **台灣** (Táiwān) = Taiwan. And frankly, darling, she was right.

"Darling, DARLING," she announced, fanning herself with a leaf she'd plucked from a branch above, "do you SEE this skin? Bumpy. Rough. Positively prehistoric. People take one look at me and think I'm some kind of dinosaur egg. But PEEL back the layers, darling—" she paused dramatically "—and you'll find the most exquisite, translucent, pearl-white flesh you have ever laid eyes on."

She demonstrated this by peeling back a tiny corner of her reddish-pink shell, revealing the glowing white fruit underneath. A shaft of afternoon sunlight caught it, and it sparkled like a jewel.

"Perfection," Lìzhī declared. "I am literally perfection in a bumpy jacket."

**荔枝** (lìzhī) = lychee were one of Taiwan's most treasured fruits. They grew in the warm, humid orchards of central and southern Taiwan, hanging in clusters from ancient trees. The fruit was seasonal — just a few weeks in early summer — and during those weeks, the entire island went lychee-crazy. Roadside stands piled high with lychees. Markets bursting with the sweet, floral fragrance. Grandmothers peeling them for grandchildren with the speed and precision of factory machines.

Lìzhī lived in the finest lychee orchard in **南投** (Nántóu) = Nantou county, in the green mountains of central Taiwan. Her tree was a hundred years old, gnarled and majestic, and it produced lychees so sweet that poets had written about them. (Admittedly, one of the poets was Lìzhī herself. She had composed an ode to her own deliciousness. It was seventeen stanzas long.)

But there was one tree in the orchard that nobody touched.

It stood at the very back, behind a rusty fence, its branches wild and untrimmed, its lychees dark and small and strange-looking. The orchard owner, Mr. Huáng, called it the "old tree" and shook his head whenever anyone asked about it.

"Forbidden," he said. "Bad fruit. Ugly. Not for selling."

Lìzhī had always taken Mr. Huáng at his word. Why would she question him? The old tree's lychees WERE ugly — small, dark, bumpy in all the wrong ways. Their shells were almost brown instead of the proper rosy-pink. They hung in lopsided clusters like bunches of tiny, sad fists.

"Poor things," Lìzhī said, looking at the old tree from a safe distance. "Not everyone can be gorgeous. It's genetics, darling. Nothing to be ashamed of."

She went back to admiring her own reflection in a dewdrop.

Everything changed when a girl named Xiǎo-Méi came to the orchard.

Xiǎo-Méi was twelve, she was visiting her grandfather Mr. Huáng for the summer, and she had the kind of curiosity that made adults nervous. She asked questions — about everything, constantly, with the relentless energy of someone who had not yet been taught that some questions are "rude."

"Grandpa, why is that tree behind a fence?" she asked on her first day.

"Because it's bad. Bad fruit."

"What makes it bad?"

"It's ugly."

"Ugly HOW?"

"Small. Dark. Wrong color. Nobody wants to buy ugly lychees."

"Have you ever tasted them?"

Mr. Huáng paused. "No. No need. You can TELL they're bad by looking."

Xiǎo-Méi stared at him with the expression children use when adults say something that doesn't make sense, which is more often than adults would like to admit.

"That's like saying you can tell a book is bad by its cover," she said.

"Books don't have bumpy shells."

"Grandpa."

"Fine! Fine. But I'm telling you, those lychees are no good."

Xiǎo-Méi was not convinced. She spent the next three days orbiting the fence like a satellite around a forbidden planet. She studied the old tree from every angle. She noticed things: the tree's roots were deeper than any other tree in the orchard. Its branches, though wild, were strong. And the lychees — small and dark though they were — clung to the branches with a tenacity that suggested they had something to prove.

On the fourth day, she climbed the fence.

"**小心**!" (xiǎoxīn) Lìzhī called from her tree — "be careful!" — not because she was worried about the girl, but because climbing fences was undignified and dignity was important.

Xiǎo-Méi ignored the fancy lychee and walked through the tall grass to the old tree. Up close, the tree was even more impressive — and more strange. Its bark was darker than the other trees, almost black, and carved with markings that might have been natural and might have been made by human hands a very long time ago.

The lychees hung within arm's reach. Xiǎo-Méi picked one.

It was warm from the sun. Smaller than a regular lychee — barely bigger than a large marble. The shell was dark reddish-brown, rough, and not at all pretty.

She peeled it.

And that's when Lìzhī fell off her branch.

Not literally — lychees don't fall off branches — but emotionally, because what Xiǎo-Méi revealed under that ugly shell was the most extraordinary thing Lìzhī had ever seen.

The flesh inside wasn't white.

It was PINK.

A deep, blushing, rosy pink, translucent as stained glass, with a fragrance that hit the air like a symphony made of flowers and honey and summer rain. It was, without exaggeration, the most beautiful fruit flesh in the entire orchard.

Xiǎo-Méi bit into it.

Her eyes went so wide they almost left her face.

"**歐伊西**!" (ōuyīxī) she gasped — "YUMMY!" — but the word felt pathetically inadequate. This wasn't yummy. This was TRANSCENDENT. The flavor was unlike any lychee she'd ever tasted — sweeter, deeper, with floral notes of rose and jasmine and something she couldn't name but that tasted like the color of sunset.

"GRANDPA!" she screamed. "GRANDPA, COME HERE!"

Mr. Huáng came running, convinced his granddaughter was being attacked by a snake or a bee or possibly a bear (there are no bears in Nantou lychee orchards, but grandfathers don't think rationally when grandchildren scream).

"TASTE THIS!" Xiǎo-Méi shoved the peeled lychee at him.

Mr. Huáng, still catching his breath, took a bite.

He chewed.

He stopped chewing.

He stared at the lychee. He stared at the tree. He stared at his granddaughter.

"**好吃**" (hǎochī) he whispered — "delicious" — and his voice sounded the way voices sound when someone realizes they've been wrong about something for a very long time.

"What IS this tree?" Xiǎo-Méi demanded.

Mr. Huáng sat down on the grass. He looked at the old tree with new eyes — the deep roots, the strong branches, the small dark fruit that hid pink jewels inside.

"My grandfather planted this tree," he said slowly. "It's a very old variety. A heritage lychee. They stopped growing them commercially because..." He swallowed. "Because they're too small. Too dark. They don't LOOK like what customers expect. So everyone switched to the big, pretty varieties. This tree is the last one."

"The last one in the WORLD?"

"Maybe. Probably. I don't know. I never thought to check. I just... I looked at the fruit and saw something ugly, and I assumed..."

"You assumed wrong."

"I assumed wrong."

Back on her branch in the pretty part of the orchard, Lìzhī was having what could only be described as an existential crisis.

She — the most fabulous, the most gorgeous, the most perfectly pink-shelled lychee in the orchard — had been out-dazzled by the ugly tree. The FORBIDDEN tree. The tree she had pitied.

"Darling," she said to herself, and for once the word wasn't performative. "DARLING. Have I been... wrong?"

She had spent her entire life believing that beauty was the outside. The shell. The presentation. The rosy-pink exterior. And here was a fruit with the worst shell in the orchard and the most magnificent inside.

"Peel back the layers," Lìzhī murmured — her own catchphrase, turned back on herself like a mirror. "PEEL back the layers."

That evening, Xiǎo-Méi brought both types of lychee to the farmhouse kitchen. She peeled them side by side — Lìzhī's picture-perfect pink shell revealing standard white flesh, and the old tree's ugly brown shell revealing that extraordinary pink interior.

"Which one is more beautiful?" Xiǎo-Méi asked her grandfather.

"The one you have to look twice at," he said.

Mr. Huáng spent the rest of that summer caring for the old tree. He cut back the grass, pruned the wild branches, and repaired the rusty fence — not to keep people out, but to mark the tree as special. He sent fruit samples to the agricultural college in Taichung. The professor who tasted them nearly wept.

"This is a **玉荷包** (yùhébāo)," the professor said. "A heritage variety we thought was extinct. It's the original Taiwanese lychee. The one all the modern varieties descended from. And it's been growing in your orchard this whole time?"

"Behind a fence," said Mr. Huáng quietly. "Because I thought it was ugly."

Lìzhī listened to all of this from her branch. And slowly — with the dramatic reluctance that is the birthright of every socialite — she did something she had never done before.

She climbed down from her beautiful tree, crossed the orchard, squeezed through the fence, and positioned herself on a branch of the old tree.

"Darling," she said to the small, dark lychees around her. "I owe you an apology."

The old lychees said nothing. They didn't need to. They had been extraordinary all along. They were simply waiting for someone to notice.

"I judged you by your shell," said Lìzhī. "And I, of all fruit, should have known better. My ENTIRE catchphrase is 'peel back the layers.' And I never peeled back YOURS."

A small, dark lychee nudged her gently. It smelled like roses.

"**不好意思**" (bù hǎo yìsi) Lìzhī said — "I'm sorry."

And she meant it. For the first time in her fabulous, dramatic, shell-obsessed life, she truly, deeply meant it.

When the harvest came, Xiǎo-Méi picked lychees from both trees. The pretty ones went in one basket. The heritage ones went in another. And when customers came to the roadside stand, she offered both.

"Which one should I buy?" they asked.

"Both," said Xiǎo-Méi. "One teaches you about beauty on the outside. The other teaches you about beauty on the inside. And together, darling—" she grinned, and nobody knew where she'd picked up that word "—together, they teach you about beauty, period."

The customers peeled. They tasted. They understood.

And Lìzhī, sitting proudly in the "premium heritage" basket beside her new friends from the old tree, had never felt more beautiful.

Because true beauty, darling, isn't the shell.

It's everything underneath.

## Goodnight Blessing

Tonight, darling dreamer, may you have the courage to peel back the layers — of the world, of others, and of yourself. May you never judge a fruit by its shell or a person by their outside. The most extraordinary things are often hidden in the plainest packages. Close your eyes and know that whatever you look like on the outside, you are pink and perfect and precious on the inside. Goodnight, beautiful one. Goodnight.
