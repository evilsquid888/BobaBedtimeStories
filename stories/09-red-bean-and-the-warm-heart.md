# Story 09: Red Bean and the Warm Heart

**Main Character:** Hóng Dòu (紅豆 hóngdòu) — Red Bean
**Guest Characters:** None
**Setting:** Traditional douhua shop
**Theme:** Nurturing others
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 紅豆 (hóngdòu) = red bean

---

If you ever needed proof that the smallest things can hold the biggest hearts, you needed only to visit Grandma Liú's Douhua Shop on Yǒngkāng Street in Taipei and meet Hóng Dòu.

Hóng Dòu was a **紅豆** (hóngdòu) = red bean. A single, plump, deep burgundy bean, no bigger than your pinky fingernail, with a tiny white stripe down one side and a warm, grandmotherly expression that somehow communicated "have you eaten yet?" without saying a word.

She lived in the red bean pot — the giant iron cauldron that sat on the back burner of Grandma Liú's kitchen, bubbling gently from dawn to midnight, filling the tiny shop with the sweet, earthy aroma of slow-cooked red bean soup. The pot was Hóng Dòu's home, her office, and her whole world.

And every single day, she cooked for everyone else.

"Everyone needs a little sweetness," she said, stirring the pot with a ladle three times her size. "That's what red bean is for. We're not flashy like mango. We're not dramatic like shaved ice. We're the warm thing at the bottom of the bowl that makes you close your eyes and think of home."

Grandma Liú's shop was legendary. It had been on Yǒngkāng Street for forty-seven years, serving **豆花** (dòuhuā) = tofu pudding with toppings: red bean, peanuts, taro balls, and sweet ginger syrup. The shop was tiny — just six tables and a counter — but the line outside stretched down the block every afternoon. Tourists, office workers, grandmothers, students, taxi drivers — they all came for Grandma Liú's douhua.

And they all came, whether they knew it or not, for Hóng Dòu's red beans.

Because Hóng Dòu didn't just cook. She LOVED. Every bean in that pot was simmered with care — low heat, slow time, a pinch of sugar at exactly the right moment. The beans came out tender, sweet, with skins so soft they practically dissolved on your tongue. Food critics called them "transcendent." Regular customers just called them "the reason I get out of bed."

Hóng Dòu called them "my babies."

"**吃飽了嗎**?" (chī bǎo le ma) she asked every pot of beans as they cooked — "have you eaten yet?" — which was funny because THEY were the food, but Hóng Dòu didn't see the irony. In her world, caring for others was simply what you did. Even if the others were also beans.

The trouble started on a Thursday.

Grandma Liú came into the shop that morning looking different. Tired. She moved slowly — slower than usual — and her hands shook when she lifted the pot lids. She didn't sing while she cooked, which was unprecedented. Grandma Liú ALWAYS sang while she cooked. Old Taiwanese songs about mountains and rivers, mostly off-key, always loud.

Today: silence.

Hóng Dòu noticed immediately. "Something's wrong," she whispered to the peanuts.

"Maybe she didn't sleep well," said the peanuts, who were not known for their emotional intelligence.

"It's more than that. Look at her eyes."

Grandma Liú's eyes, usually bright as river stones, were dull. She served the morning customers on autopilot — scoop, pour, smile, repeat — but the smile didn't reach her eyes.

At noon, when the shop was briefly empty between the lunch and afternoon crowds, Grandma Liú sat down at one of her own tables — something she never did — and pulled a letter from her apron pocket. She read it slowly. Then she folded it. Then she put her head in her hands.

Hóng Dòu climbed out of the pot (carefully — the rim was hot) and made her way across the counter, down a dish towel hanging over the edge, and onto the table. She was dripping red bean soup and leaving tiny burgundy footprints on the white tablecloth, but she didn't care.

She sat down in front of the letter.

It was from a hospital. Something about test results and follow-up appointments and words that were long and medical and scary. Hóng Dòu couldn't read all of it, but she understood enough.

Grandma Liú was sick.

"Oh," said Hóng Dòu. Just "oh." Because sometimes, when the person who takes care of everyone else is the one who needs care, there are no other words.

Grandma Liú looked up. She saw the tiny red bean on her table, dripping soup, and — impossibly, wonderfully — she laughed.

"**你怎麼跑出來了**?" (nǐ zěnme pǎo chūlái le) she said — "how did you get out here?" She picked up Hóng Dòu gently between two fingers and held her up to eye level. "Are you worried about me, little bean?"

Hóng Dòu nodded.

"Don't be. I've been making douhua for forty-seven years. A little sickness isn't going to stop me. I just..." She trailed off. "I just need to rest. But if I rest, who will cook? The beans won't simmer themselves."

"Actually," said Hóng Dòu, "they will. Because I'll be in the pot."

Grandma Liú blinked.

"Go home," said Hóng Dòu. "Rest. Sleep. I'll take care of the shop."

"A bean can't run a shop."

"A bean who has watched you cook for forty-seven years knows every recipe by heart. Low heat. Slow time. A pinch of sugar at exactly the right moment. I've got this, Grandma."

There was a long moment. The afternoon light came through the shop window and made everything golden. Grandma Liú looked at the tiny bean in her hand, and something passed between them — something warm and old and sweet, like the steam from a pot of red bean soup.

"**謝謝**" (xièxie) Grandma Liú whispered — "thank you" — and she set Hóng Dòu gently on the counter, took off her apron, and walked home.

What happened next was the most extraordinary afternoon in the history of Yǒngkāng Street.

Hóng Dòu took charge.

She couldn't reach the stove — she was a bean — so she rallied the other ingredients. The taro balls, who were round enough to roll along the counter, turned the gas knobs. The peanuts, working in teams of twelve, stirred the pots. The grass jelly (a visiting Caǎo, who happened to be passing through Taipei) kept everything cool and calm with her herbal aura.

"Low heat!" Hóng Dòu called from her command post atop the sugar jar. "Slow time! Don't rush the beans!"

"**加油**!" (jiāyóu) the peanuts chanted — "go for it!"

The afternoon crowd arrived to find the most unusual scene: a douhua shop apparently running itself, with no human behind the counter, just the smell of perfectly cooked red bean soup and the sound of tiny voices coordinating from inside the kitchen.

The customers didn't question it. This was Taipei. Weirder things happened on a daily basis.

The first customer was a businessman who had just been fired. He was still wearing his tie, which was crooked, and his eyes were the blank eyes of someone who had been called into an office at 4 PM on a Thursday, which is never good.

Hóng Dòu made his douhua herself. She supervised every step: the silky tofu pudding, the warm ginger syrup, and a generous helping of red beans — her own family, her own kin, simmered with love and placed on top like a crown.

The businessman took one bite.

His eyes closed. His shoulders dropped. The tension in his jaw — the jaw that had been clenched since 4 PM — finally relaxed.

"This tastes like my grandmother's cooking," he whispered.

"Everyone needs a little sweetness," Hóng Dòu murmured from behind the pot.

The next customer was a college student who had failed an exam. Then a mother with a crying baby. Then an old man eating alone who just wanted company. Then a tourist from Japan who was homesick. Then two kids who had been fighting and whose mother had dragged them in for "attitude adjustment via dessert."

Hóng Dòu made every bowl with the same care, the same love, the same patient sweetness. Low heat. Slow time. A pinch of sugar at exactly the right moment.

By closing time, every customer had left the shop looking a little different than when they'd entered. Softer. Warmer. Like they'd been hugged from the inside.

The businessman had straightened his tie and was already drafting a new resume. The student had texted her professor asking for extra credit. The mother's baby had fallen asleep. The old man was smiling. The tourist had called home. The two kids had stopped fighting and were sharing a bowl.

Grandma Liú came back the next morning. She looked rested — actually rested, for the first time in months. She walked into the shop and found it clean, organized, and ready for the day, with a pot of red beans already simmering on the back burner.

She peered into the pot. Hóng Dòu was floating on top, tiny ladle in hand, exhausted but triumphant.

"How was it?" Grandma Liú asked.

"Everyone got fed," said Hóng Dòu. "That's what matters."

Grandma Liú smiled — a real smile, the kind that reached her eyes and her heart and the deep, warm place where love lives.

She scooped Hóng Dòu out of the pot and placed her on the counter, next to the morning's first bowl of douhua. Then she added the red beans — gently, lovingly, one spoonful at a time.

"You know," said Grandma Liú, "people always ask me what makes my red beans special. I tell them it's the recipe. The low heat. The slow time. But it's not really."

"What is it?"

"It's the bean that stays up all night making sure everyone else gets fed before she feeds herself."

Hóng Dòu blushed — which, for a bean that was already deep burgundy, meant she turned approximately the color of a ruby.

"**吃飽了嗎**?" (chī bǎo le ma) Grandma Liú asked her — "have you eaten yet?"

"No," Hóng Dòu admitted. "I was so busy cooking I forgot."

Grandma Liú laughed, made a tiny bowl of red bean soup, and set it in front of the world's smallest, warmest, most nurturing bean.

"Eat," she said. "Let someone take care of YOU for once."

And Hóng Dòu — the bean who fed everyone, loved everyone, and never, ever forgot to ask if you were hungry — finally sat down and let herself be fed.

It was, she had to admit, **好吃** (hǎochī).

Delicious.

## Goodnight Blessing

Tonight, caring one, may you rest the way Hóng Dòu finally rested — deeply, warmly, knowing that you've done enough. You don't have to feed the whole world before you feed yourself. Close your eyes and let the sweet, warm soup of sleep simmer around you. You are loved. You are enough. And tomorrow, there will be a warm bowl waiting for you. Goodnight, little red bean. Goodnight.
