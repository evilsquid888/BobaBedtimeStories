# Story 25: Tāng Yuán and the Demon King's Midnight Snack

**Main Character:** Tāng Yuán (湯圓 tāngyuán) — Tangyuan
**Guest Characters:** None
**Setting:** Lukang Old Town, Changhua County
**Theme:** Everyone deserves warmth
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 冬至 (dōngzhì) = Winter Solstice

---

The coldest night of the year had arrived in **鹿港** (Lùgǎng) = Lukang, and Tāng Yuán was having the time of his life.

He bounced down the ancient cobblestone streets like a ping-pong ball on a trampoline — a perfectly round, glistening white glutinous rice ball with rosy cheeks, a heart full of sweet black sesame, and absolutely zero regard for the freezing temperature. Why would he care about cold? He was Tāng Yuán. He came with his own invisible warm broth. Everywhere he bobbed, a faint steam rose around him like a tiny personal hot spring.

"WOO!" he hollered, ricocheting off a red brick wall and bouncing past a shuttered incense shop. "What a BEAUTIFUL night to be round!"

A stray cat on a temple doorstep stared at him, deeply unimpressed.

"We're ALL in this soup together, friend!" Tāng Yuán called to the cat. The cat turned around and showed him its butt, which, if you know cats, is actually a sign of trust. Tāng Yuán chose to take it as a compliment.

Lukang was one of the most ancient towns in all of **台灣** (Táiwān) = Taiwan — a maze of narrow alleys, crumbling brick temples, and old shophouses that had been standing since your great-great-great-great-grandparents were in diapers. On the night of **冬至** (dōngzhì) = Winter Solstice, it was downright spooky. Fog crept between the buildings like it was looking for somewhere warm to sit. Red lanterns swung in the bitter wind, casting wobbly shadows that looked like dancing ghosts.

Most sensible food items were indoors tonight.

Tāng Yuán was not a sensible food item.

He was mid-bounce past the oldest temple on the street — a hulking stone structure whose dedication plaque had worn blank three centuries ago — when the ground shook.

Not a little shake. A the-entire-earth-just-hiccupped shake. The cobblestones rattled. Roof tiles clattered. The stray cat launched itself six feet straight up and vanished over a wall.

Tāng Yuán stopped bouncing.

The ground shook AGAIN. Then AGAIN. A crack split the stone courtyard of the old temple, zigzagging between Tāng Yuán's feet like dark lightning. And from that crack, something began to rise.

First came the horns — two enormous, curling rams' horns, each one the size of a motorcycle. Then came a head the size of a minivan, covered in dark green scales that glinted in the lantern light. Then shoulders. Then arms. Then a torso so massive it blocked out the moon.

The creature rose thirty feet into the cold night air, towering over the temple rooftops, yellow eyes glowing like headlights. It opened its mouth — a cavern of fangs — and let out a —

Actually, no. It let out a sneeze.

"AAAA-CHOOOO!"

The sneeze hit Tāng Yuán like a warm, slightly gross hurricane. He tumbled backward, rolled twice, and bumped gently into a mailbox.

The creature — and you know what, let's just say it. This was a **魔王** (Mówáng) = Demon King. The real deal. Thirty feet of scales, horns, claws, and extremely bad posture from sleeping underground for a thousand years.

The Demon King tried again. He drew in a massive breath, squared his enormous shoulders, opened his terrifying jaws, and —

"AAAA-CHOOOOOO!"

A long, gooey strand of demon snot dangled from one nostril. He looked absolutely miserable.

Tāng Yuán picked himself up, dusted off his beautiful round surface, and looked up. And up. And up.

"Bless you, big guy!" he said cheerfully.

The Demon King blinked down at the tiny white ball. He had been expecting screaming. Running. Maybe pitchforks. Not... bless you.

He attempted a menacing growl. It came out as a yawn.

"Rrrrrrr — aaaahhhhhhh." His yellow eyes watered. His scales clinked together like chattering teeth.

"Oh, you poor enormous thing," said Tāng Yuán, bobbing closer without a shred of fear. "You're FREEZING. We're all in this soup together, and you look like you need a warm bowl of something."

The Demon King looked left. Looked right. Then, in a voice so tiny and embarrassed that it was genuinely hilarious coming from a being the size of a three-story building, he said:

"I... I want tangyuan."

Tāng Yuán's rosy cheeks flushed even rosier. "Come again?"

The Demon King shuffled his building-sized feet. Scales clinked. "湯圓," he whispered. "The warm ones. In sweet soup. With ginger. I — I had them once. A thousand years ago. On dōngzhì. A family left a bowl outside the temple for the spirits, and I... I snuck up and..." He trailed off, and his yellow eyes went soft and faraway. "It was the last warm thing I remember before I went to sleep."

He sniffed. His lower lip — which was the size of a canoe — actually trembled.

"A THOUSAND YEARS without tangyuan?" Tāng Yuán was horrified. Truly, genuinely horrified. This was the worst thing he had ever heard, and he had once heard a tourist say tangyuan was "basically just wet dough."

"That's a tragedy, family. An actual tragedy. But don't you worry." He bounced up and down with fresh determination. "We are going to FIX this. Tonight. Right now. Nobody goes cold on dōngzhì — that's the RULE."

The Demon King's eyes widened. "You... you would help me? But I'm a Mówáng. I'm supposed to be scary."

Tāng Yuán looked at the strand of snot still dangling from the demon's nostril, the shivering scales, the watery eyes.

"Terrifying," he said, very seriously. "Absolutely bone-chilling. Now sit down before you fall down — we've got soup to make."

And with that, Tāng Yuán bounced off into the foggy alleys of Lukang, hollering at the top of his tiny round lungs.

"CALLING ALL INGREDIENTS! We've got an emergency! Repeat — SOUP EMERGENCY!"

One by one, they appeared. First came Old Ginger, a knobbly, no-nonsense root who had been sleeping in a basket outside a noodle shop. "What's the ruckus?" she snapped, squinting through the fog. Then she saw the thirty-foot demon sitting in the temple courtyard, hugging his own knees and shivering, and her tough exterior melted like — well, like ginger in hot water.

"Oh, for heaven's sake," she muttered, already rolling toward the nearest kitchen. "I'll get the pot."

Next came Brown Sugar, a sticky, sweet lump of caramelized goodness with a voice like warm honey. "Say no more, Yuán. Say no more. I was BORN for moments like this."

And finally, Little Osmanthus — tiny golden flowers of **桂花** (guìhuā) = sweet osmanthus, fragrant and delicate, who sprinkled herself into everything with the quiet confidence of someone who knows they make everything better. Because she did.

They found the biggest pot in Lukang — an ancient iron cauldron in the temple kitchen that hadn't been used since the Qing Dynasty. Old Ginger threw herself in first, releasing her sharp, warming bite into the water. Brown Sugar dissolved next, turning the broth into liquid gold. Little Osmanthus drifted in last, and suddenly the courtyard smelled like a warm hug from someone who really loved you.

The Demon King leaned over the pot and inhaled. His yellow eyes fluttered shut. "That smell," he breathed. "That's the smell."

"Almost ready!" Tāng Yuán announced, bouncing around the rim of the cauldron like a coach at halftime. "But we need tangyuan! A proper dōngzhì bowl isn't complete without —"

He stopped. He looked at the cauldron. He looked at the Demon King's enormous bowl — a stone basin the size of a kiddie pool that they'd borrowed from the temple garden. He looked at himself.

There was only one tangyuan in Lukang tonight.

And you know what? He didn't hesitate. Not even for a second.

"We were MADE for this!" Tāng Yuán declared, bouncing to the rim of the steaming bowl. "Every tangyuan dreams of being in the perfect bowl of soup. That's not sacrifice — that's DESTINY, family! This is our MOMENT!"

Old Ginger wiped a tear. Brown Sugar sniffled (stickily). Little Osmanthus simply nodded, because she understood.

Tāng Yuán backed up three steps. Took a running start. And executed the most spectacular cannonball in the entire history of Taiwanese cuisine.

"SOUP'S ON!"

**SPLASH.**

He hit the sweet ginger broth and sank into warmth so perfect, so complete, that he understood at last what it meant to be exactly where you were supposed to be. The broth hugged him. The osmanthus scented the steam. He was home.

The Demon King lifted the enormous basin in his clawed hands. He looked at the single round tangyuan floating in the golden broth. Brought the bowl to his lips. Took a sip.

And stopped.

A single tear — enormous, hot, and surprisingly sparkly — rolled down his scaly green cheek and splashed into the bowl like a raindrop hitting a puddle. Then another. Then another.

"**好吃**," (hǎochī) he whispered. Delicious. "It tastes... it tastes like being warm."

"Told you!" Tāng Yuán called up from inside the bowl, grinning ear to ear — or, since he didn't have ears, cheek to rosy cheek. "We're all in this soup together now, Mówáng!"

And that's when the Demon King did something nobody expected.

He set the bowl down, stood to his full thirty-foot height, and bellowed into the cold Lukang night — not a roar, but a call. A deep, rumbling invitation that rattled every window and shook every lantern on the street.

"**來來來**!" (lái lái lái) he boomed. Come come come!

From behind the temples, out of the cracks in the old walls, under the stone bridges and inside the abandoned wells — tiny demons appeared. Dozens of them. Hundreds. Little demon children with stubby horns and patchy scales and enormous eyes, no bigger than house cats, all of them shivering, all of them hungry, all of them scared.

They had been hiding from the Demon King. They'd thought he was terrifying.

(To be fair, the snot thing hadn't helped his image.)

"Come," said the Demon King, in the gentlest voice a thirty-foot demon has ever used. "There's soup."

Old Ginger threw more ginger into the cauldron. Brown Sugar dissolved another lump. Little Osmanthus worked overtime. The cauldron bubbled and grew, because that's what soup does when it's made with love — it stretches. There's always enough.

The tiny demons crept forward, then scurried, then tumbled over each other in their rush to reach the warmth. The Demon King ladled out bowl after bowl after bowl, his enormous clawed hands surprisingly gentle with the little stone cups.

And then — because warmth is like that, because it doesn't stop at the edges of the bowl — the doors of Lukang began to open. One by one, then all at once. Humans peeked out. Grandmothers in quilted jackets. Kids in pajamas. A noodle shop owner carrying a second pot. A temple keeper with extra bowls.

Nobody screamed. Nobody ran. It was dōngzhì, the longest night, and everyone — demon, human, dessert ingredient — was cold. And there was warm soup.

**不好意思** (bù hǎo yìsi) = excuse me, the noodle shop owner said politely, squeezing past the Demon King's ankle to set up a folding table. The Demon King moved his tail and said **謝謝** (xièxie) = thank you, which might have been the first time in history that a Mówáng used manners.

The cold Lukang night became the warmest Lukang night. Someone started playing an old song on an **二胡** (èrhú) = two-stringed fiddle. The tiny demons fell asleep in warm laps. The Demon King sat against the temple wall, a dozen little demons curled on his belly, a bowl of soup in his hands, and a look on his face that could only be described as home.

And from inside the soup — warm, sweet, exactly where he was meant to be — Tāng Yuán smiled his biggest smile.

"See?" he said, to no one and everyone. "We're ALL in this soup together. Always have been. Always will be."

He closed his eyes.

The broth was perfect.

The night was long.

And nobody — not a single soul in Lukang, not demon, not human, not one little round rice ball — was cold.

## Goodnight Blessing

Close your eyes now, little one. On the coldest nights, warmth doesn't come from the weather — it comes from the people who share their soup with you.
