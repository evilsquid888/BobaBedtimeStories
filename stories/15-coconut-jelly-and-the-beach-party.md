# Story 15: Coconut Jelly and the Beach Party

**Main Character:** Yēguǒ (椰果 yēguǒ) — Coconut Jelly
**Guest Characters:** None
**Setting:** Kenting Beach, southern Taiwan
**Theme:** Going with the flow
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 椰果 (yēguǒ) = coconut jelly

---

Yēguǒ was lying on a surfboard, floating on the South China Sea, with a shell necklace around his neck and absolutely zero plans for the rest of his life.

"Go with the flow, bro," he said to a passing seagull. The seagull didn't respond. Seagulls never do. But Yēguǒ didn't take it personally. He didn't take anything personally. That was kind of his whole thing.

**椰果** (yēguǒ) = coconut jelly — those translucent, chewy, slightly bouncy white cubes you find at the bottom of your bubble tea or floating in tropical dessert drinks. Made from coconut water and bacterial cellulose (which sounds gross, but trust me, it's delicious), coconut jelly was the chillest ingredient in the entire Taiwanese dessert universe.

And Yēguǒ was the chillest coconut jelly in all of **台灣** (Táiwān) = Taiwan.

He lived at **墾丁** (Kěndīng) = Kenting Beach — the tropical paradise at the very southern tip of Taiwan, where the water was turquoise, the sand was white, the palm trees swayed like they were listening to music only they could hear, and the whole vibe was "why would you ever leave?"

Kenting was Taiwan's beach vacation spot. Surfers rode waves. Divers explored coral reefs. Night market vendors sold grilled squid and mango smoothies. And everyone — EVERYONE — walked around in flip-flops and the slightly dazed expression of humans who have been touched by tropical sunshine and cannot believe this is the same island where it rains for three months straight in Taipei.

Yēguǒ had been at Kenting since he could remember. He surfed (mostly just floating on a surfboard and letting the waves push him wherever). He snorkeled (mostly just drifting above the coral and waving at fish). He did beach yoga (mostly just lying on the sand in a pose that could generously be called "reclining whatever").

Life was good. Life was flow.

The problem — if you could call it a problem, and Yēguǒ definitely wouldn't — was that a Beach Party was happening. Not just any beach party. THE beach party. The Annual Kenting Summer Beach Bash, the biggest party in southern Taiwan, with bands, DJs, fireworks, dance contests, and approximately seventeen thousand people crammed onto one beach.

And Yēguǒ's friends wanted him to help organize it.

"Dude," said his friend, a mango smoothie cup named Shaky, "we need you to be in charge of the music schedule."

"Bro," said Yēguǒ. "That sounds like... planning."

"Yes."

"And planning sounds like... not flowing."

"Yēguǒ. The party is in TWO DAYS. We have no music schedule, no stage setup, no sound check, and the DJ just texted saying he's stuck in Taichung because his scooter broke down."

"Bro, that's heavy."

"HELP US."

Yēguǒ considered this from his surfboard. A wave gently rocked him. A cloud shaped like a coconut drifted overhead. In the distance, a group of tourists were attempting to ride banana boats and failing spectacularly.

"Here's the thing, Shaky," said Yēguǒ. "Every time humans try to control a party, it gets stressful. Schedules, logistics, timelines — bro, that's not a party. That's a PROJECT. What if we just... let the party happen?"

"That's not how parties work."

"That's not how PROJECTS work. Parties are different. Parties are like waves. You don't schedule a wave. You don't plan a wave. The wave comes when the wave comes, and you ride it."

"The fireworks are scheduled for 9 PM."

"Then the fireworks are a scheduled wave. Go with it."

Shaky stared at Yēguǒ with the weary expression of someone who has asked a coconut jelly for help and received philosophy instead. "I'm going to ask the pineapple cakes. They're organized."

"**加油**!" (jiāyóu) Yēguǒ called after him — "go for it, bro!"

Two days passed. The beach party arrived. And it was, by all accounts, a disaster.

The stage was set up facing the wrong way. The speakers pointed at the ocean instead of the audience, which meant the fish got an excellent concert and the humans got ambient wave sounds. The food vendors were positioned upwind, so the smell of grilled squid overwhelmed the DJ's setup and he kept stopping to ask, "Does anyone else smell squid?" The dance floor was on sand, which is terrible for dancing because sand eats shoes and trips drunk uncles.

By 7 PM, the organizers were panicking. By 7:30 PM, they were arguing. By 8 PM, the entire committee was sitting in a circle on the beach, heads in hands, contemplating their failures.

"This is a catastrophe," moaned Shaky.

"The speakers are backwards," groaned the stage manager, a rigid rectangle of nougat candy.

"The dance floor is SAND."

"The DJ is eating squid."

Into this circle of despair drifted Yēguǒ, translucent and serene, wearing his shell necklace and carrying a coconut drink with a little umbrella in it.

"Hey, bros," he said pleasantly. "How's the party?"

"IT'S RUINED."

"Huh." Yēguǒ looked around. He looked at the backwards speakers, the squid-scented DJ booth, the sandy dance floor. He took a long sip of his coconut drink.

"Looks fine to me," he said.

"FINE?! The speakers are facing the OCEAN!"

"So turn the audience around. Face everyone toward the ocean. Sunset's in forty-five minutes. You've got a natural light show, a sound system pointed at the best backdrop in Taiwan, and a crowd that gets to watch the sky turn purple while the music plays. Bro. That's not a bug. That's a feature."

Shaky blinked. "That... actually..."

"And the squid smell? Put the food vendors IN the crowd instead of behind it. People don't go to a party to stand in a food line. They go to party. Bring the food TO them. Walking vendors. Street food meets beach party. It's immersive dining, dude."

"And the sand dance floor?"

"Bro. It's a BEACH. Take off your shoes. Dance barefoot. Sand between your toes, music in your ears, sunset in your eyes. That's not a problem. That's the dream."

The organizers looked at each other. Then they looked at Yēguǒ, who was sipping his coconut drink with the peaceful expression of someone who has never once in his life Googled "how to plan an event."

"You know what," said Shaky slowly, "he might be right."

They flipped the plan. Turned the audience around. Sent food vendors into the crowd. Took off everyone's shoes. And at 8:30 PM, as the sun touched the horizon and the DJ stopped worrying about squid and started spinning actual music, the Annual Kenting Summer Beach Bash came alive.

It was MAGNIFICENT.

The sunset was impossible — bands of orange, pink, and purple stretching across the sky like God had knocked over a paint set. The speakers, facing the ocean, mixed music with the sound of waves, creating a rhythm so natural it felt like the earth itself was the DJ. People danced barefoot in the sand, laughing as their feet sank and slid. Food vendors weaved through the crowd with skewers of **烤玉米** (kǎo yùmǐ) = grilled corn, cups of mango juice, and bags of peanut candy.

"**來來來**!" (lái lái lái) the vendors called — "come come come!" — and the party swirled around them like a warm, joyful, slightly sandy ocean current.

At 9 PM, the fireworks launched. They exploded over the South China Sea, their reflections shimmering on the dark water. The crowd gasped. The music swelled. And somewhere in the middle of it all, Yēguǒ was doing exactly what he always did.

Floating.

Not on a surfboard this time. In a cup. A large, icy, coconut-milk-and-jelly drink being held by a teenage girl who was dancing barefoot in the sand with her friends, her face lit by fireworks, her feet warm in the sand, her taste buds singing with coconut sweetness.

She took a sip. One of Yēguǒ's chewy cubes traveled up the fat straw and into her mouth. She chewed. The texture was bouncy, tropical, perfect.

"**歐伊西**!" (ōuyīxī) she said — "yummy!" — and kept dancing, the coconut drink in one hand and a sparkler in the other, the ocean at her back and the sky full of light.

Yēguǒ floated in the cup, surrounded by ice and coconut milk, listening to the muffled boom of fireworks and the distant bass of the DJ. He was comfortable. He was content. He was exactly where the flow had taken him.

"Go with the flow, bro," he murmured one last time, and let the sweet current carry him.

Because here's what Yēguǒ knew that the planners and the organizers and the stress-monsters didn't: the best moments in life are never the ones you schedule. They're the ones that happen BETWEEN the schedule. The barefoot dancing. The accidental sunset. The squid-scented DJ who accidentally drops the best beat of the night because he's grooving on grilled seafood.

You can't plan magic.

You can only show up, take off your shoes, and let the wave carry you.
