# Story 06: Pineapple Cake and the Golden Gift

**Main Character:** Feng (鳳梨酥 fènglísū) — Pineapple Cake
**Guest Characters:** Uncle Piggle Wiggle
**Setting:** Taipei 101 mall
**Theme:** Generosity
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 鳳梨酥 (fènglísū) = pineapple cake

---

Feng had wrapped himself in his finest gift box — the red one with the gold ribbon and the embossed phoenix logo — and was admiring his reflection in the polished marble floor of the Taipei 101 mall.

"Exquisite," he announced. "Simply exquisite. I was born to be wrapped beautifully."

Feng was a **鳳梨酥** (fènglísū) = pineapple cake, and he took his job very, very seriously. Pineapple cakes were Taiwan's most famous export — a golden, crumbly, buttery pastry filled with sweet pineapple jam, packaged in gorgeous boxes and given as gifts to everyone from business partners to grandmothers to foreign dignitaries who had no idea what they were but ate them on the plane home and immediately became obsessed.

Being a pineapple cake wasn't just a food thing. It was a DIPLOMACY thing.

And Feng was the most diplomatic pineapple cake in all of **台灣** (Táiwān) = Taiwan.

He lived in the fanciest bakery on the B1 level of the Taipei 101 mall — the shopping center at the base of the tallest building in Taiwan, a skyscraper so impressive it made other skyscrapers feel like they should have tried harder. The bakery's display cases gleamed like jewelry stores, and the pineapple cakes were arranged in rows like golden soldiers standing at attention.

"Allow me to present myself," Feng said to every customer who walked by. "I am a gift. Not just a snack. A GIFT. My packaging alone won a design award. Would you like to take me to someone special?"

Most customers smiled politely. Some bought him. Some didn't. Either way, Feng remained magnificent.

But there was a problem with being the perfect gift: Feng had never been given to anyone.

Not really. Sure, customers had purchased pineapple cakes from his bakery. But Feng himself — THIS particular pineapple cake, with his gold ribbon and his phoenix box — had been on the display shelf for three weeks. The bakery owner kept him there as the "sample" — the one that showed customers what they'd be getting. The advertisement. The model.

"I'm a display piece," Feng whispered one night, when the mall was closed and the lights were dim. "I'm packaging with nothing inside. No. Wait. I have pineapple jam inside. But I have no PURPOSE."

He looked through the bakery's glass door at the empty mall corridor, where the only movement was a janitor pushing a mop and a stray beetle making its way across the vast, shiny floor.

"I want to be given," Feng said. "Actually given. To someone who needs a gift."

The next morning, fate arrived in the form of the strangest visitor the Taipei 101 mall had ever seen.

He was a man — at least, he was shaped like a man — wearing a top hat, a magnificent mustache that curled at the ends like question marks, and a coat covered in patches of every color. He walked with a bounce in his step, carried a carpet bag that clinked with mysterious bottles, and hummed a tune that made everyone who heard it feel inexplicably happy.

"**來來來**!" (lái lái lái) called the pretzel vendor — "come come come!" — but the strange man waved pleasantly and walked right past.

"**好吃**!" (hǎochī) offered the bubble tea seller — "delicious!" — but the man just tipped his hat and kept walking.

He stopped in front of Feng's bakery. He peered through the glass at the rows of pineapple cakes. And then he looked directly at Feng — the display piece, the showroom model, the gift that had never been given — and smiled a smile so warm it could have melted butter.

"Ah," said the man. "There you are."

The bakery door chimed as he entered. The salesgirl asked if he needed help.

"I'd like that one, please," he said, pointing at Feng. "The one in the red box with the gold ribbon."

"Oh, that's our display sample—"

"That's the one I need."

Something in his voice — kind, certain, impossible to argue with — made the salesgirl shrug and hand over Feng's box. The man paid, tucked the box under his arm, and walked back into the mall.

Feng was trembling. He was being carried. He was LEAVING THE DISPLAY CASE.

"Excuse me," Feng said, his muffled voice coming through the gift box. "Not to be rude, but who ARE you?"

The man tilted the box and spoke through the ribbon. "My name is Uncle Piggle Wiggle. And I believe you're exactly the gift I've been looking for."

"You've been looking for a pineapple cake?"

"I've been looking for generosity. And you, my golden friend, are absolutely full of it. You just haven't had the chance to show it yet."

Uncle Piggle Wiggle carried Feng through the mall and up, up, up — past the luxury floors, past the observatory entrance, past the wind damper viewing deck — until they reached the 89th-floor outdoor observation area, where the wind was sharp and the city of Taipei spread out below like a circuit board made of lights.

But they weren't there for the view.

Sitting on a bench near the railing was a girl. She was about ten, wearing a school uniform, and she was holding a crumpled piece of paper in both hands. Her eyes were red.

"That's Mei-Ling," Uncle Piggle Wiggle told Feng quietly. "Her family just moved to Taipei from Hualien. Tomorrow is her first day at a new school. She doesn't know anyone. She's terrified."

"Why are you telling ME this?" asked Feng.

"Because you're a gift. And she needs one."

Uncle Piggle Wiggle walked over to Mei-Ling and sat down beside her. He didn't ask what was wrong. He didn't tell her everything would be fine. He just pulled a small bottle from his carpet bag — a bottle filled with something that sparkled like liquid gold — and set it on the bench between them.

"In my experience," he said gently, "the best way to make a friend is to give them something. Not because you want something back. Not because you want them to like you. But because giving is the bravest thing a person can do."

He placed Feng's gift box on Mei-Ling's lap.

Mei-Ling blinked. "What's this?"

"A gift. From someone who was made to be given."

She untied the gold ribbon. She opened the red box. And there was Feng — golden, crumbly, perfect — sitting in his tissue paper nest like a tiny edible jewel.

"**鳳梨酥**," Mei-Ling whispered. "Pineapple cake."

"Take it to school tomorrow," said Uncle Piggle Wiggle. "Share it with the person sitting next to you. You'd be amazed how many friendships start with 'would you like a piece of pineapple cake?'"

Mei-Ling looked at the cake. Then she looked at Uncle Piggle Wiggle. Then she laughed — a wet, hiccupy, surprised laugh, the kind you make when you've been crying and someone says exactly the right thing.

"**謝謝**" (xièxie) she said — "thank you."

"Don't thank me," said Uncle Piggle Wiggle, standing and tipping his hat. "Thank the cake. He's been waiting his whole life for this moment."

And then he was gone — walking away with his carpet bag and his magnificent mustache, humming that impossible tune, as if jolly mustachioed men delivering pineapple cakes on the 89th floor of skyscrapers was the most normal thing in the world.

Mei-Ling cradled Feng's box all the way home. She set him on her desk beside her new school bag. She fell asleep looking at his gold ribbon.

And Feng? Feng was GLOWING. Not metaphorically — his golden crust was actually, literally, slightly luminous. Because for the first time, he wasn't a display piece. He wasn't packaging. He was a real gift, chosen for a real person, and tomorrow he would do the thing he was born to do.

The next morning, Mei-Ling walked into her new classroom at Taipei Municipal Elementary School with Feng's box in her hands and her heart in her throat. The classroom was full of kids who all knew each other and had inside jokes and friend groups, and she was the new kid from Hualien who knew nobody.

She sat down at her assigned desk. The girl next to her glanced over, curious.

Mei-Ling took a breath. She opened Feng's box.

"Would you like a piece of pineapple cake?" she asked. Her voice shook, but only a little.

The girl's eyes went wide. "Is that from the Taipei 101 bakery? The one with the phoenix logo?"

"Yeah."

"I LOVE their pineapple cake!"

"Me too."

Mei-Ling broke Feng in half — carefully, reverently, the way you break something precious when you're choosing to share it. Golden crumbs scattered like tiny fireworks. The pineapple jam inside was bright, sweet, and perfect.

She gave half to the girl.

"I'm Mei-Ling."

"I'm Xiao-Yú."

They each took a bite at the same time.

"**歐伊西**!" (ōuyīxī) they said in unison — "yummy!" — and then they looked at each other and burst out laughing, because saying the same thing at the same time is the universal sign that you're going to be friends.

And Feng, crumbled between two sets of smiling teeth, felt something he had never felt before. Not the sadness of being broken. Not the loss of his perfect packaging. But the deep, golden, buttery joy of being shared.

Because THAT'S what a gift is for. Not to sit in a box looking beautiful. Not to win design awards. Not to be displayed behind glass. A gift is for GIVING. And the moment you give yourself away — really, truly, with your whole crumbly heart — you become the most beautiful thing in the world.

Even without the ribbon.
