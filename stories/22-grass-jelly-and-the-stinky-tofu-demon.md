# Story 22: Grass Jelly and the Stinky Tofu Demon

**Main Character:** Caǎo (仙草 xiāncǎo) — Grass Jelly
**Guest Characters:** None
**Setting:** Shenkeng Old Street, New Taipei City
**Theme:** Don't judge by smell (or appearance)
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 臭豆腐 (chòu dòufu) = stinky tofu

---

The smell hit Caǎo from six blocks away.

Now, let's be clear about something. Caǎo — a dark, inky, black-green cube of **仙草** (xiāncǎo) = grass jelly, wearing a tiny black beanie and radiating a faint herbal-minty aura — was not the kind of dessert ingredient who got worked up about things. Surprise birthday party? "Cool." Typhoon? "Whatever." That time Mango accidentally set a night market on fire with enthusiasm? "Saw it coming."

But this smell was *extraordinary*.

It was the smell of a thousand fermented dreams colliding with a dumpster that had been left in the sun and then hugged by a wet dog who had recently rolled in something unspeakable.

"Impressive," said Caǎo, adjusting his beanie. "Somebody's either making masterpiece-level stinky tofu, or a sewer exploded. Either way, I respect it."

He was sitting on the steps of an old brick building on **深坑老街** (Shēnkēng Lǎo Jiē) = Shenkeng Old Street, a narrow, charming lane in New Taipei City where practically every shop, restaurant, cart, and grandmother sold some version of **臭豆腐** (chòu dòufu) = stinky tofu. If you've never smelled stinky tofu, imagine blue cheese and gym socks had a baby, and that baby decided to become a professional wrestler. Now imagine that smell is coming from something *delicious*.

Because here's the thing about stinky tofu — and this is important, so lean in close — the stinkier it is, the more incredible it tastes. Crispy on the outside. Soft on the inside. Served with pickled cabbage and chili sauce. Absolute perfection.

Shenkeng was the stinky tofu capital of Taiwan. Every shop had its own secret fermentation recipe. The whole street smelled like... well, like Shenkeng. Locals loved it. Tourists made faces, took one brave bite, and immediately ordered three more plates.

Caǎo fit right in. Grass jelly had always been the cool, quiet side dish served alongside stinky tofu — a herbal, refreshing contrast to all that funky intensity. He'd come to Shenkeng for what he called "a vibe alignment retreat," which mostly meant sitting very still and looking mysterious.

But that smell. That was not regular Shenkeng smell. That was something else entirely.

He followed it.

Around the corner, behind a stack of wooden crates near the old tofu factory, he found the source.

It was a demon.

Specifically, it was the smallest, roundest, most pathetic-looking demon Caǎo had ever seen. She was about the size of a rice cooker, with pudgy arms, stubby legs, two tiny horns that looked like they'd been installed crooked, enormous watery eyes, and a smile so big and hopeful it could power a small city.

She was also crying. Big, snotty, hiccupping sobs.

And she smelled like concentrated stinky tofu that had been fermented for approximately one thousand years.

"Hey," said Caǎo.

The little demon shrieked, fell over backwards, scrambled up, and tried to hide behind a crate that was significantly smaller than she was.

"Please don't run away!" she wailed. "I know I smell bad! Everybody says so! The other demons said I was too stinky even for demons and that's saying something because demons literally live in swamps!"

Caǎo blinked slowly. "I wasn't running away. I was standing here. Standing is, like, the opposite of running."

The demon peeked out. "You're... you're not going to hold your nose?"

"I don't have a nose."

"Oh." She blinked. "Well, you're not going to make a face?"

"This is my face. It always looks like this."

And that was true. Caǎo's expression was a permanent state of calm, unbothered coolness, like a cat who had just been told the house was on fire and had decided the house could sort itself out.

The little demon crept out from behind the crate. "I'm Xiǎo Chòu," she sniffled. "小臭. It means 'Little Stinky.' My parents named me that because even when I was born, I smelled like—"

"Like stinky tofu. Yeah, I picked up on that."

"The other demon kids kicked me out of the group. They said I was embarrassing. They said no one would ever want to be friends with someone who smells this bad." Her enormous eyes welled up again. "Am I really that terrible?"

Caǎo looked at her for a long moment.

"Kid," he said, "you're standing in the middle of Shenkeng Old Street. You know what this entire town smells like?"

Xiǎo Chòu sniffed. "Um..."

"Exactly like you. This whole street is literally famous for smelling like you. They put it on postcards. Tourists come from Japan specifically to smell this. You're not a problem. You're on-brand."

Xiǎo Chòu's eyes went wide. "Really?"

"Come on. I'll show you."

And so began possibly the most fragrant walking tour in Taiwanese history.

Caǎo — cool, unhurried, beanie tilted at exactly the right angle — led Xiǎo Chòu down the full length of Shenkeng Old Street. At every shop, he stopped and pointed.

"Fried stinky tofu. Classic. See how they put pickled cabbage on top? The crunch is essential."

Xiǎo Chòu pressed her face against the glass. The shop owner saw her, took one sniff, and gave a thumbs up. "Wow, you smell amazing! Like our premium aged batch!"

Xiǎo Chòu looked at Caǎo in shock. He shrugged. "Told you."

Next shop. "Grilled stinky tofu on a stick. Street food royalty. The char marks are where the magic happens."

Next shop. "Stinky tofu **火鍋** (huǒguō) = hot pot. You throw the tofu in a boiling pot of spicy broth with vegetables and noodles and it becomes the single greatest cold-weather food known to humankind or demonkind."

Next shop. Caǎo paused. "And this... this is stinky tofu ice cream."

Xiǎo Chòu stared. "That can't be real."

"It's real. This is Taiwan. We put everything in ice cream. Stinky tofu, wasabi, pork floss, cheese and mango together. We are a nation of culinary daredevils and we have zero regrets."

Xiǎo Chòu giggled for the first time. It sounded like a tiny bell being rung inside a sock.

"But Caǎo," she said, suddenly serious, "those are all tofu. I'm a *demon*. Demons aren't supposed to smell like food. Demons are supposed to be scary."

Caǎo adjusted his beanie. "Kid, I'm a cube of herbal jelly wearing a hat. I'm supposed to be a side dish. Life doesn't always go according to the job description, and that's kind of the best part."

He sat down on a stone bench. Xiǎo Chòu climbed up next to him. Even sitting still, her stinky tofu aroma wafted down the street like an invisible flag, powerful and unapologetic.

"Here's the thing about stinky tofu," Caǎo said, in the tone of someone delivering ancient wisdom while also being extremely chill about it. "The smell is what scares people away at first. But the smell is also what brings people back. The people who actually try it? They fall in love. Every time. The stink isn't the problem. The stink is the *invitation*."

Xiǎo Chòu was quiet for a moment. Then she whispered, "The stink is the invitation?"

"Yeah. It's like a test. The brave ones follow the smell. The brave ones get the good stuff."

She sat up a little straighter. Her tiny horns gleamed.

That's when the rumbling started.

Down the far end of the old street, a crowd appeared. Demon children — dozens of them — small, medium, and one who was frankly the size of a refrigerator. They had horns and tails and bat wings and one of them appeared to be on fire, which nobody seemed concerned about. They were sniffing the air like a pack of extremely enthusiastic puppies.

"WHAT IS THAT INCREDIBLE SMELL?" bellowed the refrigerator-sized one.

"It smells like the BEST stinky tofu in the UNIVERSE!" shouted a tiny demon with six eyes.

"FOLLOW THE STINK!" they all screamed in unison.

And they charged. Directly toward Xiǎo Chòu.

She panicked. "Caǎo! They're coming! What do I do?"

Caǎo stood up. He brushed off his beanie. He looked at the stampeding horde of hungry demon children with the same expression he used for everything — total, unshakeable calm.

"You stand right there," he said. "And you own it."

Then he did something extraordinary. He walked — *strolled*, really, because Caǎo never rushed — over to the nearest fried stinky tofu stall. He picked up a plate of the crispiest, most golden, most magnificently stinky fried tofu on the whole street. He set it down right in front of Xiǎo Chòu.

And then he slid himself onto the plate. Right next to the tofu. A perfect dark cube of herbal grass jelly, cool and refreshing, sitting beside golden fried stinky tofu like they'd been paired together since the beginning of time.

Because they had. That's what grass jelly does. It's the **完美搭配** (wánměi dāpèi) = perfect pairing.

"Caǎo, what are you DOING?" Xiǎo Chòu squeaked.

"Completing the combo," he said calmly. "Stinky tofu and grass jelly. Hot and cool. Crispy and smooth. Funky and herbal. It's the balance of the universe on a single plate. I'm exactly where I'm supposed to be."

The demon kids arrived like a cheerful, starving avalanche.

"STINKY TOFU! AND GRASS JELLY! THE ULTIMATE COMBO!"

"WHO FOUND THIS? WHO FOUND THE BEST FOOD EVER?"

Xiǎo Chòu raised her pudgy hand. "Um... I think... I did? I mean, my smell sort of..."

"YOUR SMELL IS AMAZING!"

"YOU'RE THE BEST DEMON EVER!"

"CAN YOU COME WITH US EVERYWHERE? WE WANT TO FIND FOOD THIS GOOD ALL THE TIME!"

And then they ate. Everything. The stinky tofu vanished in a frenzy of happy crunching. The pickled cabbage disappeared. The chili sauce was licked clean. And Caǎo — cool, smooth, herbal Caǎo — was devoured in exactly three bites by a demon kid who declared, with bits of grass jelly on her chin, that it was "the most refreshing thing I have ever tasted in my entire demon life."

Xiǎo Chòu watched in horror. "CAǍO! They ATE you!"

From somewhere inside the demon kid's extremely satisfied stomach, a faint, calm voice echoed:

"Yeah. I completed the perfect flavor combination. No big deal."

A pause.

"Pretty comfortable in here, actually. It's warm. Kind of cozy."

Another pause.

"Tell Mango I said 'later.' She'll know what it means. She won't, actually. But it'll bother her, and that's funny."

The demon children crowned Xiǎo Chòu their official Best Friend and Smell Leader. They hoisted her onto their shoulders — well, the refrigerator-sized one hoisted her; the others cheered — and paraded her down Shenkeng Old Street while every shop owner waved and tossed free samples.

Xiǎo Chòu laughed so hard her tiny horns wobbled.

She never felt stinky again. Or rather, she still smelled exactly the same — like a glorious, fearless, unapologetic stinky tofu masterpiece — but now she knew the truth.

The stink was never the problem.

The stink was the invitation.

And the brave ones? They always follow the smell.

## Goodnight Blessing

The ones who are brave enough to get close will discover something wonderful. So don't hide your stink. Don't dim your strange. Just be exactly, magnificently, unapologetically you. Somewhere out there, your perfect pairing is waiting. Now close your eyes. Breathe in. Let the cool, herbal quiet of grass jelly settle over you like a minty shadow. Sleep deep, sleep brave, and dream of old streets where everything smells like adventure. Goodnight, you beautifully weird little human. Wǎn ān. **晚安.**
