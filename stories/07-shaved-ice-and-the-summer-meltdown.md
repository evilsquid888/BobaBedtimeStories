# Story 07: Shaved Ice and the Summer Meltdown

**Main Character:** Bīng (剉冰 cuòbīng) — Shaved Ice
**Guest Characters:** Piper Paddlefoot (Emperor Penguin, Penguinsnowboard)
**Setting:** Raohe Night Market, Taipei
**Theme:** Impermanence
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 剉冰 (cuòbīng) = shaved ice

---

Bīng was having a CRISIS.

"THIS," he announced to absolutely everyone within a fifty-meter radius, "is LITERALLY the WORST DAY of my ENTIRE EXISTENCE."

Bīng was a mountain of **剉冰** (cuòbīng) = shaved ice — a towering, glorious, gravity-defying pile of finely shaved ice drenched in mango syrup, condensed milk, and red bean, crowned with a scoop of taro ice cream, three gummy bears (he'd added those himself), and a tiny paper umbrella that he insisted on wearing at all times because fashion is important even when you're a frozen dessert.

And he was melting.

To be fair, EVERYTHING was melting. It was August in Taipei, which meant the air temperature was approximately the same as the inside of a dragon's mouth. The humidity was 97%, which meant the air wasn't just hot — it was WET hot, the kind of hot where you step outside and immediately feel like you're wearing a warm, damp sweater made of air.

**熱死了** (rè sǐ le) = "so hot I could die" didn't even begin to cover it.

The **饒河夜市** (Ráohé yèshì) = Raohe Night Market was packed — hundreds of sweaty, happy people shuffling between food stalls in the thick summer air. The market stretched in a long, covered corridor, lit by fluorescent lights and decorated with red banners, and the smells were extraordinary: pepper buns, medicinal stew, grilled corn, stinky tofu, and the sweet, creamy perfume of a dozen different dessert stalls.

Bīng's stall was right in the middle of it all, and he was the star attraction. Or he HAD been, before the melting started.

"MORE IS MORE!" he had shouted just an hour ago, when his toppings were perfectly arranged and his ice mountain was pristine. He'd struck poses. He'd sparkled. He'd made children gasp and tourists reach for their cameras.

But ice doesn't last in August.

First, the taro ice cream scoop slid sideways like a tiny purple avalanche. Then the condensed milk started running in rivers down his sides. The red bean topping, once a proud crown, began a slow, tragic descent toward the bowl. And Bīng himself — the ice, the actual ice that was his BODY — was turning from a magnificent mountain into a disappointing puddle.

"I'm DIMINISHING," Bīng wailed. "I'm becoming LESS. This is a TRAGEDY of EPIC PROPORTIONS."

The pepper bun vendor next door rolled his eyes. "You do this every night."

"Because it happens every night! Do you know what it's like to melt? Of course you don't! You're BREAD. You just get STALE. That takes DAYS. I'm losing myself in MINUTES."

"Drama queen."

"Drama KING, thank you very much."

Bīng's sunglasses — tiny, fashionable, perched on the peak of his ice mountain — slid down as his summit lowered. He caught them with a slushy hand and jammed them back on. Dignity. Even in dissolution, dignity.

That's when the penguin showed up.

She came sliding — literally sliding — down the night market corridor on a glittery blue snowboard, weaving between pedestrians with the casual grace of someone who snowboards through crowded places as a lifestyle choice. She was an Emperor penguin, sleek and tall (for a penguin), with a confident gleam in her eye and an absolutely magnificent sense of balance.

She skidded to a stop in front of Bīng's stall, flipped her snowboard up, and caught it under one flipper.

"Slide into it!" she said. "Whoa — you look like you're having a rough night, mountain man."

Bīng stared. "Who... what... is that a SNOWBOARD? In a NIGHT MARKET? In AUGUST?"

"I'm Piper Paddlefoot," said the penguin. "I go where the ice is. And you, my drippy friend, are definitely ice. Or... you were. What happened?"

"SUMMER happened! Heat happened! The cruel, relentless laws of THERMODYNAMICS happened! I am MELTING, Piper! I am becoming WATER!"

Piper looked at the puddle forming beneath Bīng's bowl. She looked at the dripping toppings. She looked at the tiny paper umbrella, which was now at a thirty-degree angle like a sinking ship's mast.

"Huh," she said. "That's rough. But also... kind of beautiful?"

Bīng sputtered. "BEAUTIFUL? I'm a PUDDLE!"

"Not yet. You're somewhere between a mountain and a puddle. That's the interesting part." Piper hopped up onto the stall counter and sat beside Bīng's bowl. "Back where I come from, ice is everywhere. Glaciers, icebergs, frozen oceans. And you know what? They all melt eventually. The biggest glacier in Antarctica is melting right now. Has been for years."

"That's supposed to make me feel BETTER?"

"It's supposed to make you think. The glacier doesn't sit around crying about melting. It flows into the ocean and becomes something new. Currents, waves, rain, clouds, snow — eventually it becomes a glacier again. It's a cycle. Nothing is really lost."

Bīng stopped wailing for a moment. His sunglasses dripped. "But I won't become a glacier. I'll become a puddle on a night market floor. Someone will MOP me."

"And then?"

"And then I'll be in the mop bucket. And then someone will pour the mop bucket out. And then I'll be in the gutter. And then—"

"And then you'll evaporate. And become a cloud. And rain down on a mountain. And someone will collect the water and freeze it. And shave it into ice. And pile toppings on it. And there you'll be again. Brand new. Fresh. An even BIGGER mountain."

Bīng considered this. His remaining ice shifted as he tilted his head. "You really think so?"

"I'm a penguin who rides a snowboard through Taiwanese night markets. I don't do 'think.' I do 'know.'" Piper grinned. "Slide into it, Bīng. Don't fight the melt. RIDE it."

Something shifted in Bīng's frozen core. Not a physical shift (although plenty of those were happening as he continued to melt). An emotional one.

He looked at his toppings — the mango syrup making golden rivers down his sides, the red bean scattered like rubies, the condensed milk painting white trails through everything. It was messy. It was chaotic. It was, if you looked at it the right way, gorgeous.

"You know what?" said Bīng, straightening his sunglasses. "You're right. I've been fighting the melt my whole life. Every night, I get built up, and every night I melt. And every night, I act like it's the end of the world."

"But it's not."

"It's not. It's just... the end of THIS shape. This mountain. This version of me."

"And tomorrow?"

"Tomorrow, I'll be a NEW mountain. Even better. Even taller. Even more toppings."

"That's the spirit!"

Bīng drew himself up — or rather, drew himself together, since he was getting shorter by the minute — and looked out at the night market crowd. A family was approaching the stall. A mother, a father, and two kids with wide, excited eyes.

"**來來來**!" (lái lái lái) Bīng called out — "come come come!" — with every ounce of his diminishing but still magnificent voice. "GET your shaved ice! The MOST DELICIOUS, most BEAUTIFUL, most WONDERFULLY TEMPORARY shaved ice in ALL of Raohe Night Market!"

The family stopped. The kids pointed. "Mama! Mama! **剉冰**!"

"MORE IS MORE!" Bīng bellowed, and even though he was now only half the mountain he'd been, he was twice as bright. His toppings cascaded. His syrup sparkled. His paper umbrella stood at attention.

The mother ordered two bowls. The vendor — a sweaty, cheerful man who had been making shaved ice for thirty years — built two fresh mountains of ice and loaded them with toppings. But it was Bīng's bowl that the family chose to photograph: the slightly melted, perfectly imperfect, gloriously messy original.

"**好吃**!" (hǎochī) the father said after his first bite — "delicious!"

"**歐伊西**!" (ōuyīxī) the kids shouted — "yummy!"

And they ate it all. Every drip, every drop, every melting, swirling, beautiful mouthful. They ate it with the pure joy of people who understand that the best things in life don't last forever — and that's exactly what makes them precious.

Bīng's bowl was empty by 9:47 PM. Just a pool of sweet, colorful water at the bottom, with a paper umbrella floating on top like a tiny boat.

Piper hopped down from the counter. "Not bad, mountain man. Not bad at all."

"Same time tomorrow?" Bīng's voice was faint now — just a whisper from the last crystal of ice clinging to the side of the bowl.

"Wouldn't miss it. Slide into it, Bīng."

"More is more, Piper."

The last ice crystal melted.

And the next morning, the vendor filled the ice shaver with a fresh block of frozen water — water that had once been rain, that had once been a cloud, that had once been a puddle on a night market floor — and began shaving.

A new mountain rose.

Taller. Brighter. Even more toppings.

Bīng was back.
