# Story 16: Egg Waffle and the Night Market Showdown

**Main Character:** Dàn (雞蛋糕 jīdàngāo) — Egg Waffle
**Guest Characters:** Finnegan Flipsworth (Rockhopper Penguin, Penguinsnowboard)
**Setting:** Fengjia Night Market, Taichung
**Theme:** Performing for joy
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 雞蛋糕 (jīdàngāo) = egg waffle cake

---

"STEP RIGHT UP, FOLKS! Step RIGHT up!"

Dàn's voice could be heard three stalls down, which was impressive for a waffle cake the size of a golf ball. But what Dàn lacked in size, he made up for in VOLUME, ENTHUSIASM, and the unshakable belief that he was the greatest street performer the world had ever produced.

**雞蛋糕** (jīdàngāo) = egg waffle cake — those adorable, golden-brown, egg-shaped mini cakes cooked in special griddle molds at night markets all over Taiwan. They came in shapes: fish, stars, hearts, bears, and — most commonly — little puffy eggs, crispy on the outside, soft and cakey on the inside, smelling like butter and happiness.

Dàn was an egg-shaped one, naturally. He had a golden-brown crust, a soft cakey interior, a tiny showman's top hat that he'd fashioned from a bottle cap, and the energy of a caffeine-powered firework.

He lived at **逢甲夜市** (Féngjǐa yèshì) = Fengjia Night Market in Taichung — the biggest night market in central Taiwan and, by some measures, the entire country. Fengjia was a universe unto itself: a sprawling maze of food stalls, game booths, clothing shops, and mystery stores that sold things you didn't know you needed until you bought three of them. Every night, thousands of people descended on Fengjia to eat, shop, play, and experience the organized chaos that makes Taiwan's night markets the best in the world.

And every night, Dàn performed.

His stage was the counter of Mr. Lín's Egg Waffle Stall, positioned at a prime corner location where two busy aisles met. Mr. Lín cooked the waffles in a cast-iron griddle that sizzled and steamed, and Dàn stood on top of the display tray and put on a show.

He sang. He danced. He told jokes. He did impressions of other food items ("I'm a stinky tofu! I smell like a GYM but I taste like a DREAM!"). He performed tiny acrobatic flips, tumbling from one egg-shaped mold to another with the grace of a gymnast and the recklessness of a dessert with nothing to lose.

"**來來來**!" (lái lái lái) he bellowed — "come come come!" — in perfect imitation of every night market vendor who ever lived.

The crowd loved him. Kids pointed and laughed. Parents bought extra bags of egg waffles just because Dàn's performance made them smile. Mr. Lín had the best sales in Fengjia, and he knew exactly why.

But lately, something had been bothering Dàn.

A new stall had opened across the aisle. It was called "The Waffle King," and it was everything Dàn's stall wasn't: slick, modern, with LED signs, a social media presence, and a waffle iron that could produce fifty waffles per minute in twelve different flavors. The owner was a young man with a marketing degree who used words like "brand synergy" and "customer engagement metrics."

The Waffle King was winning.

Not because the waffles were better (they weren't — Mr. Lín's recipe had been passed down for three generations and was objectively superior). But because the Waffle King had something Dàn didn't: a professional show. A hired MC with a wireless microphone. A choreographed mascot dance. A TikTok account with forty thousand followers.

"Step right up," Dàn whispered, watching from his tray as customers drifted to the other side of the aisle. "Step... right... up."

Nobody stepped.

Mr. Lín noticed the empty counter and sighed. "Maybe we need LED signs too," he said.

"We don't need SIGNS!" Dàn protested. "We need HEART! We need SOUL! We need—"

"Customers."

"Those too."

That night, Dàn sat on the cooling griddle after the market closed and stared at the dark, empty aisle. The LED lights of the Waffle King glowed across the way like a rival sun. Dàn's top hat — his tiny bottle cap hat, which he'd worn for two years — suddenly felt silly. Small. Unprofessional.

"Maybe I should quit performing," he muttered. "Maybe I'm just a waffle. Not a show."

"WOOOHOOO — wait, how do I stop?!"

The scream came from above. Dàn looked up just in time to see a blur of red, orange crest feathers, and fish stickers come rocketing down the aisle on a snowboard, crash through a stack of cardboard boxes, rebound off a trash can, spin three times in the air, and land — PERFECTLY — on Dàn's counter.

The creature was a Rockhopper penguin. Small, wild-eyed, with an explosion of yellow-orange crest feathers and the manic expression of someone who treats physics as a suggestion. He was standing on a red snowboard covered in fish stickers, breathing hard, and grinning like he'd just won the lottery.

"NAILED IT!" the penguin announced. Then he noticed Dàn. "Oh hey! Is this a waffle? Am I on a waffle? SICK!"

"Who... WHAT... how did you—" Dàn sputtered.

"Finnegan Flipsworth! Rockhopper penguin, professional snowboarder, amateur everything else! I was boarding on a parking garage rooftop and kind of... launched into the night market. By accident. Mostly by accident. Like sixty percent accident. Maybe fifty." He extended a flipper. "What's your deal?"

"I'm Dàn. I'm an egg waffle. I used to perform for the crowd, but..." He looked across at the Waffle King's dark LED signs. "I'm thinking about retiring."

"RETIRING? From PERFORMING?" Finnegan's crest feathers stood straight up in alarm. "Bro, that's like a fish retiring from swimming! That's like me retiring from crashing into things! UNACCEPTABLE!"

"The other stall is better. They have LEDs and a mascot and forty thousand TikTok followers."

"So? Since when does performing require LED lights?"

"Since everyone started watching LED lights instead of me."

Finnegan hopped off his board and waddled over to Dàn. For a chaotic penguin, his eyes were surprisingly focused.

"Can I tell you something?" Finnegan said. "I am TERRIBLE at snowboarding. Like, objectively terrible. I can't stop. I can't turn left. I once snowboarded into a fish market and was banned from three countries. But you know why I keep doing it?"

"Because you're insane?"

"Because it's FUN! The moment I stop having fun — the moment it becomes about stats and followers and being the BEST — that's the moment it stops being worth doing. I don't snowboard to be professional. I snowboard because when I'm in the air, doing a flip I definitely cannot land, screaming at the top of my lungs, I feel ALIVE."

Dàn looked at his tiny top hat. He picked it up and turned it over in his hands.

"I started performing because I loved it," he said quietly. "The kids' faces. The laughter. The way a boring Tuesday night market became something SPECIAL because a waffle cake did a backflip on a griddle. I didn't do it for followers. I did it for JOY."

"Then do it for joy! Forget the Waffle King! Forget the LEDs! Do what you were BORN to do!"

"What if nobody watches?"

Finnegan grinned — the wide, slightly unhinged grin of a penguin who has never once worried about an audience.

"Then you perform for yourself. And for me. And I am a VERY enthusiastic audience. WOOOHOOO!"

Something ignited in Dàn's cakey core. Not a new flame — the old one, the original one, the one that had made him leap onto a counter two years ago and shout "STEP RIGHT UP!" for the first time. It had never gone out. It had just gotten... quiet.

The next night, Fengjia Night Market was packed. The Waffle King was running its usual slick operation — LED lights, hired MC, the works. The crowd drifted in that direction, pulled by the glow.

And then, from Mr. Lín's humble corner stall, a tiny voice rang out:

"STEP. RIGHT. UP!"

Dàn was on the counter. Top hat on. Steam rising from the griddle behind him. And he wasn't just performing — he was UNLEASHING.

He sang a ridiculous song about being a waffle cake that he made up on the spot. He did backflips between the egg molds. He told jokes so bad they were good ("Why did the waffle cross the road? To get to the BATTER side!"). He did an impression of a stinky tofu having an identity crisis. He invited kids to guess what shape the next waffle would be.

And behind him, on the rooftop of the stall, Finnegan Flipsworth was doing snowboard tricks — grinding along the roof edge, launching off a ventilation pipe, spinning in the air with his fish-sticker board catching the night market lights.

"WOOOHOOO — wait, how do I stop?!" Finnegan yelled, crashed into a stack of paper bags, bounced off, did an accidental triple spin, and landed on the counter beside Dàn.

The crowd LOST it.

Not the social-media-friendly, polite-clapping crowd. The REAL crowd. The belly-laughing, pointing, cheering, grabbing-their-friends-and-saying-"you-have-to-see-this" crowd. The crowd that had come to a night market not for brand synergy, but for something real, something spontaneous, something JOYFUL.

Within twenty minutes, Mr. Lín's stall was surrounded. Within thirty, the line stretched down the aisle. Within an hour, the Waffle King's hired MC had stopped performing because everyone was watching the egg waffle and the penguin.

"**太棒了**!" (tài bàng le) the crowd roared — "AWESOME!"

"**好吃**!" (hǎochī) people shouted as they bit into Mr. Lín's fresh egg waffles — "DELICIOUS!"

Dàn performed until his golden crust was glistening with the steam from the griddle and his tiny top hat was crooked from all the backflips. He performed until his cakey interior was warm and soft from the heat. He performed until a little boy reached into the display tray, picked him up, and took a bite.

The boy chewed.

His face transformed — the way faces do when someone eats something made with love.

"**歐伊西**!" (ōuyīxī) he said. "Mama, this is the BEST waffle!"

And Dàn, crunchy and warm and being eaten by a kid who was still laughing from the show, felt the purest joy he had ever known. Not the joy of winning. Not the joy of beating the competition. The joy of performing — really, truly performing — not for followers or fame or forty thousand TikTok viewers, but for one kid, one laugh, one night in the most magical market in Taiwan.

"Step right up," he whispered one last time, crumbly and content. "The show... is the thing."

## Goodnight Blessing

May your stage be wherever you stand, and may your audience be whoever is lucky enough to be there. Step right up to your dreams. The show is always worth it. Goodnight, showstopper. Goodnight.
