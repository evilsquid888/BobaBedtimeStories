# Story 21: Boba and the Midnight Demon Parade

**Main Character:** Boba (波霸 bōbà) — Tapioca Pearl
**Guest Characters:** None
**Setting:** Keelung, during Ghost Month Festival
**Theme:** Sharing makes you whole
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 鬼月 (guǐ yuè) = ghost month

---

The trouble started — as the best trouble always does — with a tiny sneeze.

"ACHOO!"

It came from behind a stack of pineapple cakes at the Keelung night market, and it was followed by the unmistakable sound of a very small someone bonking their head on a table leg.

"Ow ow ow ow ow."

Boba froze mid-bounce. She'd been rolling through the market stalls at top speed (her favorite speed), dodging feet and flip-flops, when that sneeze stopped her like a speed bump. She tilted her glossy, black-brown body and squinted into the shadows beneath the cake display.

Two enormous eyes stared back at her.

Correction: two enormous, *adorable*, tear-filled eyes — set in a round red face no bigger than a rice ball, topped with two stubby horns that looked like they'd been installed crooked by someone who'd lost the instruction manual.

"Um," said Boba.

"Um," said the tiny creature.

They stared at each other for exactly three seconds.

Then Boba grinned. "Let's GO! What are you?"

---

Now, here's the thing you need to know. It was **鬼月** (guǐ yuè) = ghost month in Taiwan — that time of year when the gates between the spirit world and the living world swing open like a screen door with a busted latch. Lanterns lined the streets of **基隆** (Jīlóng) = Keelung, the harbor city famous for its rain, its temples, and its absolutely legendary night market.

During Ghost Month, most folks set out food offerings for visiting spirits and tried very hard not to think about what might be lurking in the alleyways after midnight.

Boba had never been afraid of midnight. Midnight was when the good snacks came out.

But what she'd found under that pineapple cake table wasn't a ghost at all.

It was a demon kid.

And — oh, dear reader — it was the cutest thing she had ever seen in her entire round life.

The little oni had rosy cheeks, a button nose, and horns so tiny they looked like candy corn someone had glued on as a joke. It wore a miniature tiger-striped loincloth and clutched a toy-sized iron club that appeared to be made of foam.

"Don't scream," whispered the demon kid, trembling. "Everyone keeps screaming and it hurts my feelings."

"Scream?" Boba bounced closer. "Why would I scream? You look like a plushie that escaped from a claw machine."

The demon kid's lip wobbled. "Really?"

"Really really. I'm Boba. What's your name?"

"Gōngō." The little demon sniffled. "It means 'Dumpling' in demon language." He paused. "Actually, I made that up. I just really like dumplings."

Boba was already in love.

---

Gōngō, it turned out, was not alone.

He led Boba behind the night market to a narrow alley between a temple and a fried chicken stand, and there — huddled together like a basket of the world's most improbable kittens — were eleven more demon children.

They were red, blue, green, and one unfortunate shade of orange that looked like a traffic cone. They had horns of every size (all small, all slightly wonky). They had enormous puppy-dog eyes that could melt a glacier. They had tiny fangs that couldn't have pierced a marshmallow.

One of them was crying because a pigeon had looked at her.

"We came through the Ghost Month gate," Gōngō explained, wringing his little clawed hands. "We traveled allllll the way from the spirit world because we heard — we HEARD — that Taiwan has the best snacks in any dimension."

"This is true," Boba confirmed, because she was honest.

"And what we want," Gōngō continued, his eyes going so wide they practically consumed his face, "what we want more than ANYTHING in the whole entire afterlife — is **珍珠奶茶** (zhēnzhū nǎichá) = boba milk tea."

All twelve demon kids clasped their hands together and made a sound like a choir of squeaky toys.

"But nobody will SERVE us!" wailed a blue demon in the back. "Every time we walk up to a stall, the humans go AAAAAAH and run away and knock over all the chairs!"

"One lady threw a shoe at me," said the orange demon sadly.

"To be fair," said a green demon, "you do look like a traffic cone, Mikan."

"THAT'S NOT HELPFUL, PUDDING."

Boba held up her tiny tapioca arms. "Okay, okay. First of all — you're all named after food?"

Twelve demon children nodded solemnly.

"I'm Mochi," said a red one.

"I'm Pudding," said the green one.

"I'm Soup," said a very round blue one.

"I respect that deeply," said Boba. "Second of all — nobody runs away from ME. Let's bounce!"

---

The plan was simple: Boba would order the boba milk tea, the demon kids would hide around the corner, and everyone would be happy.

The plan lasted approximately nine seconds.

Because demon children, as it turns out, have the patience and stealth capabilities of a bag of firecrackers rolling down a staircase.

Boba had barely reached the **奶茶** (nǎichá) = milk tea stall when Gōngō poked his head out from behind a trash can and stage-whispered, "IS THAT IT? IS THAT THE TEA? IT SMELLS AMAZING!"

Then Mikan climbed on top of Soup's shoulders to get a better look and both of them toppled into a display of **鳳梨酥** (fènglí sū) = pineapple cakes, sending golden pastries flying in every direction.

Then — and this was the part Boba would remember forever — tiny demon Mochi tried to drink directly from the tea-brewing machine, got her horn stuck in the spout, and hung there kicking her little legs while screaming, "I REGRET NOTHING!"

The tea vendor fainted.

The crowd scattered.

A grandma with an umbrella chased Pudding around a lantern pole three full times before Boba managed to explain that the demons were friendly, harmless, and — critically — paying customers.

(They were paying in spirit-world currency, which turned out to be small glowing pebbles that smelled like jasmine. Boba decided not to address the exchange rate.)

After much negotiation, apology, and one demon being gently removed from the tea machine with cooking tongs, they finally sat down with twelve tiny cups of boba milk tea.

The demon children stared at the cups like they'd been handed the moon.

"The bubbles," whispered Gōngō reverently. "They're so... *round*."

"Thanks," said Boba.

Then came the drinking.

Oh, reader. The drinking.

Soup tried to eat the straw. Just — chomped it. Bit it clean in half.

"That's not the food part!" Boba yelped.

"But it was right THERE," Soup protested, chewing thoughtfully. "It's pretty good, actually."

Mikan discovered the ice cubes and screamed. "THERE ARE COLD ROCKS IN MY DRINK! COLD! ROCKS!"

"Ice," Boba said patiently. "Those are ice."

"THE COLD ROCKS ARE ATTACKING ME!"

Pudding, meanwhile, was trying to suck a tapioca pearl through the straw with such intensity that his face turned from green to purple. When the pearl finally shot through, it hit him in the back of the throat and he made a sound like a surprised tuba.

"BWAAAAAAP."

"Incredible," he said, once he'd recovered. "Again. I want to do that again immediately."

They drank every last drop in under four minutes. Twelve cups, gone. The demon kids sat in a blissful pile, making small happy sounds like a litter of purring kittens.

"More?" said Gōngō hopefully.

---

And this, dear reader, is where our story takes its magnificent turn.

Because at the center of the Keelung Ghost Month Festival, under a canopy of floating lanterns, the annual Mega Milk Tea Competition was underway. The challenge: build the biggest, most glorious cup of boba milk tea the city had ever seen.

The reigning champion, a tea master named Auntie Jade, stood before a cup the size of a bathtub. It was filled to the brim with the creamiest, most fragrant milk tea in all of Taiwan — a perfect golden-brown that caught the lantern light like liquid amber.

But Auntie Jade was frowning.

"We don't have enough pearls!" she announced, staring into the magnificent cup. "We're three hundred tapioca pearls short! The tea is perfect, but without enough boba, it's just... milk tea. It's just tea with IDEAS!"

The crowd gasped.

The demon children, who had followed the commotion like moths to a very large flame, pressed their faces against the barrier ropes. Their enormous eyes reflected the giant cup like twin moons.

"That," breathed Gōngō, "is the most beautiful thing I have ever seen."

"It needs more pearls," said Mochi sadly.

And then — very slowly — every single demon child turned and looked at Boba.

Boba looked at the cup.

Boba looked at the demon kids.

Boba looked at herself — round, glossy, black-brown, and absolutely, perfectly, undeniably a tapioca pearl.

Something lit up inside her. Not fear. Not hesitation. It was the feeling a firework gets the moment the fuse reaches the powder. It was the feeling a song gets when the chorus finally, FINALLY hits.

"Oh," said Boba quietly. "Oh, I was BORN for this."

She climbed the rim of the giant cup. The crowd went silent. The demon children held their breath. Auntie Jade looked up in confusion.

Boba stood on the edge, her tiny arms spread wide, the warm steam rising around her like a hug from the universe itself.

"I am a tapioca pearl!" she announced, her voice ringing across the night market. "I was made from cassava root and rolled with love and cooked until I was JUST the right amount of chewy! My whole life — my WHOLE, ROUND LIFE — has been building to this moment! I wasn't put on this earth to sit on a counter! I was put on this earth to be in a CUP OF TEA!"

She looked down at the demon children's shining faces.

"AND TO BE SLURPED BY THE CUTEST LITTLE WEIRDOS THE SPIRIT WORLD HAS EVER PRODUCED!"

"LET'S! GO!"

She bounced.

The splash was magnificent.

It was the kind of splash that poets write about — a perfect arc of golden milk tea droplets catching the lantern light, a tiny black-brown sphere disappearing into the creamy depths with the grace of a diver and the enthusiasm of a cannonball.

The crowd erupted.

Auntie Jade, to her eternal credit, looked into the cup, saw one very happy tapioca pearl doing a backstroke, and said, "Well. That's one. Only two hundred and ninety-nine to go."

(Boba's heroism was, perhaps, more symbolic than practical. But symbolism COUNTS, okay?)

And then — because Ghost Month miracles are real and demon children have very powerful wishing magic — the cup began to fill. Pearl after pearl materialized in the tea, as if the spirit world itself had decided to chip in. Three hundred pearls. Four hundred. Five hundred. The cup overflowed with the most perfect boba anyone had ever seen.

Auntie Jade won the competition in a landslide.

The demon children got the first serving.

---

What happened next was a slurping of legendary proportions.

Twelve demon kids. One giant straw each. And the biggest cup of boba milk tea in Keelung history.

The sound was like twelve bathtubs draining simultaneously. It was like a typhoon made of joy. It was, frankly, disgusting and also deeply beautiful.

Gōngō sucked up a pearl, chewed with his eyes closed, and whispered, "I have achieved enlightenment."

Soup ate three more straws.

Mikan made peace with the ice cubes. ("The cold rocks are my friends now," she announced solemnly.)

Pudding went BWAAAAAAP four more times and loved every single one.

And Boba? Boba rose through the straw like a queen ascending a throne — up, up, up through the plastic tube, the tea rushing around her, the light at the top growing brighter — until she popped out into the warm night air and landed in Gōngō's open palm.

"That," said Boba, dripping with milk tea and glory, "was the single greatest moment of my life."

The demon children cheered so loudly that three lanterns fell out of the sky.

They slurped up every last pearl. Every drop of tea. Every melted ice cube (even Mikan's). They ate the cup holder. They ate the decorative napkins. Soup ate one more straw for the road.

"Best snack in any dimension," Gōngō declared, patting his round belly. "Confirmed."

As the Ghost Month gate began to shimmer at the edge of the harbor, ready to take them home, the twelve demon kids lined up and bowed to Boba — a formal, deeply respectful spirit-world bow that made their tiny horns wobble.

"Will you come back next year?" Boba asked.

"Every year," said Gōngō. "Forever and ever. Until the stars go out and even the ghosts get sleepy."

"Then I'll be here," said Boba. "With extra pearls."

The demon children walked into the shimmering gate one by one, waving their stubby arms, their little horns catching the last of the lantern light. Soup turned around one final time, ate the gate's doorframe, burped, and disappeared.

Boba watched them go, still dripping, still smiling, still the most perfectly round and complete version of herself she had ever been.

Because here's the secret, the one that Boba learned on a warm Ghost Month night in Keelung, surrounded by lanterns and tiny demons and the smell of the best milk tea in the world:

You don't become less by giving yourself away.

You become whole.

---

## Goodnight Blessing

Close your eyes now, little one. The lanterns are dimming over Keelung harbor, and the night market is humming its last lullaby — the sizzle of a final scallion pancake, the clink of a teacup being set down gently, the soft giggle of a demon child who stayed just a little too long because the snacks were just that good.

Somewhere, a round little tapioca pearl is drying off, telling the story of the night she jumped, and smiling so wide her whole glossy face is nothing but grin.

You are enough. You are whole. And the best things you'll ever do are the things you give away on purpose, with a running start and a magnificent splash.

Sleep well. Dream sweet. And if you hear a tiny sneeze in the dark tonight — don't worry.

It's just a friend you haven't met yet.

Goodnight.
