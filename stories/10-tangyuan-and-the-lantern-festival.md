# Story 10: Tangyuan and the Lantern Festival

**Main Character:** Tāng Yuán (湯圓 tāngyuán) — Tangyuan (Glutinous Rice Ball)
**Guest Characters:** None
**Setting:** Pingxi, New Taipei City
**Theme:** Family and togetherness
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 湯圓 (tāngyuán) = tangyuan

---

Tāng Yuán was round, and he was proud of it.

"We're all in this soup together!" he announced, bobbing happily in a pot of warm sweet ginger broth alongside eleven other tangyuan — his brothers, sisters, cousins, and one tangyuan of unclear relation who showed up at every family gathering and nobody had the heart to ask who invited her.

**湯圓** (tāngyuán) = tangyuan were glutinous rice balls, served in sweet soup, and they were the official food of the Lantern Festival — the fifteenth day of the Lunar New Year, when all of **台灣** (Táiwān) = Taiwan celebrated with lanterns, fireworks, and enough sugar to power a small city.

And TODAY was the Lantern Festival.

Tāng Yuán's family — the entire extended Yuán clan — was being transported to **平溪** (Píngxī) = Pingxi, a tiny mountain town in New Taipei City famous for one thing and one thing only: sky lanterns.

If you've never seen a Pingxi sky lantern release, imagine this: thousands of people, each holding a paper lantern the size of a garbage bag, painted with wishes and dreams in bright calligraphy. On the count of three, everyone lights the candle inside and releases. The lanterns rise — slowly, magically, impossibly — into the black sky, like a river of fire flowing upward. For a few minutes, the sky is full of warm, floating lights, each one carrying someone's hope toward the stars.

It was, by any reasonable measure, the most beautiful thing in Taiwan. And that was saying something, because Taiwan had a LOT of beautiful things.

Tāng Yuán was being carried to Pingxi in a large thermos by the Chén family: Mama Chén, Bàba Chén, teenage daughter Yú-Xīn, twelve-year-old son Zhì-Míng, and Grandmother Chén, who was eighty-four years old and had more opinions per square centimeter than any human being in recorded history.

"The tangyuan is too sweet this year," Grandmother Chén declared from the back seat of the family car.

"Mama, you say that every year," said Bàba Chén.

"Because every year, it's too sweet. When I was a girl, tangyuan had CHARACTER. It had BITE. Now everything is sugar sugar sugar."

"I think it's perfect," said Zhì-Míng.

"You think everything is perfect. You're twelve. You don't know what imperfect is yet."

Inside the thermos, Tāng Yuán chuckled. He loved the Chén family. They bickered constantly — about tangyuan sweetness, about which radio station to listen to, about whether Zhì-Míng was spending too much time on his phone — but underneath the bickering was something warm and solid, like the broth he was floating in.

Family. The real kind. The kind that drove you crazy and kept you sane at the same time.

They arrived in Pingxi at dusk. The tiny mountain town was transformed: every street was lined with red lanterns, every shop was selling sky lanterns and firecrackers, and the air hummed with the excited chatter of a thousand families, all there for the same reason.

"**來來來**!" (lái lái lái) shouted the sky lantern vendors — "come come come!" — waving their products like enormous paper tubes.

Mama Chén bought the family's sky lantern — a big one, large enough for everyone to write a wish on. She set it on a table at the launch site and handed out brushes and ink.

"Everyone writes a wish," she instructed. "One wish each. Then we launch together."

Grandmother Chén wrote hers first, in the elegant, old-fashioned calligraphy she'd learned as a schoolgirl: "Health for my family."

Bàba Chén wrote: "A good year for the business."

Mama Chén wrote: "Peace of mind."

Yú-Xīn wrote something she covered with her hand and refused to show anyone, which meant it was either about a boy, a K-pop band, or both.

Zhì-Míng stared at his blank section of the lantern. His brush hovered. He didn't write.

"**怎麼了**?" (zěnme le) Mama Chén asked — "what's wrong?"

"I don't know what to wish for," Zhì-Míng said. He looked embarrassed. "Everything I can think of sounds dumb."

"No wish is dumb," said Bàba Chén.

"Mine is," said Zhì-Míng. He stared at the ground. "I wish... I wish we could do this forever. This. All of us. Together. Going to Pingxi. Eating tangyuan. Writing wishes. I wish nothing would change."

The family went quiet. Not uncomfortable quiet — the other kind. The kind where everyone is thinking the same thing but nobody wants to be the first to say it because it's too big for words.

Grandmother Chén cleared her throat. "Write that," she said firmly. "That is the best wish on this lantern."

Zhì-Míng wrote it. His calligraphy was messy — twelve-year-old calligraphy always is — but the characters were clear: 永遠在一起 (yǒngyuǎn zài yīqǐ) = together forever.

Inside the thermos on the table, Tāng Yuán felt something crack inside him — not his shell, but his heart. Specifically, the black sesame filling inside his glutinous rice body shifted, the way feelings shift when they're too big for your chest.

"Family," he whispered to his eleven relatives in the broth. "That's what we're for. Not just food. Not just tradition. We're the thing that brings them together."

"We know," said his sister, a pink tangyuan filled with peanut. "We've always known."

The countdown began. Across the launch field, hundreds of families held their lanterns aloft. Candles were lit inside the paper shells. The lanterns began to glow — warm, orange, alive.

"Three!" the crowd shouted.

The Chén family gathered around their lantern, each person holding an edge.

"Two!"

Zhì-Míng looked at Grandmother Chén. Grandmother Chén looked at Zhì-Míng. She reached over and squeezed his hand.

"One!"

"**放**!" (fàng) — "RELEASE!"

A thousand lanterns rose.

Tāng Yuán couldn't see it from inside the thermos, but he could FEEL it. The collective gasp of a thousand people watching their wishes rise toward the stars. The warmth of the candle light reflecting off every face. The soft, hushed wonder that falls over a crowd when something truly, deeply beautiful happens.

The lanterns rose higher, higher, becoming a river of light flowing into the black mountain sky. Each one carried a wish: for health, for love, for success, for happiness, for things too private and too precious to say out loud.

And somewhere among them, in the messy calligraphy of a twelve-year-old boy: together forever.

"**太棒了**" (tài bàng le) Grandmother Chén breathed — "beautiful" — and for once, she had no criticism to add.

Afterward, the family sat at a plastic table at a night market stall, and Mama Chén unscrewed the thermos. She ladled out bowls of tangyuan in sweet ginger soup — one bowl for each family member, with three tangyuan per bowl.

Tāng Yuán and two of his cousins landed in Zhì-Míng's bowl.

The boy looked down at the round, white, bobbing rice balls. Steam rose from the broth. The lanterns were still visible in the sky, distant now, like extra stars.

"**吃飽了嗎**?" (chī bǎo le ma) Mama Chén asked — "have you eaten yet?"

"Not yet," said Zhì-Míng. He picked up his spoon.

"Wait!" said Grandmother Chén. "We eat together. On the count of three."

"**一**... **二**... **三**!" (yī... èr... sān)

Five spoons dipped into five bowls at the same moment. Five bites of tangyuan. Five sets of eyes closing in the same expression of warm, sweet satisfaction.

Tāng Yuán felt Zhì-Míng's teeth press gently through his glutinous rice skin, and then the black sesame filling inside him burst free — warm, sweet, nutty, and rich. It flooded the boy's mouth with the taste of something ancient and simple and good.

"**歐伊西**" (ōuyīxī) Zhì-Míng said softly, his mouth full, his eyes shining. "Yummy."

And inside that bite — inside that moment of warm soup and sweet sesame and family gathered under a sky full of floating wishes — Tāng Yuán felt it.

Togetherness.

Not the kind that lasts forever, because nothing does — not tangyuan, not lanterns, not even the strongest wishes. But the kind that MATTERS forever. The kind that becomes a memory so warm and sweet that it stays with you through every cold night and hard day and lonely moment for the rest of your life.

The kind that tastes like home.

"We're all in this soup together," Tāng Yuán whispered, one last time, before the warmth took him completely.

And he meant every word.
