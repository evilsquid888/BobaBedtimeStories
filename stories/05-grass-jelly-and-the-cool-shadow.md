# Story 05: Grass Jelly and the Cool Shadow

**Main Character:** Caǎo (仙草 xiāncǎo) — Grass Jelly
**Guest Characters:** None
**Setting:** Tainan old temples
**Theme:** Being yourself
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 仙草 (xiāncǎo) = grass jelly

---

Nobody ever noticed Caǎo, and that was exactly how she liked it.

While the other desserts in Taiwan bounced, stretched, wobbled, and generally made spectacles of themselves, Caǎo sat in the corner of a temple courtyard in Tainan, wearing a tiny black beanie, reading poetry, and radiating an aura of such profound chill that the temperature around her was literally two degrees cooler than the rest of the city.

"Chill," she said to no one. "I've got this."

Caǎo was **仙草** (xiāncǎo) = grass jelly — a dark, inky, black-green cube of herbal jelly made from a plant called mesona. In Taiwan, grass jelly was everywhere: in shaved ice, in milk tea, in sweet soups. It was the quiet ingredient that made everything better without ever demanding attention.

Which suited Caǎo perfectly.

She lived in **台南** (Táinán) = Tainan, the oldest city in Taiwan and, in Caǎo's objective opinion, the best. Tainan was the kind of city where ancient temples sat next to 7-Elevens, where hundred-year-old banyan trees shaded streets named after Confucian virtues, and where the food — oh, the food — was so good that people drove three hours from Taipei just to eat breakfast.

And the temples. Tainan had hundreds of temples. Ornate, colorful, centuries-old buildings with curved roofs and dragon sculptures and stone courtyards where old men played chess and cats slept in pools of sunlight.

Caǎo's temple was called the Koxinga Shrine, and it had the most magnificent banyan tree in all of Tainan. The tree was four hundred years old, its roots spreading across the courtyard like the tentacles of a gentle, leafy octopus. In its shade, the air was always cool.

That's where Caǎo sat. In the shade. Being cool. Because that's what grass jelly does.

The trouble started when the Tainan Dessert Festival was announced.

Posters appeared all over the city: bright, neon, impossible to ignore. "TAINAN DESSERT FEST!" they screamed in letters the size of cars. "SHOW THE WORLD YOUR SWEETNESS! PRIZES! GLORY! FREE SAMPLES!"

Every dessert ingredient in Tainan lost its mind.

The mango ice stalls started practicing dramatic presentations. The peanut candies rehearsed dance routines. The **豆花** (dòuhuā) = tofu pudding vendors hired a social media consultant. Even the ancient, venerable coffin bread — which was technically a savory dish but refused to be excluded on principle — was practicing its stage presence.

Everyone was preparing to perform, to shine, to BE SEEN.

Everyone except Caǎo.

"Hard pass," she said, turning a page of her poetry book.

"But Caǎo!" protested Mango, who had zoomed down from Pingtung specifically to compete. "This is the biggest dessert event of the year! Don't you want to show everyone how great grass jelly is?"

"Grass jelly doesn't need to show," said Caǎo. "Grass jelly IS."

"That doesn't even make sense."

"It makes perfect sense. You're just not chill enough to understand it."

Mango huffed and zoomed away, leaving a trail of orange juice droplets on the temple stones.

The Festival arrived on a Saturday so hot that the pavement was literally cooking eggs. **熱死了** (rè sǐ le) = "so hot I could die" was the most common phrase in the city, spoken by approximately every single person, animal, and inanimate object in Tainan. The trees drooped. The rivers steamed. The street dogs found shade and refused to move until December.

The Festival was held in a big open square near the Chihkan Tower, another of Tainan's ancient landmarks. Stages had been set up, and one by one, the dessert ingredients performed.

Mango did a flashy athletic display, bouncing across the stage and leaving juice trails that sparkled in the sun. The crowd cheered.

A group of **鳳梨酥** (fènglísū) = pineapple cakes performed a synchronized dance in their gift boxes. Elegant. Impressive. The judges applauded.

The shaved ice — an enormous, flamboyant mountain of flavored ice — made the most dramatic entrance, sliding onto the stage in a cloud of cold mist with seventeen different toppings arranged like a crown. "MORE IS MORE!" it bellowed, and half the audience fainted from the spectacle.

Caǎo watched all of this from the shade of a nearby temple doorway.

"Amateurs," she murmured.

But then something happened that shook even Caǎo's legendary cool.

A little girl wandered away from the Festival crowd and into the temple courtyard. She was about seven years old, wearing a yellow sundress that was damp with sweat, and she was crying.

Not loudly. Not dramatically. Just quietly, the way children cry when they think nobody is watching.

She sat down under the banyan tree — right next to Caǎo's spot — and drew her knees up to her chest.

Caǎo observed her with one eye, keeping the other on her poetry book. She said nothing for a full minute. Then:

"Hot out there, huh?"

The girl sniffled. "Yeah."

"**熱死了**," Caǎo agreed.

Another minute passed.

"I don't like the Festival," the girl whispered.

"Yeah? Why not?"

"Everyone is so... loud. And bright. And... much. I tried to watch the shaved ice show and there were so many people and so much noise and I just..." She wiped her eyes. "I just wanted somewhere quiet."

Caǎo set down her poetry book. She looked at the girl — really looked at her — and saw something she recognized. Not sadness, exactly. More like... the feeling of being a cool, dark, quiet thing in a world that only celebrated bright, loud, flashy things.

"You know what I am?" Caǎo asked.

The girl looked at the inky black-green cube in the tiny beanie. "Grass jelly?"

"Yep. **仙草**. You know what that means? The characters literally translate to 'immortal grass.' Pretty metal name for a dessert, right?"

The girl almost smiled. "Yeah."

"Here's the thing about grass jelly. It's never the star. It's never the headliner. You go to a shaved ice shop, you don't say 'give me a bowl of grass jelly.' You say 'give me shaved ice WITH grass jelly.' It's always the WITH. The supporting player. The thing that makes everything else better."

"That sounds kind of sad," said the girl.

"Does it? Or does it sound like a superpower?"

The girl thought about it.

"Think about it this way," Caǎo continued, leaning back against the banyan root. "Without grass jelly, shaved ice is just ice and sugar. It's fine. It's sweet. But it's missing something. It's missing the COOL. The herbal. The bitter-sweet depth that balances everything out. Grass jelly doesn't need the spotlight because grass jelly IS the thing that makes the spotlight worth watching."

"Like... being the person who listens instead of the person who talks?"

"Exactly like that."

The girl was quiet for a moment. Then she said, "My mom says I'm too quiet. She wants me to be more like my sister. My sister does taekwondo and plays piano and talks to everyone at parties. I just... read."

"What do you read?"

"Poetry."

Caǎo grinned — a slow, cool, grass-jelly grin. She picked up her poetry book and held it out. "Ever read Li Bai?"

"The Tang Dynasty poet?"

"Kid, you and I are going to get along great."

They sat together under the banyan tree for the rest of the afternoon, the girl and the grass jelly, reading poetry and not talking much and being perfectly comfortable in the silence. The sounds of the Festival drifted over the temple wall — cheering, music, the distant cry of "MORE IS MORE!" — but in the shade of the four-hundred-year-old tree, everything was cool and calm and exactly right.

Around sunset, the girl's mother came looking for her. She found her daughter sitting in the temple courtyard with a dreamy smile and a small black-green cube on her lap.

"There you are! The Festival is almost over! They're announcing the winners!"

"That's okay," said the girl. "I found something better."

Her mother looked confused but led her back to the Festival square, where the judges were ready to announce the Best Dessert of Tainan.

The mango was preening. The shaved ice was melting with anticipation. The pineapple cakes were holding hands nervously.

And then the head judge — a tiny, ancient woman who had been eating Tainan desserts for approximately nine decades — stepped up to the microphone.

"The winner," she said, "is grass jelly."

Silence.

"WHAT?" said Mango.

"HOW?" said the shaved ice.

"We weren't even on stage," said Caǎo.

"You didn't need to be," said the judge. "I've been watching this Festival all day. Everything was delicious. Everything was impressive. But you know what was missing from almost every dish? Balance. Coolness. Depth. The quiet thing that makes the loud things sing. That's grass jelly. It doesn't perform. It doesn't beg for attention. It just makes everything better. And in my ninety-two years of eating, I've learned that the best ingredient is the one you don't notice until it's gone."

The crowd erupted. The girl in the yellow sundress cheered louder than anyone.

Caǎo accepted the trophy — a small golden bowl — with characteristic composure.

"Chill," she said into the microphone. "I've got this."

The crowd laughed. The judge smiled. And in the warm Tainan twilight, Caǎo carried her trophy back to the temple courtyard and set it under the banyan tree, where it caught the last golden light of the day.

Later that evening, the girl came back. She brought a bowl of shaved ice with grass jelly from her favorite shop. She sat under the tree, took a bite, and closed her eyes.

"**好吃**" (hǎochī) she whispered — "delicious."

And in the bowl, cool and dark and perfectly herself, Caǎo smiled.

Because she had always known the truth: you don't have to be the loudest thing in the room to be the most important. Sometimes the coolest thing you can do is just... be cool.

Goodnight, cool soul. Goodnight.
