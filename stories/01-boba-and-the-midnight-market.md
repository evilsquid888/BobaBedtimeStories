# Story 01: Boba and the Midnight Market

**Main Character:** Boba (波霸 bōbà) — Tapioca Pearl
**Guest Characters:** None
**Setting:** Shilin Night Market, Taipei
**Theme:** Courage to explore
**Mandarin Spotlight:** 夜市 (yèshì) = night market

---

Boba had never left the cup.

That might sound strange to you, but if you were a perfectly round, perfectly glossy tapioca pearl living at the bottom of a tall plastic cup of milk tea, you'd understand. The cup was safe. The cup was familiar. The cup had a nice bendy straw that gave everyone a ride to the top whenever they wanted to see the world outside.

But Boba didn't want rides to the top. Boba wanted OUT.

"Let's bounce!" she whispered to herself every night, pressing her tiny sticky hands against the plastic wall. Through the foggy cup, she could see lights — thousands of them — red lanterns, neon signs, and golden bulbs strung like jewels across the most magical place in all of **台灣** (Táiwān) = Taiwan: the **夜市** (yèshì) = night market.

Specifically, the Shilin Night Market. The big one. The legend.

Every evening, Boba watched the parade of smells drift past her cup. Sizzling **臭豆腐** (chòu dòufu) = stinky tofu, which smelled like a gym sock had fallen in love with a deep fryer. Grilled squid on sticks. Pepper buns so hot they steamed like tiny volcanoes. And somewhere in the distance, the sweet, creamy fragrance of fresh milk tea being poured into cups just like hers.

"One day," Boba whispered. "One day I'll see it all."

The other tapioca pearls thought she was nuts.

"Why would you leave?" said Pearl Number Forty-Seven, a particularly comfortable pearl who had found the perfect spot near the bottom where the milk tea was warmest. "We've got everything we need right here. Sugar. Warmth. Occasional straw rides. What more could you want?"

"Adventure," said Boba.

Pearl Number Forty-Seven rolled her eyes. Which was impressive, since she was a sphere and her entire body was technically one big eye-roll.

That night — a sticky, electric Tuesday in August — everything changed. The vendor who owned their cup got distracted by an argument between a customer and a papaya milk seller over whose drink was more refreshing. ("PAPAYA!" "MILK TEA!" "PAPAYA!" It was very heated.) In the chaos, the cup tipped.

Not all the way. Just enough.

The lid popped. The straw fell out. And Boba, who had been pressed right against the opening, felt the warm night air hit her face for the first time.

"**加油**" (jiāyóu) she whispered to herself — "go for it" — and she bounced.

She bounced right out of the cup, off the counter, and onto the wet, glittering pavement of the Shilin Night Market.

For a moment, she just lay there, a tiny black pearl on the concrete, staring up at a sky full of steam and lantern light. She was the smallest thing in the biggest, loudest, most wonderful place she'd ever seen.

And she was absolutely terrified.

The ground shook with footsteps — hundreds of humans in flip-flops, sneakers, and high heels stomping past in every direction. To Boba, each foot was the size of a building. She rolled frantically to the left to avoid a rubber sandal, then to the right to dodge a shopping bag that crashed down like a meteor.

"Okay," she panted, wedging herself into a crack between two floor tiles. "Okay. This is fine. I'm fine. I'm a brave tapioca pearl in a night market. Totally fine."

She was not fine.

A river of spilled milk tea flowed past her hiding spot — a terrible, tragic river of wasted deliciousness — and Boba watched her own kind float by, lifeless pearls who had been abandoned on the ground. She shuddered.

"Not me," she said firmly. "I didn't come out here to become sidewalk decoration."

She took a breath (do tapioca pearls breathe? In this story they do, so don't worry about it) and started rolling. Rolling was what Boba did best. She was, after all, a sphere. Spheres are made for rolling. It's basically their whole thing.

She rolled past the first stall: a mountain of fried chicken so golden it looked like it had been dipped in sunset. The vendor was shouting "**來來來**!" (lái lái lái) = "come come come!" and waving tongs like a conductor leading an orchestra of grease.

She rolled past the second stall: a game booth where you could win stuffed animals by throwing balls at stacked cans. Boba considered volunteering as a ball but decided she had bigger plans.

She rolled past the third stall — and stopped.

Because the third stall was the most beautiful thing she had ever seen.

It was a tiny, ancient-looking cart with a hand-painted sign that read: "阿嬤的珍珠奶茶" — Grandma's Boba Milk Tea. Behind the cart stood the oldest, smallest, most wrinkled woman Boba had ever seen, and she was doing something extraordinary.

She was making tapioca pearls. By hand.

Boba watched, transfixed, as the old woman's fingers — quick and sure despite their age — kneaded cassava starch and brown sugar into a dark, glossy dough. She rolled it into ropes, cut it into tiny pieces, and then — with a gentleness that made Boba's heart squeeze — she rolled each piece into a perfect sphere between her palms.

"That's how I was made," Boba breathed. "Someone did that for ME."

She had never thought about it before. She had just... existed. One moment, nothing. The next moment, round and bouncy and floating in tea. But someone — some grandmother, some baker, some pair of careful hands — had rolled her into being.

The old woman finished a batch and dropped the pearls into a pot of boiling water. They sank, then slowly floated to the top, one by one, like little black moons rising.

"**好吃**" (hǎochī) the old woman murmured to herself — "delicious" — and smiled.

Boba wanted to stay and watch forever, but the night market had other plans. A gust of wind from a giant exhaust fan blew her sideways, and she went tumbling through the crowd like a tiny bowling ball, ricocheting off shoes and chair legs and the wheels of a passing cart.

"AAAAH!" she yelled, which sounded like a tiny squeak that nobody heard.

She pinballed through the market at tremendous speed, catching glimpses of wonders as she flew: a stall selling coffin bread (yes, it's shaped like a coffin, and yes, it's delicious — Taiwan is wild like that); a man sculpting sugar into dragons; a woman painting names on grains of rice with a brush thinner than an eyelash.

Finally, she crashed into something soft.

Something purple.

Something that said, "Good things take time to steam."

It was a chunk of taro, sitting calmly on the edge of a dessert display, watching the chaos with the patience of a mountain.

"Sorry!" Boba gasped. "I'm so sorry! I was rolling and there was wind and shoes and—"

"Breathe," said the taro chunk. It adjusted a pair of tiny reading glasses and peered down at her. "You must be new out here."

"I just escaped my cup," Boba admitted.

"Ah." The taro nodded wisely. "A bouncer. We get those sometimes. I'm Taro, by the way."

"Boba."

"I know. I can tell by the panic." Taro smiled — a slow, purple, gentle smile. "So, little pearl. Now that you're out in the **夜市**, what are you going to do?"

Boba looked around. The market stretched in every direction, a maze of light and smoke and music and laughter. It was enormous. It was overwhelming. It was the most incredible thing she had ever seen.

"Everything," she said.

Taro chuckled. "That's a very tapioca pearl answer. But here's some advice from someone who's been steaming for a while: you don't have to do everything tonight. The night market will be here tomorrow, and the night after that. The brave thing isn't doing everything at once. The brave thing is taking the first bounce."

Boba considered this. "I already took the first bounce. I bounced right out of my cup."

"Then you've already done the hardest part."

They sat together for a while — the tiny black pearl and the wise purple taro — watching the river of people flow past. Boba saw a little girl tug her mother's hand and point at a bubble tea shop, eyes wide with excitement. She saw a group of teenagers laughing so hard that one of them snorted milk tea out of his nose (not recommended). She saw an old couple sharing a single cup of tea, passing the straw back and forth without a word, because after fifty years together, who needs words?

"It's beautiful," Boba said.

"It is," agreed Taro. "And you know what makes it beautiful? Everyone here came because they were hungry for something. Food, fun, connection. The night market gives whatever you need. You just have to be brave enough to show up."

A vendor nearby was calling out for the last batch of **珍珠奶茶** (zhēnzhū nǎichá) = boba milk tea. The most famous drink in all of Taiwan. The drink that had given Boba her name, her purpose, her very existence.

Boba looked at the cup being prepared — the creamy tea, the ice, the empty space at the bottom waiting for pearls.

"I think," said Boba slowly, "I think I'm ready."

"Ready for what?" asked Taro.

"Ready for the cup. But this time, it's MY choice. I'm not stuck in a cup because I've never known anything else. I'm choosing the cup because I've SEEN everything else, and I know where I belong."

She bounced — one glorious, magnificent, three-inch-high bounce — off the display, through the air, and landed with a satisfying PLOP right in the fresh cup of milk tea.

The milk tea was cold and sweet and perfect. The ice cubes clinked around her like wind chimes. And from her new cup, held in the hands of the little girl who had been pointing at the bubble tea shop, Boba could see the whole night market spreading out before her.

"**謝謝**" (xièxie) = "thank you," the little girl said to the vendor.

Then she took a long sip through the fat boba straw, and Boba felt herself rising — up, up, up through the tea, through the straw, toward the stars and the lanterns and the beautiful, noisy, extraordinary world.

And it was, without question, the most delicious feeling in the universe.

## Goodnight Blessing

Tonight, little dreamer, may you be as brave as a tiny tapioca pearl who bounced out of her cup and into the world. May your adventures be sticky and sweet, and may you always find your way back to the people who love you. The night market will be there tomorrow. For now, close your eyes and float in the warm, sweet tea of sleep. Goodnight, little boba. Goodnight.
